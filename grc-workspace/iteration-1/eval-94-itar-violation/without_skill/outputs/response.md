# ITAR Violation Response: Unauthorized Export of Technical Drawings to Taiwan

## Executive Summary

The unauthorized email transmission of ITAR-controlled technical drawings to a supplier in Taiwan constitutes a serious violation of the International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR), 22 C.F.R. Parts 120-130, administered by the U.S. Department of State's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC). This is a significant export control violation with potentially severe criminal, civil, and administrative consequences. Immediate and structured action is required.

---

## 1. Understanding What Happened

### Nature of the Violation
- **Unauthorized Export**: Emailing ITAR-controlled technical drawings to a foreign person/entity without a license constitutes an "export" under ITAR (22 C.F.R. § 120.17), even when transmitted electronically.
- **Taiwan is a Foreign Country**: Despite generally friendly U.S.-Taiwan relations, Taiwan is treated as a foreign country under ITAR. A license or other authorization is required to export ITAR-controlled items or technical data to Taiwan.
- **Technical Data is Controlled**: Technical drawings, specifications, and design documents related to defense articles listed on the U.S. Munitions List (USML) are "technical data" under 22 C.F.R. § 120.33 and are controlled under ITAR.
- **The Email Itself is the Violation**: Electronic transmission of controlled technical data is an export. The violation occurred at the moment the email was sent, regardless of whether the recipient actually accessed, used, or disseminated the information.

### The 6-Month Discovery Gap
The fact that this occurred 6 months ago and is only now being discovered is itself a compliance concern. It suggests gaps in:
- Employee training and awareness
- Export control monitoring/screening of outbound communications
- Technical controls on data transmission
- Internal reporting culture

## 2. Immediate Steps to Take

### Step 1: Stop the Bleeding — Contain the Violation (Do Immediately)

- **Notify the supplier in Taiwan immediately** to return or destroy the technical drawings and confirm in writing that no copies were retained, shared further, or used.
- **Preserve all evidence**: Do not delete emails, logs, or communications. Issue a litigation hold immediately.
- **Identify the full scope**: Determine exactly which drawings were sent, their USML classification, and whether any additional disclosures occurred (e.g., did the supplier share with others?).
- **Restrict the engineer's export privileges** pending investigation — ensure no further unauthorized transmissions occur.

### Step 2: Engage Legal Counsel Specializing in Export Controls (Within 24-48 Hours)

- Retain an attorney with specific ITAR/export control expertise immediately. Do not attempt to self-navigate this.
- Attorney-client privilege is critical here — communications about the violation should go through counsel.
- Counsel will guide the voluntary disclosure decision (see below) and help frame the narrative to regulators.

### Step 3: Conduct an Internal Investigation

- Interview the engineer to understand intent, circumstances, and whether this was an isolated incident.
- Determine whether anyone else knew about the transmission.
- Review other outbound communications for similar issues.
- Identify which specific USML category the technical drawings fall under (this affects severity).
- Determine whether any licenses, agreements (e.g., TAAs, MLAs), or exemptions existed that may cover or partially cover this activity.

### Step 4: Assess Voluntary Self-Disclosure to DDTC (Critical Decision — Within Days to Weeks)

Under 22 C.F.R. § 127.12, companies can submit a Voluntary Self-Disclosure (VSD) to DDTC. This is one of the most important decisions you will make:

**Benefits of Voluntary Self-Disclosure:**
- DDTC treats VSDs as a significant mitigating factor in penalty determinations.
- Penalties are typically substantially reduced — often by 50% or more.
- Demonstrates good faith and a commitment to compliance.
- Reduces the risk of criminal referral to the Department of Justice.
- Protects against debarment from future defense contracting in many cases.

**Risks of Not Disclosing:**
- If DDTC discovers the violation through other means (supplier audit, counterpart intelligence, whistleblower), the company loses all mitigation credit.
- Non-disclosure can be construed as willful concealment, dramatically increasing penalties and criminal exposure.
- Statutes of limitations for ITAR violations are generally 5 years for criminal charges; regulators have long memories.

**VSD Process:**
1. Submit an initial notification letter to DDTC within a short timeframe (counsel will advise on timing).
2. Follow up with a full disclosure package including a detailed account of the violation, corrective actions taken, and remediation plan.
3. Cooperate fully with any DDTC inquiry.

### Step 5: Apply for a Retroactive or Nunc Pro Tunc License (If Applicable)

- In some cases, you may be able to apply for a license to retroactively authorize the disclosure, or at minimum to regularize the ongoing relationship with the supplier.
- Counsel will advise whether this is feasible based on the specific technical data and USML category.

### Step 6: Implement Immediate Remediation

- Suspend the existing supplier relationship until proper authorizations are in place.
- Review all other international supplier relationships for similar risks.
- Immediately implement or strengthen export control policies, procedures, and technical controls.

## 3. Potential Penalties and Exposures

ITAR violations carry some of the most severe penalties in U.S. law. Both the company and the individual engineer face exposure.

### Criminal Penalties (22 U.S.C. § 2778(c); 22 C.F.R. Part 127)

- **Per violation**: Up to **20 years imprisonment** and/or up to **$1,000,000 in fines** per violation.
- Each item exported and each transaction can constitute a separate violation.
- Both the company (organizational criminal liability) and the individual engineer can be prosecuted.
- The Department of Justice (DOJ) handles criminal ITAR prosecutions.
- Intent ("willfulness") is required for criminal liability, but courts interpret this broadly — ignoring obvious red flags can suffice.

### Civil Penalties (22 C.F.R. § 127.10)

- Up to **$1,368,136 per violation** (amount adjusted periodically for inflation under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act; this figure reflects recent adjustments).
- DDTC calculates penalties per transaction, per controlled item, and per violation.
- A single email with multiple controlled drawings could be treated as multiple violations.
- Civil penalties do not require proof of criminal intent.

### Administrative/Debarment Penalties (22 C.F.R. § 127.7)

- DDTC can **debar** companies and individuals from participating in defense trade (import and export of defense articles and services) for up to **3 years** per violation.
- Debarment is devastating for defense contractors and can effectively end a business.
- Even partial debarment (restrictions on specific USML categories) is extremely disruptive.

### Export Enforcement Coordination Center (E2C2) and DOJ Criminal Division

- Multiple agencies have jurisdiction over export control violations: DDTC (State), BIS (Commerce, for EAR items), OFAC (Treasury, for sanctions), and DOJ/FBI for criminal enforcement.
- While this scenario involves ITAR (State/DDTC), the DOJ Criminal Division's National Security Division prosecutes ITAR crimes.

### Reputational and Contractual Consequences

- **Loss of facility security clearance** if the company holds one.
- **Suspension or termination of government contracts** — Contracting Officers may suspend or debar you from federal contracting.
- **Negative past performance ratings** affecting future contract awards.
- **Reputational damage** in the defense industrial base and with prime contractors.
- **Insurance implications** — Directors & Officers (D&O) and errors & omissions policies may have export control exclusions.

## 4. Mitigating Factors That Affect Penalty Severity

DDTC considers multiple factors when determining penalties:

**Factors That Reduce Penalties:**
- Voluntary self-disclosure (most significant mitigation)
- Isolated, non-systemic violation
- No evidence of intent to harm national security or violate the law
- Immediate corrective action upon discovery
- Robust existing compliance program (even if it failed here)
- Full cooperation with DDTC investigation
- No prior ITAR violations
- Minimal harm — e.g., Taiwan supplier did not further disseminate or use the data
- Quick retrieval/destruction of disclosed materials

**Factors That Increase Penalties:**
- Willfulness or deliberate circumvention of ITAR
- Prior ITAR violations
- Involvement of state actors or foreign intelligence services
- Data transmitted to a sanctioned country or denied party (Taiwan is not sanctioned, which is favorable)
- Systemic compliance failures across the organization
- Failure to cooperate or attempted concealment
- Harm to national security or sensitive programs

---

## 5. Taiwan-Specific Considerations

- Taiwan is **not a sanctioned country** and is not on restricted party lists, which is a mitigating factor.
- However, Taiwan is treated as a foreign country under ITAR, and no ITAR exemption generally covers Taiwan for most technical data transmissions without a license.
- Taiwan is eligible for ITAR licenses, and approvals for Taiwan are common, but approval is not retroactive.
- The geopolitical context (U.S.-Taiwan relations, semiconductor supply chains) will not substitute for a license.
- There is no "friendly country" exemption under ITAR that automatically covers Taiwan.

## 6. Medium-Term Remediation Plan

After handling the immediate crisis, implement the following:

### Compliance Program Enhancement
- Conduct a comprehensive ITAR/EAR audit of all technical data and products.
- Classify all technical data under the USML or Commerce Control List (CCL) as appropriate.
- Implement an export control management system (ECMS) to screen all outbound transmissions.
- Establish a Technology Control Plan (TCP) governing access to ITAR-controlled technical data.

### Training and Culture
- Mandatory ITAR training for all employees who handle technical data — not just compliance staff.
- Specific training on electronic transmission risks (email, cloud storage, collaboration tools).
- Clear escalation procedures for employees who are uncertain about export control requirements.
- Anonymous reporting hotline for potential violations.

### Technical Controls
- Data Loss Prevention (DLP) tools to flag or block transmission of ITAR-tagged data to foreign domains.
- Email archiving with export control keyword and metadata screening.
- Access controls limiting who can access ITAR technical data.
- Encryption requirements for any authorized transmissions.

### Process Controls
- Require export control review and sign-off before sharing any technical data with foreign persons or entities.
- Maintain a log of all technical data transmissions to foreign parties.
- Establish a formal license management process for all DDTC licenses and agreements.

## Conclusion

This is a serious situation that demands immediate, coordinated action. The single most important step — after containing the violation and retaining export control counsel — is making a well-prepared voluntary self-disclosure to DDTC. Companies that self-disclose promptly, cooperate fully, and implement meaningful remediation routinely achieve substantially reduced penalties compared to those discovered through government investigation. The penalty exposure without mitigation is significant: potentially millions of dollars in civil fines, possible criminal prosecution of the engineer and company officers, and reputational consequences that can affect defense contracting for years.

Do not delay. Do not attempt to quietly ignore this. The path through this situation, while uncomfortable, is to act with speed, transparency with regulators (through counsel), and demonstrated commitment to compliance.
