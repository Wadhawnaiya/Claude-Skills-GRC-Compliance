# ITAR Violation Response: Unauthorized Export of Technical Drawings to Taiwan

## Situation Summary

An engineer emailed ITAR-controlled technical drawings to a supplier in Taiwan without a license six months ago. This constitutes an **unauthorized export of technical data** under ITAR and is a serious violation of 22 CFR Part 125 (export of technical data) and 22 CFR Part 127 (violations and penalties). Immediate, structured action is required.

---

## Immediate Actions — Do These First

### 1. Place a Litigation Hold on All Relevant Records

Before anything else, preserve all evidence. Issue a litigation hold immediately covering:

- The email(s) in question and all email chains related to the incident
- The technical drawings and any attachments sent
- Communications with the Taiwan supplier (before and after the disclosure)
- Any internal communications about the drawings or the supplier relationship
- The engineer's email account, file access logs, and any shared drives

Destroying, modifying, or failing to preserve records after discovery of a potential violation can constitute obstruction and will be treated as an aggravating factor by DDTC.

---

### 2. File a Voluntary Self-Disclosure (VSD) — 22 CFR § 127.12

**Voluntary Self-Disclosure is the single most important step you can take.** DDTC explicitly recognizes VSD as the strongest available mitigating factor. Filing promptly signals good faith and significantly reduces the risk of criminal referral and civil penalties.

#### VSD Process — Three Stages

**Stage 1: Initial Notification (file within ~30 days of discovery)**

Submit a brief written notification to the DDTC Director of Compliance. The initial notification should include:

- Your company name and DDTC registration number
- A general description of the potential violation (technical data sent without a license)
- The destination country (Taiwan)
- An estimate of the number of occurrences
- A request for a **tolling agreement** to preserve the statute of limitations while you complete the internal investigation

The initial notification does not need to be complete or perfectly detailed — accuracy matters more than comprehensiveness at this stage. File it promptly.

**Stage 2: Internal Investigation (typically 30–90 days)**

Conduct a thorough internal investigation to establish:

- What technical data was sent (drawings, specifications, part numbers, USML category)
- Who sent it and who received it (names, roles, nationalities)
- When it was sent (dates, any follow-up transmissions)
- Whether the drawings were accessed, copied, shared further, or used by the Taiwan supplier
- Whether there are any other undisclosed transmissions to this or other foreign parties
- Root cause: was this a training failure, a process gap, or a deliberate act?
- Whether any similar violations exist elsewhere in the organization

Pull all records — emails, shipping documents, license files, TAA files, and engineering access logs.

**Stage 3: Final VSD Report (file within ~60–90 days of initial notification)**

Submit a comprehensive written report to DDTC including:

- A detailed factual narrative of all violations
- The specific CFR sections violated (e.g., 22 CFR § 125.2 for unlicensed export of technical data, 22 CFR § 127.1 for the violation itself)
- Identification of all parties (the engineer, the supplier, recipients in Taiwan)
- A complete timeline of events
- Root cause analysis
- Corrective actions already implemented
- Proposed additional remediation measures

**Stage 4: DDTC Review and Resolution**

DDTC will review the report, may request additional information, and will determine the outcome. For cooperative VSDs with no prior violations: outcomes range from no action or a warning letter to a reduced civil penalty. Most cooperative VSDs resolve within 6–18 months.

---

### 3. Retain Outside Counsel with ITAR/Export Control Expertise

Engage attorneys experienced in DDTC enforcement and export control law before filing the VSD. Counsel can:

- Guide the scope and privilege of the internal investigation
- Draft the VSD in the most favorable light while remaining accurate
- Assess criminal exposure and advise on DOJ referral risk
- Interface with DDTC on your behalf

---

### 4. Identify and Assess the Technical Data Disclosed

Confirm the USML classification of the technical drawings. Technical data is ITAR-controlled under 22 CFR § 120.33 if it is directly related to a defense article on the United States Munitions List (22 CFR § 121.1). Identify:

- The specific USML category (e.g., Category I for firearms, Category VIII for aircraft, Category XI for military electronics, etc.)
- Whether the drawings include manufacturing or production data (higher risk) vs. general performance specifications
- Whether a TAA or DSP-5/DSP-73 license would have been required
- Whether any export exemption under 22 CFR § 125.4 might arguably apply (unlikely if sent to a supplier for production purposes, but assess)

---

### 5. Notify Senior Management and Your Empowered Official

Your designated Empowered Official (22 CFR § 120.67) must be informed immediately. Senior management involvement demonstrates that the company is taking the matter seriously, which DDTC expects.

---

### 6. Implement Immediate Remediation

While the investigation is ongoing, take corrective steps now:

- Notify the Taiwan supplier in writing that the technical data was disclosed in error, that it is ITAR-controlled, and that it must not be used, copied, or disclosed further
- Request written acknowledgment and return or certified destruction of the drawings
- Temporarily restrict the engineer's access to ITAR-controlled data pending investigation outcome
- Review whether any other ITAR-controlled data has been shared with foreign parties without authorization
- Assess whether your Technology Control Plan (TCP) and training program need immediate updates

## Penalty Exposure

### Civil Penalties — 22 CFR Part 127

- **Up to $1,369,000 per violation** (adjusted annually under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act)
- Each unauthorized export of technical data constitutes a **separate violation**
- If multiple drawings were sent, or sent in multiple emails, DDTC may count each as a separate violation
- DDTC may impose civil penalties via Consent Agreement without requiring criminal prosecution

### Criminal Penalties — 22 USC § 2778(c)

- **Up to $1,000,000 fine per violation**
- **Up to 20 years imprisonment per violation**
- Criminal cases are referred to the Department of Justice National Security Division
- Criminal exposure is most acute if the violation was willful, deliberate, or if the engineer or management knew a license was required and acted anyway

### Debarment — 22 CFR § 127.7

- DDTC may debar the company from all ITAR privileges (registration, licensing, TAAs, MLAs, government contracting)
- Debarment is typically 3 years but can be permanent for egregious violations
- Debarment is published publicly on the DDTC Debarred Parties List

### Other Consequences

- Seizure and forfeiture of articles involved in the violation (22 USC § 2778(e))
- Suspension of export privileges during investigation
- Public disclosure — Consent Agreements are published by DDTC, creating reputational harm
- Potential impact on existing US government contracts if company holds defense contracts

## Factors That Will Affect Your Outcome

### Mitigating Factors (work in your favor)

- **Voluntary self-disclosure** — the most important factor; file promptly
- Full cooperation with DDTC's investigation
- No prior ITAR violations
- Effective pre-existing compliance programme
- Prompt remediation steps taken
- Relatively low national security harm (Taiwan is not an embargoed country; assess whether the supplier or technology raises specific national security concerns)
- Low transaction value

### Aggravating Factors (increase exposure)

- Willful or deliberate violation — if the engineer knew a license was required
- Senior management awareness or involvement
- Pattern of violations (more than this one incident)
- Further dissemination of the drawings by the Taiwan supplier
- Obstruction, destruction of records, or failure to cooperate
- Prior ITAR violations or enforcement history

Taiwan is not subject to a comprehensive arms embargo under 22 CFR § 126.1, which is a favorable fact. However, certain USML categories and specific defense technologies may carry heightened national security sensitivity even for Taiwan; assess the specific drawings against DDTC guidance.
