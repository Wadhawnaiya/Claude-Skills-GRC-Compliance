# ITAR Compliance Requirements: German Engineer Visit for Defense Electronics Project

## Executive Summary

Hosting a foreign national (German citizen) at your facility who will have access to ITAR-controlled technical data triggers the **"deemed export" rule** under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR), 22 C.F.R. Parts 120-130. This is one of the most commonly misunderstood and mishandled compliance obligations in defense contracting. Releasing ITAR-controlled technical data to a foreign national — even within the United States — constitutes an export to that person's country of nationality and requires authorization from the U.S. Department of State, Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC), unless a valid exemption applies.

**Failure to comply can result in criminal penalties up to $1,000,000 per violation and 20 years imprisonment, civil penalties up to $1,394,010 per violation (as adjusted for inflation), and loss of export privileges.**

---

## Step 1: Determine Nationality and Verify Identity

Before anything else, establish the engineer's exact legal status:

- **Confirm citizenship**: A German citizen is a national of Germany. Germany is not on any U.S. embargo or restricted-party list and is a NATO ally, but foreign national status still triggers ITAR deemed export requirements.
- **Check for dual nationality**: If the engineer holds citizenship in any other country (especially a Country Group D:5, embargoed, or specially designated country), this significantly changes the analysis. ITAR applies to the most restrictive nationality.
- **Verify immigration status in the U.S.**: Permanent residents ("green card" holders) are generally treated as U.S. persons under ITAR (22 C.F.R. § 120.62), but verify this carefully. Temporary visa holders are foreign nationals.
- **Screen against restricted party lists**: Run the individual's name through:
  - DDTC Debarred Parties List (22 C.F.R. Part 127)
  - BIS Denied Persons List, Entity List, Unverified List
  - OFAC SDN and Consolidated Sanctions List
  - FBI Terrorist Screening Database (through appropriate channels)

**Do not allow access to any controlled data until screening is complete and documented.**

---

## Step 2: Identify and Classify the ITAR-Controlled Technical Data

Conduct a thorough review of all technical data the German engineer would be exposed to during the 3-week collaboration:

- **Confirm USML categorization**: Identify which category of the U.S. Munitions List (USML) covers the defense electronics project (e.g., Category XI for military electronics, Category XII for fire control, etc.).
- **Define the scope of technical data**: "Technical data" under ITAR (22 C.F.R. § 120.33) includes information required for the design, development, production, manufacture, assembly, operation, repair, testing, maintenance, or modification of defense articles. This includes specifications, drawings, software source code, manuals, and even verbal discussions.
- **Identify what the engineer will actually access**: Map out exactly which technical data elements are ITAR-controlled versus those that may be publicly available, EAR-controlled, or otherwise uncontrolled. Not all information on the project may be ITAR-controlled.

---

## Step 3: Determine if an Exemption Applies

Before applying for a license, evaluate whether a valid ITAR exemption covers the intended activities. The most relevant exemptions for this scenario include:

### 3a. NATO Exemption (22 C.F.R. § 126.16)
- Germany is a NATO member. This exemption may authorize certain defense services and technical data transfers among NATO member governments and their contractors.
- **Important caveats**: The exemption applies to government-to-government activities or activities in furtherance of a government contract. The engineer must be working on a project under a NATO contract or agreement. Specific conditions and documentation requirements apply.
- This exemption does **not** apply to all defense electronics work — review the regulation carefully.

### 3b. U.S. Government Agencies Exemption (22 C.F.R. § 126.4)
- If the project is conducted under a U.S. government contract that explicitly authorizes the foreign national's participation, verify whether the contracting agency has a relevant agreement (e.g., a Government-to-Government (G2G) agreement with Germany).

### 3c. Technical Data in the Public Domain (22 C.F.R. § 120.33)
- If some of the technical data is already in the public domain (published, ITAR-approved public release), that specific data does not require authorization for sharing.

### 3d. Fundamental Research Exemption (22 C.F.R. § 120.34)
- Likely **does not apply** to a defense electronics project with controlled technical data, as this exemption is typically for basic and applied research at accredited institutions without access restrictions.

**If no exemption clearly applies, you must obtain a license or other authorization before the engineer arrives.**

---

## Step 4: Apply for the Appropriate ITAR Authorization (If No Exemption Applies)

If an exemption does not cover the activities, you must apply to DDTC for one of the following:

### 4a. DSP-5: Permanent Export License for Technical Data
- Used for the export (including deemed export) of unclassified technical data.
- Filing is done through DDTC's Defense Export Control and Compliance System (DECCS).
- **Processing time**: DDTC's average processing time is 35-45 days for routine applications, but can be significantly longer. **Do not wait until the last minute.**
- Include a detailed technical description of the data to be shared, the USML category, end-use statement, and information about the receiving party (the German engineer and their employer).

### 4b. Technical Assistance Agreement (TAA) (22 C.F.R. § 124.1)
- Required if the collaboration involves providing defense services (not just data), or if there is an ongoing relationship involving the transfer of know-how.
- A TAA is a contract that must be approved by DDTC before execution.
- Processing time is similar to or longer than a DSP-5.

### 4c. Manufacturing License Agreement (MLA) (22 C.F.R. § 124.6)
- Required if the German engineer will be involved in manufacturing defense articles.

**Recommendation**: Engage your export control officer or outside export counsel immediately to determine the correct authorization type and begin the application process.

---

## Step 5: Pre-Arrival Internal Compliance Measures

While awaiting or alongside license applications, implement the following:

### 5a. Engage Your Export Control Officer (ECO)
- Your organization should have a designated ECO or compliance point of contact. Loop them in immediately.
- If you do not have an ECO, consult with legal counsel specializing in ITAR/export control.

### 5b. Notify and Involve the Project Contracting Officer (if applicable)
- If this is a government contract, notify your Contracting Officer (CO) and review your contract for any foreign national access restrictions (e.g., DD Form 254, Security Classification Specification).
- The DD Form 254 may explicitly prohibit or restrict foreign national access — this is an absolute bar regardless of ITAR licensing.

### 5c. Obtain Facility Security Officer (FSO) Approval (if applicable)
- If your facility holds a Facility Clearance (FCL) under the National Industrial Security Program Operating Manual (NISPOM), your FSO must be involved. The Defense Counterintelligence and Security Agency (DCSA) must be notified of planned foreign national visits to cleared facilities.
- A **Foreign National Visit (FNV)** request may need to be submitted to DCSA, potentially requiring government approval before the visit.
- For German nationals, there are established processes under the U.S.-Germany bilateral security agreement.

### 5d. Implement an Access Control Plan
- Define and document exactly which areas of your facility the engineer may access.
- Restrict physical and electronic access to only the ITAR-controlled data covered by your authorization.
- Segregate any classified, sensitive, or unauthorized data from areas where the visitor will work.
- Implement need-to-know limitations — the engineer should only access data directly relevant to their specific collaboration role.

### 5e. Prepare Required Documentation
Compile and have ready:
- Copy of the approved ITAR license or exemption citation
- Passport copy and visa information for the engineer
- Results of restricted party screening
- Visitor non-disclosure agreement (NDA) — tailor for ITAR obligations
- End-use certificate or statement of end use
- Visitor log/access records

---

## Step 6: Prepare Required Agreements with the Engineer and Their Employer

### 6a. Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)
- Execute a comprehensive NDA that includes ITAR-specific language: prohibiting re-transfer of controlled technical data to third parties or third countries without U.S. government authorization.
- The NDA should reference the specific USML category and the scope of authorized access.

### 6b. Employee/Visitor ITAR Briefing and Certification
- Provide a written ITAR briefing to the engineer before or upon arrival.
- The briefing should cover: what ITAR is, what data they are authorized to access, prohibitions on re-export or retransfer, and consequences of violations.
- Obtain a signed acknowledgment that they understand their obligations.

### 6c. Agreements with the German Employer (if applicable)
- If the engineer's German employer will also receive or use the technical data, a TAA or DSP-5 covering that employer may also be required.
- Ensure the German company is not on any restricted party list.

---

## Step 7: IT and Cybersecurity Controls

ITAR-controlled technical data in electronic form requires specific controls:

- **Network access**: Do not give the visitor access to your general corporate network. Provide access only to systems containing the specific ITAR data covered by your authorization.
- **Email and file transfer**: Monitor and restrict the ability to email or transfer ITAR-controlled files. Use encrypted, access-controlled repositories.
- **BYOD prohibition**: Do not allow the engineer to use personal devices to access ITAR data. Provide a facility-managed device if needed.
- **Physical media**: Control USB drives, external hard drives, and printing of ITAR data. Log all removable media use.
- **Cloud storage**: Do not use unapproved cloud storage for ITAR data — most standard cloud services (e.g., commercial Google Drive, Dropbox) are not authorized for ITAR storage without specific ITAR-compliant configurations.

---

## Step 8: During the Visit — Ongoing Compliance

- **Escort requirements**: Depending on your facility security requirements, the engineer may need to be escorted at all times in controlled areas.
- **Meeting monitoring**: If meetings involve ITAR technical discussions, ensure only authorized personnel (with appropriate clearances or authorization) are present. Document attendees.
- **Visitor log**: Maintain a detailed visitor log recording dates, times, areas accessed, and data accessed.
- **No unapproved discussions**: Ensure your employees know not to share any ITAR-controlled data beyond the scope of the approved authorization, even in casual conversation.

---

## Step 9: Post-Visit Compliance

- **Retrieve all access credentials**: Revoke facility access, badge, and system credentials on the last day.
- **Document retention**: Retain all ITAR-related records (license, visitor logs, NDAs, briefing acknowledgments, access records) for a minimum of **5 years** as required by 22 C.F.R. § 122.5.
- **Report any violations**: If any unauthorized disclosure occurred, report it to DDTC as a Voluntary Disclosure (22 C.F.R. § 127.12) promptly. Voluntary disclosure is a significant mitigating factor in DDTC enforcement actions.

## Critical Warnings

1. **Do not allow access to any ITAR-controlled data until authorization is confirmed.** A license application is not authorization — only an approved license is.
2. **Three weeks is a very short lead time for a DDTC license application.** If you have not already started the process, contact DDTC or legal counsel immediately. DDTC does not typically offer expedited processing except in extraordinary circumstances.
3. **The NATO exemption is not automatic** — it has strict conditions. Do not assume it applies without careful legal review.
4. **Verbal discussions of technical data are controlled** — ITAR applies to oral, written, and electronic transfers of technical data. Brief all employees who will interact with the visitor.
5. **Consult legal counsel**: This analysis is informational. Given the complexity of ITAR and the potential consequences of violations, you should obtain advice from a qualified export control attorney before proceeding.
