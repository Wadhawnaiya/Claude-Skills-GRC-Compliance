# ITAR Compliance: Foreign National Visiting for Defense Electronics Project

## Summary

Hosting a German engineer at your facility to collaborate on a defense electronics project involving ITAR-controlled technical data triggers **deemed export** obligations under 22 CFR § 120.50. You must complete several compliance steps **before the engineer arrives** — failure to do so could constitute an unlicensed export of technical data, exposing your organization to civil penalties of up to $1,369,000 per violation and criminal penalties up to $1,000,000 and 20 years imprisonment per violation (22 CFR Part 127; 22 USC § 2778).

---

## 1. Recognize This as a Deemed Export Situation

Under **22 CFR § 120.50**, a "deemed export" occurs when ITAR-controlled technical data is released to a foreign national inside the United States. That release is treated as an export to the foreign national's **country of citizenship** — in this case, Germany.

**Critical point on citizenship vs. visa status**: The deemed export determination is based on the engineer's **nationality (German citizenship)**, not their work visa type, work authorization status, or length of stay. A German national on any visa category — tourist, J-1, H-1B, etc. — is treated as a deemed export to Germany. Do not assume that a US work authorization or long-term visa changes this analysis.

**Persons exempt from deemed export rules** (22 CFR § 120.62) are limited to US persons: US citizens, lawful permanent residents ("green card" holders), and protected persons under 8 USC § 1324b. A visiting German engineer does not fall into any of these categories.

---

## 2. Determine Whether a License or TAA Is Required

Germany is not subject to a comprehensive ITAR arms embargo (22 CFR § 126.1), so the analysis turns on the nature of the technical data and whether any license exemption applies.

**Step 1 — Classify the technical data**: Confirm the specific USML category (22 CFR § 121.1) covering the defense electronics project data (e.g., Category XI – Military Electronics, Category XV – Spacecraft, or another applicable category). The license requirement applies to technical data directly related to defense articles on the USML.

**Step 2 — Assess exemptions**: Review whether any narrow exemption under 22 CFR Parts 123–126 applies. For most defense electronics collaboration involving a foreign national's access to controlled technical data, no general exemption will cover the disclosure.

**Step 3 — Obtain authorization before access is granted**: If no exemption applies, you must obtain one of the following **before** the engineer is permitted to access any ITAR-controlled technical data, areas, systems, or discussions:

- **Technical Assistance Agreement (TAA)** under 22 CFR § 124.1: This is the standard authorization for sharing ITAR-controlled technical data and providing defense services to a foreign person. It must identify all parties, define the precise scope of data and services, include mandatory clauses (retransfer prohibition, US government rights, record-keeping), and be submitted via DDTC's D-Trade portal. **Processing time is typically 30–60 days** — begin this process well in advance of the visit.

- **Individual Export License (DSP-5 or technical data license under 22 CFR Part 125)**: An individual license may be appropriate if the scope of access is narrow and a full TAA is not warranted. Consult your Empowered Official (EO) and legal counsel to determine which instrument is appropriate.

> **Do not allow the engineer access to any ITAR-controlled data, systems, or discussions until the TAA or individual license is in place and approved by DDTC.**

---

## 3. Screen the Visitor Against Restricted Parties Lists

Before the visit is confirmed and again before the engineer arrives, conduct and document screening against:

- **DDTC Debarred Parties List** (22 CFR § 127.7) — persons debarred from ITAR activities
- **OFAC Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List** — persons subject to US sanctions
- **BIS Denied Persons List, Entity List, and Unverified List** — persons restricted under EAR (relevant for thoroughness)
- **US State Department Watch Lists**

Screening must be documented with the date run, lists checked, search terms used, and result. Retain records for 5 years (22 CFR § 122.5). If any screening returns a potential match, do not proceed until your legal/compliance team has cleared the match.

---

## 4. Technology Control Plan (TCP) — Visitor Procedures

Your TCP (required as a matter of ITAR compliance best practice and often as a condition of TAA/license approval) must include procedures governing foreign visitor access. Before the visit:

**a. Physical access controls**
- Define which areas of your facility the engineer is authorized to access.
- Restrict access to areas where ITAR-controlled hardware, data, or discussions are present to only those covered by the TAA/license scope.
- Assign an escort for any area where the engineer's access is not explicitly authorized.
- Issue visitor badges that visually distinguish the engineer from cleared personnel.

**b. IT and data access controls**
- Segregate ITAR-controlled networks, file shares, and systems from any systems the engineer will use.
- Do not provide login credentials or access to shared drives, repositories, or collaboration platforms that contain ITAR-controlled data unless specifically covered by the authorization and your TCP controls.
- If the engineer will use a laptop or device, ensure it is not connected to systems containing unauthorized ITAR data.

**c. Visitor log and documentation**
- Maintain a visitor log documenting: name, citizenship, purpose of visit, dates, areas accessed, escort name, and TAA/license reference number.
- Brief the engineer on ITAR obligations upon arrival: no photography of controlled items, no retransfer of data to third parties, no disclosure to other foreign nationals not covered by the agreement.

**d. Brief relevant staff**
- Inform all personnel who will interact with the engineer about the scope of authorized access — what they may and may not discuss or share.

## 5. Pre-Arrival Checklist Summary

| Action | Regulatory Basis | Timing |
|--------|-----------------|--------|
| Identify USML category for project technical data | 22 CFR § 121.1 | Immediately |
| Confirm engineer's citizenship (not just visa) | 22 CFR § 120.50 | Immediately |
| Determine TAA or individual license requirement | 22 CFR § 124.1; 22 CFR Part 125 | Immediately |
| Apply for and receive TAA/license from DDTC (D-Trade) | 22 CFR § 124.1 | 30–60 days before visit |
| Screen engineer against DDTC Debarred, OFAC SDN, and BIS lists | 22 CFR § 127.7; OFAC regulations | Before confirming visit; again before arrival |
| Update TCP to include visitor procedures and access scope | 22 CFR compliance best practice | Before visit |
| Define and enforce physical access restrictions | TCP; TAA conditions | Before arrival |
| Segregate ITAR-controlled IT systems and data | TCP | Before arrival |
| Brief staff on authorized disclosure scope | TCP; 22 CFR § 124.9 | Before arrival |
| Establish visitor log | 22 CFR § 122.5 | Day of arrival |
| Brief engineer on ITAR obligations | TAA conditions | Day of arrival |

---

## 6. Key Risk Reminders

- **Do not allow the visit to begin before DDTC authorization is received.** Even informal conversations about ITAR-controlled technical data constitute a deemed export and require a license.
- **Oral disclosures count.** A deemed export violation can occur in a meeting room, hallway, or phone call — not only through document transfer.
- **Three weeks is a long engagement.** Ensure the TAA or license scope fully covers all data, services, and discussions planned for the entire visit, including any incidental access.
- **Germany is a close US ally, but ITAR still applies.** The Australia/UK Treaty exemption (22 CFR § 126.7) does not extend to Germany; no blanket NATO-partner exemption exists under ITAR.

---

*This guidance is based on 22 CFR Parts 120–130 (ITAR) as currently in effect. Consult your Empowered Official and export control legal counsel to confirm the appropriate authorization instrument and verify exemption availability for your specific project.*
