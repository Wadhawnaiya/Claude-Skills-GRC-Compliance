# Technical Assistance Agreement (TAA) for Missile Guidance System Design Data — India

## 1. Regulatory Framework and USML Classification

Sharing missile guidance system design data with a foreign defence manufacturer requires a **Technical Assistance Agreement (TAA)** under **22 CFR § 124.1**. This is the mandatory authorisation instrument for the export of ITAR-controlled technical data and defence services to foreign persons.

### USML Classification

Missile guidance systems fall under **USML Category IV** (22 CFR § 121.1, Category IV): "Launch Vehicles, Guided Missiles, Ballistic Missiles, Rockets, Torpedoes, Bombs and Mines." This category covers all military rockets, missiles, and associated guidance and control systems.

Additionally, components of the guidance system may independently fall under:
- **USML Category XII**: Fire Control, Guidance and Control Equipment (military gyroscopes, targeting systems, guidance computers)
- **USML Category XI**: Military Electronics (navigation and targeting electronics)

All related technical data — blueprints, drawings, specifications, software, test reports — constitutes **ITAR-controlled technical data** under 22 CFR § 120.33 and cannot be exported without a TAA or other DDTC authorisation.

**A TAA is the correct and required instrument** here, not a DSP-5 (which covers hardware) or an MLA (which covers licensed foreign manufacture).

---

## 2. India-Specific Considerations

India is a **Major Defence Partner** of the United States and has benefited from significant defence trade cooperation, including a foundational agreement (GSOMIA/COMCASA/BECA/LEMOA framework). However, this status **does not exempt** US exporters from ITAR licence requirements.

Key points:
- India is **not** a treaty partner under the Australia–UK–US Defence Trade Cooperation Treaties (22 CFR §§ 126.6–126.7), which provide streamlined licensing only for the UK and Australia
- India is **not** covered by the Canada exemption (22 CFR § 126.5)
- A full DDTC-approved TAA is **required** before sharing any ITAR-controlled technical data with the Indian defence manufacturer
- India is not on the 22 CFR § 126.1 comprehensive arms embargo list, so approval is in principle available, subject to DDTC and interagency review

## 3. Mandatory TAA Clauses (22 CFR § 124.9)

The following clauses are legally required in every DDTC-approved TAA:

### 3.1 Scope of Agreement
Precise and exhaustive description of the technical data and/or defence services to be shared. For a missile guidance system, this must identify:
- Specific design documents, drawings, specifications, and software
- System and subsystem descriptions (e.g., inertial navigation unit, guidance computer algorithms, seeker data)
- Any permitted defence services (e.g., engineering consultation, training, integration support)

Vague or overly broad scope descriptions will result in DDTC rejection or requests for additional information.

### 3.2 Identification of All Parties
- US licensor (your organisation) with DDTC registration number
- Foreign licensee (the Indian defence manufacturer), full legal name and address
- Any authorised sub-licensees (must be individually named and approved by DDTC)
- No additional parties may access the technical data without a TAA amendment

### 3.3 Retransfer Prohibition
The TAA must expressly prohibit the foreign licensee from further disclosing, transferring, or retransferring the technical data to any third party — including other Indian government agencies, sub-contractors, or subsidiaries — without prior written approval from DDTC. This is a mandatory clause under 22 CFR § 124.9(a).

### 3.4 US Government Rights
The US Government (including DDTC, DoD, and designated agencies) must have the right to:
- Review all records relating to the TAA and technical data transfers
- Audit the foreign licensee's compliance with the agreement
- Terminate the TAA if required by national security or foreign policy considerations

This clause cannot be waived or modified.

### 3.5 Audit Rights
The US licensor must retain the right to audit the foreign licensee's compliance with the TAA, including access to relevant records, facilities, and personnel. This protects the US exporter and satisfies the US Government's oversight requirements.

### 3.6 Record-Keeping — 5-Year Retention
Both the US licensor and the foreign licensee must maintain all records associated with the TAA — including technical data transfer logs, correspondence, end-use records, and any sub-licensing records — for a minimum of **5 years** (22 CFR § 122.5). Records must be available for inspection by DDTC, US Customs, DoD, or any authorised US Government agency.

### 3.7 Term and Renewal
- TAAs are valid for a maximum of **5 years**
- Renewal requires a new DDTC application before expiry
- The agreement must state the effective date and expiry date

### 3.8 Security Classification Handling (if applicable)
If any of the missile guidance design data is classified, the TAA must include provisions for handling classified information in accordance with US Government security requirements, including applicable National Disclosure Policy (NDP-1) authorisation.

### 3.9 Amendment Requirement
The TAA must state that any change to the scope, parties, authorised countries, or technical data requires a formal amendment approved by DDTC before taking effect.

## 4. Congressional Notification Requirement

Because missile guidance systems are **Significant Military Equipment (SME)** under ITAR and represent a major defence capability transfer, the following applies:

- Under **22 USC § 2776**, the US Government is required to notify Congress before approving certain defence article and service transfers
- For commercially licensed sales or transfers involving SME to countries such as India, congressional notification thresholds may apply (typically for transfers valued at **$14 million or more** for SME, or where national security significance warrants it)
- DDTC will conduct interagency review (including DoD/DSCA, Department of Commerce, and intelligence community) prior to approval
- The nature of missile guidance systems — a Category IV USML item with direct strategic and proliferation implications — is likely to trigger heightened scrutiny and may require a formal congressional notification period (typically **30 days** for NATO allies/Australia/Japan/NZ/Israel/South Korea, or **15 days** for others) before DDTC may issue final approval

Your organisation should plan for this additional timeline in project scheduling and engage legal counsel and a US export control consultant early in the process.

## 5. DDTC Approval Process — Step by Step

### Step 1: Confirm DDTC Registration
Verify that your organisation is currently registered with DDTC under 22 CFR § 122.1. Registration must be active before submitting a TAA application. Registration is via the DDTC portal at registration.pmddtc.state.gov using Form DS-2032.

### Step 2: Draft the TAA
Prepare the TAA document incorporating all mandatory clauses listed in Section 3 above. Legal counsel experienced in ITAR is strongly recommended. The agreement must:
- Be in English
- Clearly identify all technical data and defence services
- Include all required 22 CFR § 124.9 clauses

### Step 3: Submit via D-Trade Portal
Submit the TAA application through DDTC's **D-Trade electronic portal** (dtrade.state.gov). The submission package typically includes:
- The draft TAA agreement (PDF)
- Cover letter explaining the purpose and scope
- Description of the foreign licensee, including ownership information and any government affiliation
- End-use statement from the Indian manufacturer
- Details of the programme or contract the TAA supports
- Any relevant US Government programme documentation (if applicable)

### Step 4: DDTC Review and Interagency Coordination
- Standard processing time: **30–60 days** for most TAAs
- For Category IV items (missiles/guidance systems), expect extended review due to interagency coordination requirements and potential congressional notification
- DDTC may issue a Request for Additional Information (RAI) — respond promptly to avoid further delays
- DDTC conducts review in consultation with DoD (DSCA), Department of Commerce, and the intelligence community

### Step 5: Congressional Notification (if triggered)
If the transfer meets the threshold for congressional notification under 22 USC § 2776, DDTC will notify Congress. A mandatory review period (15 or 30 days) must pass before DDTC issues the TAA approval, unless Congress acts to block it.

### Step 6: TAA Issuance
Once approved, DDTC issues the TAA with assigned agreement number, conditions, and an expiry date. No technical data may be shared until the approved TAA is in hand.

### Step 7: Implementation Controls
After approval:
- Implement a Technology Control Plan (TCP) covering access controls, data segregation, and training
- Maintain transfer logs for all disclosures made under the TAA
- Brief the Indian manufacturer on their obligations under the TAA
- Track the 5-year expiry and initiate renewal proceedings well in advance

## 7. Key Risk Warnings

1. **No transfer before TAA approval**: Sharing any missile guidance design data — including drawings, specifications, emails, or oral briefings — with the Indian manufacturer prior to DDTC TAA approval is an ITAR violation subject to criminal penalties up to $1,000,000 per violation and 20 years imprisonment (22 USC § 2778).

2. **Deemed export risk**: If Indian nationals employed at your US facilities have access to the missile guidance design data, a deemed export licence (TAA or individual licence) is required for those individuals before access is granted (22 CFR § 120.50).

3. **Third-party sub-contractors**: If the Indian manufacturer intends to involve sub-contractors, each must be individually authorised in the TAA or a TAA amendment — unauthorised sub-licensing is a violation.

4. **Amendment discipline**: Any expansion of scope, addition of parties, or change in authorised countries requires a DDTC-approved amendment before the change is implemented.
