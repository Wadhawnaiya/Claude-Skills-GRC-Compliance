SECTION 508 UNDUE BURDEN EXCEPTION: GUIDANCE FOR FEDERAL AGENCIES

QUESTION: Can a small federal agency claim an undue burden exception for a legacy grants management system with $3.5M remediation costs that exceed the entire IT budget?

SHORT ANSWER: Yes, an undue burden exception may be available, but it is not a full exemption from accessibility obligations. The agency must follow a specific process, document the determination formally, and still provide alternative means of access to people with disabilities.

---

1. LEGAL BASIS

The undue burden exception for federal agencies is grounded in two primary authorities:

a) Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended — codified at 29 U.S.C. § 794d. This statute requires federal agencies to ensure that electronic and information technology (EIT) developed, procured, maintained, or used by the federal government is accessible to people with disabilities, but it includes provisions for exceptions where compliance would impose an undue burden.

b) E202.5 of the Revised Section 508 Standards (36 CFR Part 1194), published by the U.S. Access Board (effective January 18, 2018). This provision explicitly establishes the Undue Burden Exception, stating that an agency is not required to comply with Section 508 requirements where doing so would impose an "undue burden" on the agency. This is the operative regulatory provision governing the process and scope of the exception.

---

2. WHAT "UNDUE BURDEN" MEANS AND THE FACTORS CONSIDERED

Under E202.5 and the broader framework of Section 508, "undue burden" is defined as significant difficulty or expense. This is not a bright-line financial threshold — it is a fact-specific determination that must weigh multiple factors, including:

- The overall financial resources of the agency as a whole (not just the component or program office managing the system)
- The size of the agency, including number of employees
- The nature and cost of the specific accessibility compliance measures required
- The impact of the compliance cost relative to the agency's total IT budget and annual appropriations
- Whether alternative approaches exist that would reduce cost while still improving accessibility
- The type of operation of the agency and the financial impact on the agency's overall program

In your scenario, the $3.5M remediation cost relative to an entire IT budget is a strong factual predicate for an undue burden finding — but it must be documented and assessed against all available resources, not just the immediate IT budget line.

---

3. REQUIRED PROCESS: WRITTEN DETERMINATION BY AGENCY HEAD

The undue burden exception is NOT self-executing. Agencies cannot simply decide internally that compliance is too expensive and move on. The Revised Section 508 Standards require a formal process:

Step 1 — Document the Finding: The agency must prepare a written determination that explains the basis for the undue burden claim. This documentation must include:
  - A description of the ICT (Information and Communications Technology) at issue (the legacy grants management system)
  - The specific accessibility requirements that cannot be met
  - A detailed analysis of the costs of compliance (the $3.5M remediation estimate)
  - An analysis of the agency's available resources and why compliance would impose significant difficulty or expense
  - A description of the alternative means of access that will be provided

Step 2 — Signed by Agency Head or Designee: The written determination must be signed by the agency head or a duly authorized official to whom the agency head has formally delegated this authority. This cannot be a line manager or program officer decision alone — it requires senior leadership accountability.

Step 3 — Maintain the Documentation: The agency must retain this documentation and make it available as needed (e.g., for audits, congressional oversight, or complaints filed with oversight bodies).

Step 4 — Periodic Re-evaluation: The undue burden determination is not permanent. It must be revisited and re-evaluated when circumstances change — specifically, if the ICT is subsequently altered, upgraded, or replaced, the agency must reassess whether full compliance is now achievable.

---

4. ALTERNATIVE MEANS OF ACCESS IS STILL REQUIRED

This is a critical point: the undue burden exception does NOT excuse the agency from all accessibility obligations. Even where a valid undue burden determination is made, the agency must still provide an alternative means of access to the information or functionality for individuals with disabilities.

This means the agency must:
- Identify what the grants management system does and what outputs/functions people with disabilities need
- Provide a functionally equivalent alternative pathway — this could include:
  * Accessible document versions of key data outputs
  * A staffed help desk or point of contact who can assist users with disabilities in accessing grants information
  * Accessible alternative formats (e.g., accessible PDFs, data exports in accessible formats)
  * Phone-based or in-person assistance to complete grant-related tasks
- Document what alternative means of access will be provided in the written undue burden determination

The alternative access must be genuinely usable and not impose unreasonable burdens on the individual with a disability. Providing a phone number that goes unanswered, for example, would not satisfy this requirement.

---

5. RE-EVALUATION WHEN ICT IS ALTERED OR REPLACED

The undue burden exception is tied to the current state of the ICT and the agency's current resources. If the grants management system is:
- Significantly altered or upgraded
- Replaced with a new system
- Subject to a new procurement or modernization initiative

...then the agency must re-evaluate whether the undue burden exception still applies or whether the new system must be made fully Section 508 compliant from the outset. In practice, a modernization or re-procurement is an opportunity — and likely an obligation — to build in full accessibility compliance, since new systems do not inherit the undue burden exception of their predecessors.

---

6. PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATIONS

Given the scenario described:

a) Engage your agency's Section 508 Coordinator immediately — every federal agency is required to have one.

b) Commission a phased remediation analysis — the $3.5M figure may assume full immediate remediation; a phased approach over multiple budget cycles may reduce the annual burden and make compliance more achievable.

c) Consult with the agency's General Counsel before finalizing the undue burden determination — the written determination must withstand legal scrutiny.

d) Notify the Access Board and OMB if required — some agencies are required to report accessibility exceptions through established reporting mechanisms.

e) Document the alternative means of access in detail, and test it with actual users with disabilities where possible.

f) Include Section 508 compliance requirements in any future modernization planning or budget requests to ensure successor systems are fully compliant.
