EXPORT COMPLIANCE PROGRAMME DESIGN FOR A 200-PERSON SEMICONDUCTOR EQUIPMENT COMPANY
=====================================================================================
Prepared under the Export Administration Regulations (EAR), 15 CFR Parts 730–774
Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS), U.S. Department of Commerce

Company profile: 200 employees, semiconductor capital equipment manufacturer, export
destinations: Europe, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, occasional India.

======================================================================================
PART ONE: THE SEVEN BIS ELEMENTS OF AN EFFECTIVE EXPORT COMPLIANCE PROGRAMME (ECP)
======================================================================================

BIS has identified seven elements of an effective ECP. A documented, fully functioning
ECP is the single largest mitigating factor in an enforcement action — it can reduce
civil penalties substantially and, in borderline cases, result in a Warning Letter rather
than a monetary penalty. For a semiconductor equipment company, the stakes are
particularly high: your core products almost certainly fall under ECCNs 3B001 and/or
3B002, which carry NS, AT, and NP controls and are central to the Foreign Direct Product
Rule (FDPR) regime for advanced chips. Here is how to build each element.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 1 — MANAGEMENT COMMITMENT
----------------------------------------------------------------------

What BIS expects:
  Senior leadership must visibly and substantively champion export compliance — not
  simply sign a policy and move on. This means resources, authority, and accountability
  flowing from the top.

Specific actions for your company:

  1.1 Export Compliance Policy
      Draft a written, board-approved Export Compliance Policy signed by the CEO or
      President. The policy must state that compliance with EAR (and any applicable ITAR
      obligations) is a condition of doing business, that violations will not be tolerated,
      and that resources will be provided to maintain the programme.

  1.2 Designated Export Control Officer (ECO)
      Appoint an ECO in writing. For a 200-person company with semiconductor equipment
      exports to Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and India, this should be a dedicated
      position, not a collateral duty. The ECO must have direct access to senior leadership
      and authority to stop shipments. Document the ECO's authority, reporting line, and
      budget.

  1.3 Compliance Reporting Structure
      Implement quarterly compliance reporting to the CEO/Board. Reports should cover:
      screening results, classification updates, licence status, training completion rates,
      and any identified compliance gaps or near-misses.

  1.4 Annual ECP Review Certification
      The ECO should certify annually to the board that the ECP is operating effectively,
      identify any gaps, and propose remediation with timelines and budgets.

  1.5 Resources
      Commit budget for: screening software (see Element 5), ERP integration, legal
      counsel with EAR expertise, and periodic third-party ECP audits. BIS rewards
      companies that invest in compliance infrastructure, not just paper policies.

  Semiconductor-specific note: Given the heightened scrutiny on semiconductor
  manufacturing equipment — particularly in light of the October 2022 and October 2023
  BIS rules on advanced computing — management commitment must include explicit
  attention to China-related transactions and FDPR applicability. Your CEO and board
  should be briefed on what these rules mean operationally.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 2 — RISK ASSESSMENT
----------------------------------------------------------------------

What BIS expects:
  A systematic identification of all items, transactions, customers, and channels that
  present export control risk, with documented rationale.

Specific actions:

  2.1 Product Classification Audit (Start Here)
      Every product in your portfolio must be classified under the EAR. For semiconductor
      capital equipment, the primary ECCNs are:

        3B001 — Equipment for the manufacture of semiconductor devices or materials:
          - Lithography equipment (photolithography, e-beam, ion beam)
          - Etch equipment (plasma, wet etch)
          - Deposition equipment (CVD, PVD, ALD, MBE)
          - Ion implantation equipment
          - Rapid thermal processing equipment
          Controls include: NS, AT, and in some cases NP or MT
          Note: 3B001.a through 3B001.j have specific technical parameter thresholds
          that determine control.

        3B002 — Equipment for the inspection and testing of semiconductor devices:
          - Wafer probers, testers, parametric test equipment
          - In-process and post-process metrology tools
          Controls: NS, AT

        3D001/3D002/3E001/3E002 — Corresponding software (D) and technology (E) for
          3B equipment. Software and technology ECCNs must be classified separately
          from the equipment itself.

        EAR99 — Lower-end equipment, spare parts, consumables with no CCL entry may
          be EAR99. Do not assume; verify against the CCL.

      Build an ECCN Classification Log with these fields for every product, subassembly,
      component, and software package:
        - Part number and description
        - Technical parameters reviewed (e.g., minimum feature size, throughput)
        - ECCN assigned
        - Reason(s) for Control (NS, AT, NP, etc.)
        - Date of classification
        - Classifier name
        - Next review date (trigger: product modification, new regulatory guidance)

  2.2 Geography Risk Assessment
      Your destination mix presents the following risk profile:

        Europe (EU/UK/EEA): Generally A:1, A:5 countries. Low-risk for most 3B001/3B002
          exports; many licence exceptions available (e.g., STA for A:5 countries). Still
          requires screening and classification.

        Japan: A:1, A:5. Low licence risk for most items. Japan is a Wassenaar Arrangement
          member and a close ally. Japan's own export controls (FEFTA/Foreign Exchange Act)
          mirror EAR in many respects. However, Japan-origin semiconductor equipment is
          subject to FDPR analysis.

        South Korea: A:1, A:5. Similar to Japan. Low licence risk for NS-controlled items.
          KITA alignment with multilateral regimes.

        Taiwan: Country Group B (not A:5). Taiwan is not a Wassenaar member but is
          treated favourably. However, proximity to China and active PRC pressure on
          Taiwan-based chipmakers (TSMC, UMC, etc.) elevates the diversion and re-export
          risk. Taiwan-based fab customers may be producing chips for Chinese end-users.
          Enhanced due diligence required.

        India: Country Group B. India is an occasional destination. India became an
          A:5-equivalent partner (U.S.-India iCET initiative) but for EAR purposes India
          remains Country Group B for most purposes. India has its own import controls
          under SCOMET. For 3B001/3B002 equipment, verify licence exception availability
          on a case-by-case basis.

  2.3 Customer/Channel Risk Assessment
      Segment customers by risk tier:
        Tier 1 (Low): Large, established IDMs and foundries in EU/Japan/Korea with
          documented end-use (Intel, Samsung, TSMC, Infineon, etc.)
        Tier 2 (Medium): Distributors, trading companies, smaller fabs; Taiwan and
          India customers; customers whose end-use is not wholly transparent
        Tier 3 (High): Any customer with connections to China; distributors who
          sub-distribute in D:1 countries; any customer on or near any restricted list

  2.4 Technology Risk Assessment (Deemed Exports)
      For a semiconductor equipment company, your engineers regularly work with
      3E001/3E002-controlled technology. Every foreign national employee or visitor who
      accesses controlled technology is a deemed export risk. Conduct a deemed export
      risk assessment covering: lab access, CAD/design files, process recipes, and
      system software source code.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 3 — WRITTEN POLICIES AND PROCEDURES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

What BIS expects:
  Procedure-level written guidance for every process that touches controlled items or
  transactions. A high-level policy alone is insufficient.

Procedures your company needs:

  3.1 ECCN Classification Procedure
      - Who classifies (ECO + engineering)
      - How to access the CCL (Part 774)
      - How to complete and maintain the Classification Log
      - Triggers for reclassification (product change, new guidance)
      - Use of CCATS (Commodity Classification Automated Tracking System) for
        official BIS classification when uncertain

  3.2 Restricted Party Screening Procedure (see Element 5 for full detail)
      - Which lists to screen
      - When to screen (order acceptance, pre-shipment, periodic re-screen)
      - How to handle a potential match
      - Escalation path

  3.3 Order Acceptance and Licence Determination Procedure
      - Sales team check: Does the customer, destination, or stated end-use raise flags?
      - ECO check: Is a licence required? Is a licence exception available?
      - Approval workflow before accepting an order that triggers a licence requirement
      - "Hold for compliance" authority (ECO can stop any shipment)

  3.4 Licence Application and Management Procedure
      - How to apply via SNAP-R (snap-r.bis.doc.gov)
      - Documentation required for 3B001/3B002 applications (technical specs,
        end-user statements on BIS-711 form, end-use certificates)
      - Licence condition tracking (re-export authorisations, reporting requirements)
      - Licence expiry calendar and renewal process (apply 60 days before expiry)

  3.5 End-User Statement and Due Diligence Procedure
      - When an End-User Statement (EUS) is required (high-risk transactions,
        Tier 2/Tier 3 customers, any 3B001 to Country Group B)
      - Use of BIS Form BIS-711 (Statement by Ultimate Consignee and Purchaser)
      - KYC due diligence steps for new distributors and resellers
      - Distributor agreement provisions (downstream compliance obligations,
        right to audit, no-reexport-to-D:1 clauses)

  3.6 Deemed Export Procedure
      - Pre-hire screening for foreign national candidates who will access controlled tech
      - Access control for controlled technology (lab, CAD tools, source code repositories)
      - Deemed export licence application process via SNAP-R
      - Most-restrictive nationality rule for dual nationals (§ 734.13)

  3.7 FDPR Assessment Procedure
      - For all foreign-manufactured products incorporating your US technology or
        produced on equipment you manufactured: FDPR analysis checklist
      - Two-prong test (technology/software prong + destination prong)
      - Documentation of FDPR analysis for each product line

  3.8 Recordkeeping Procedure
      - List of record types to retain (see Element 7)
      - Five-year retention from date of export (§ 762.6)
      - Electronic storage, backup, and retrieval requirements
      - Response to BIS inspection requests (generally within 5 business days)

  3.9 Voluntary Self-Disclosure (VSD) Procedure
      - How to recognise a potential violation
      - Immediate escalation to ECO and legal counsel
      - Preliminary Inquiry process
      - Criteria for filing an initial notification to OEE
      - Full VSD submission within 180 days
      - Internal documentation during VSD process

  3.10 Trade Show, Demo, and Visitor Access Procedure
      - Controls on technology disclosed during trade shows or customer site visits
      - Foreign visitor access to controlled equipment or technology in US facilities
      - Temporary export of demo equipment (TMP licence exception, § 740.9)

ELEMENT 4 — TRAINING AND AWARENESS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

What BIS expects:
  Mandatory, role-appropriate training for all employees who may touch controlled
  transactions, with documented completion and annual refresh.

Training programme structure:

  4.1 Role-Based Training Tracks

      Track A — All Employees (1-hour annual awareness):
        - What is export control and why it matters
        - EAR vs. ITAR (don't assume semiconductor equipment is ITAR-free; verify)
        - What is an ECCN and what is EAR99
        - Restricted parties — what they are and why you must not engage
        - Red flag recognition (15 red flags from Supplement 3 to Part 732)
        - How to report concerns (escalation path to ECO)

      Track B — Sales, Business Development, Customer Success (2-3 hours annual):
        - Deeper dive on licence requirements and exceptions for your destination mix
        - End-user statements and due diligence
        - How to handle a customer who pushes back on compliance requests
        - Red flags in customer inquiries
        - Distributor management requirements

      Track C — Engineering, R&D, Product Management (2-3 hours annual):
        - Deemed exports — what they are and how they arise (visual access, oral briefings,
          electronic transmission, source code access)
        - Most-restrictive nationality rule
        - FDPR — when your technology creates FDPR obligations for foreign manufacturers
        - 3B001/3B002 technical parameter thresholds — when does a design change affect
          the ECCN?
        - Cloud and repository access controls for controlled technology
        - Fundamental research exclusion and its limits

      Track D — ECO, Legal, Finance (deep dive, 4-8 hours, plus continuing education):
        - Complete ECCN classification methodology
        - Licence exception analysis (all 14 exceptions in Part 740)
        - SNAP-R licence application process
        - FDPR comprehensive analysis
        - Enforcement regime (civil and criminal penalties, § 764)
        - VSD process (§ 764.5)
        - Advanced computing rules (October 2022/2023 BIS rules)
        - Entity List additions and update monitoring

      Track E — HR and Recruiting:
        - Deemed export screening in the hiring process
        - Foreign national pre-hire compliance checklist
        - How to handle a candidate who triggers deemed export concern

  4.2 Training Administration
      - Annual mandatory training for all tracks; completion required within 30 days
        of hire for new employees and annually thereafter
      - Track completion records retained for 5 years
      - Signed acknowledgement by each employee
      - Training content updated whenever regulations change materially (BIS
        entity list additions, new FDPR rules, new licence exceptions)

  4.3 Practical Exercises
      - Annual tabletop exercise for the ECO and leadership: a realistic scenario
        (e.g., discovery of shipments to an Entity List party's subsidiary; FDPR
        analysis of a new OEM partnership with a Japanese company; a deemed export
        situation with a newly hired Chinese national engineer)

ELEMENT 5 — RESTRICTED PARTY SCREENING
----------------------------------------------------------------------

What BIS expects:
  Screening of all parties to every transaction against all applicable government lists,
  on a defined cadence, with documented procedures for handling matches.

5.1 Mandatory Lists to Screen

  You must screen against, at minimum, the following lists:

  BIS Lists (15 CFR Part 744 and Part 764):
    (a) Entity List (Supplement 4 to Part 744)
        - Effect: Licence required for ALL items subject to EAR, destined for or involving
          listed entities; most licence exceptions unavailable
        - Key relevance: Many Chinese semiconductor companies and research institutes
          are on the Entity List. Taiwan-based customers producing for Chinese
          Entity List companies create a prohibited-end-user risk.

    (b) Denied Persons List (Part 764)
        - Effect: Absolute prohibition — you may not export, reexport, or transfer
          any item subject to EAR to or with a denied person; no exceptions
        - Applies to persons acting as agents, freight forwarders, banks, and intermediaries

    (c) Unverified List (Supplement 6 to Part 744)
        - Effect: Cannot use any licence exceptions; must obtain a UVL Statement from
          the party before proceeding with any transaction
        - Signals: Party's bona fides could not be verified by BIS end-use checks

    (d) Military End-User (MEU) List (Supplement 7 to Part 744)
        - Effect: Licence required for items listed in Supplement 2 to Part 744
          (which includes many semiconductor manufacturing items) to listed military
          end-users in China, Russia, and other D:5 countries

  State Department Lists:
    (e) DDTC Debarred List (22 CFR Part 127)
        - While ITAR-specific, debarred persons are prohibited from participating in
          ITAR transactions and frequently overlap with EAR concerns; best practice
          is to screen all parties against this list

  OFAC Lists (Treasury Department):
    (f) Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List
        - Effect: Full asset block; US persons may not transact with SDNs regardless
          of whether items are subject to EAR. Non-compliance is a Treasury matter,
          not BIS, but the prohibition is absolute.

    (g) OFAC Consolidated Sanctions List
        - Includes SDNs, Foreign Sanctions Evaders, Sectoral Sanctions (SSI list),
          Non-SDN Chinese Military-Industrial Complex (NS-CMIC) companies, and more

  Single-Search Resource:
    The Consolidated Screening List (CSL) at trade.gov/consolidated-screening-list
    combines BIS Entity List, Denied Persons List, Unverified List, and MEU List with
    State's Debarred List and OFAC's SDN and Consolidated Sanctions List into a single
    searchable interface. Use the CSL as your primary search tool, but supplement with
    direct source checks for high-risk transactions, as the CSL may have a brief lag
    behind individual agency publications.

5.2 Who to Screen

  Screen ALL parties to every transaction, not just the buyer. This includes:
    - Buyer / purchaser
    - End-user (if different from buyer)
    - All intermediate consignees
    - Freight forwarder and logistics providers
    - Customs brokers
    - Banks and financial intermediaries involved in payment
    - Brokers or agents
    - Parent companies and subsidiaries of any of the above (ownership/control analysis)

  For semiconductor equipment: Pay particular attention to end-users at the wafer
  fabrication level. Your direct customer may be a trading company; the actual fab
  is the end-user. Screen both.

5.3 When to Screen (Screening Cadence)

  At order inception:
    - Screen all parties when a quote is first requested or an order is received,
      before any commitment is made to the customer

  Before shipment:
    - Re-screen all parties immediately before each shipment; lists are updated
      continuously and a party clean at order acceptance may be listed by shipment date
    - This is especially important for long-cycle semiconductor equipment orders
      (equipment may be ordered 6-12 months before delivery)

  During ongoing relationships:
    - Re-screen existing distributors, agents, and regular customers at minimum
      monthly; for China-adjacent channels, consider weekly re-screening
    - Automate this via ERP integration (SAP GTS, Oracle AGIS, Visual Compliance,
      Descartes Denied Party Screening, or similar)

  When parties change:
    - Any change in ownership, address, banking details, or shipping instructions
      triggers an immediate re-screen — these are classic red flags for diversion

  When new lists are published:
    - BIS publishes Entity List amendments in the Federal Register, often with
      immediate effective dates. Subscribe to BIS update alerts at bis.gov.
      When a new list is published, run all active customers and open orders against it.

5.4 Handling a Potential Match

  When a screening result returns a potential match, follow this process:

  Step 1 — Do Not Ship
    Immediately place a hold on the order or shipment. No item subject to EAR may
    be exported or transferred while a potential match is unresolved.

  Step 2 — Escalate to ECO and Legal Counsel
    The screening analyst escalates to the ECO within 1 business day. ECO engages
    export control counsel for any non-trivial match.

  Step 3 — True Hit vs. False Positive Analysis
    Conduct a structured comparison:
      - Name: exact match, phonetic similarity, known aliases?
      - Address: same country, city, street?
      - Date of birth / incorporation date (for individuals/companies)
      - Ownership and control linkages
      - Nature of business compared to the listed reason for control
    Document the analysis in writing with the date and the analyst's name.

  Step 4 — False Positive Disposition
    If the match is clearly a different entity (e.g., same common name, different
    country, no other overlapping identifiers), document the false positive
    determination and the basis for it. Retain the record for 5 years. Proceed
    with the transaction.

  Step 5 — True Hit Disposition
    If the match is confirmed or cannot be ruled out:
      (a) Refuse the transaction — do not proceed with the sale, shipment, or service
      (b) Do not tip off the customer that they have been identified as a restricted
          party (while EAR has no explicit "tipping off" prohibition as severe as OFAC,
          alerting an SDN to their status creates additional exposure)
      (c) For SDN hits: OFAC may require reporting; consult counsel
      (d) Document the true hit, the decision to refuse, and all communications
      (e) If items have already shipped to a party that is subsequently listed,
          consult counsel immediately regarding potential VSD obligations (§ 764.5)

  Step 6 — Record Retention
    All screening records — positive, negative, and false positive — are retained
    for 5 years from the date of the transaction or screening event. For systematic
    screening, retain dated screenshots or system-generated reports.

5.5 Semiconductor-Equipment-Specific Screening Considerations

  China Channel Risk:
    Even for your legitimate destinations (Japan, Korea, Taiwan), screen for
    connections to Chinese entities. Taiwan-based customers often produce for
    Chinese fabs. A shipment to TSMC's fab in Taiwan is legitimate; a shipment
    to a trading company that re-sells to a Yangtze Memory or CXMT subsidiary
    is a potential violation. Your distributor agreements in Taiwan should require
    downstream screening and no-reexport clauses for any controlled equipment.

  Entity List Alumni:
    Monitor not only current Entity List entries but watch for successor entities,
    renamed subsidiaries, and shell companies. The Entity List entry for Huawei
    includes hundreds of non-designated affiliates; transactions with them still
    require BIS licensing analysis.

  India-Specific:
    India's Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT) SCOMET
    list controls some semiconductor manufacturing equipment. For Indian transactions,
    screen against SCOMET in addition to the US lists, and confirm your Indian
    customer's import authorisation for 3B001/3B002 equipment where required.

ELEMENT 6 — DUE DILIGENCE (KNOW YOUR CUSTOMER)
----------------------------------------------------------------------

What BIS expects:
  Going beyond list screening to actually know who your customers are, what they
  will do with your equipment, and whether your channel partners are reliable.

  6.1 Customer Onboarding KYC
      For all new customers and distributors:
        - Business registration documents
        - Ownership and control structure (beneficial ownership)
        - Description of end-use and end-facility
        - References from existing suppliers or customers
        - For high-risk Tier 2/3 customers: site visit or third-party due diligence report

  6.2 End-User Statements (EUS)
      For any 3B001/3B002 equipment sold to Country Group B customers, Taiwan customers,
      or any transaction where the end-use is not transparent from your direct sales
      relationship, obtain a signed End-User Statement covering:
        - Identity of the actual end-user and end-facility
        - Specific intended end-use of the equipment
        - Certification that the equipment will not be re-exported without BIS authorisation
        - Certification that the party is not acting as an agent for a third party not
          identified in the transaction

      Use BIS Form BIS-711 (Statement by Ultimate Consignee and Purchaser) as the
      baseline template. For Japanese and Korean customers, a Japanese/Korean language
      version with certified translation is acceptable.

  6.3 Distributor Agreements
      Distributor and reseller agreements must include:
        - Obligation to comply with US export laws (EAR, OFAC)
        - Prohibition on re-export or transfer to restricted parties or destinations
          without BIS authorisation
        - Flow-down of restricted party screening obligations
        - Right of your company to audit the distributor's compliance records
        - Termination right for export control violations
        - Record-retention obligation aligned with Part 762 (5 years)

  6.4 Red Flag Investigation
      BIS publishes 15 red flags in Supplement 3 to Part 732. Any of the following
      require you to pause the transaction, investigate, and document your findings:
        - Customer reluctant to provide end-use information or end-user identity
        - Ordered item is inconsistent with the customer's stated business
          (e.g., a general trading company ordering precision lithography equipment)
        - Payment from an unusual third-country account or unusual payment structure
        - Shipping route is circuitous or through unusual transshipment hubs
          (e.g., routing semiconductor equipment through Singapore or UAE to a
          destination that should receive direct shipments)
        - Customer declines installation, training, warranty, or service
          (unusual for complex capital equipment)
        - Customer uses a freight forwarder as the address of record
        - Customer's ordering volume or frequency is inconsistent with known capacity
        - Requests for unusual technical documentation or modifications

      For each red flag identified, document: the flag, your investigation, and your
      conclusion about whether the transaction is legitimate. Unresolved red flags
      are grounds to refuse a transaction.

  6.5 Post-Sale Monitoring
      For high-value or high-risk equipment:
        - Installation sign-off confirming equipment is at the declared end-facility
        - Periodic check-ins with customers (annual for low-risk, quarterly for Tier 2)
        - BIS may conduct post-shipment verifications (PSVs) at customer sites;
          cooperation is mandatory and confirms your compliance posture

ELEMENT 7 — RECORDKEEPING AND AUDITS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

What BIS expects:
  Retention of all export-related records for 5 years, with timely retrieval capability,
  and regular internal and external audits of the ECP.

  7.1 Recordkeeping Requirements (§ 762.6)

  Retain for 5 years from the date of export or reexport:

      Transaction Records:
        - Purchase orders and order confirmations
        - Commercial invoices
        - Packing lists
        - Bills of lading, air waybills, ocean bills
        - Electronic Export Information (EEI) filings via AES/ACE
        - Shipping instructions

      Classification Records:
        - ECCN Classification Log entries for each product
        - CCATS determinations (if obtained from BIS)
        - Classification rationale memoranda

      Screening Records:
        - Dated screenshots or system reports of every restricted party screening
          (positive, negative, and false positive)
        - False positive analysis documentation
        - True hit resolution documentation

      Licensing Records:
        - All SNAP-R licence applications (BIS-748P)
        - Issued licences and all conditions
        - Licence exception documentation (basis for each exception claimed)
        - End-user statements and BIS-711 forms
        - Advisory opinions received from BIS

      Due Diligence Records:
        - Customer KYC files
        - Distributor agreements and compliance certifications
        - Red flag investigation files

      Training Records:
        - Training materials, version history
        - Completion records with employee signatures/acknowledgements

      VSD Records (retain permanently, not just 5 years):
        - Initial notifications to OEE
        - Full VSD submissions
        - OEE correspondence
        - Internal investigation findings

  7.2 Record Format and Accessibility
      Records may be maintained in any readable electronic or paper format.
      Electronic storage must be backed up, with access controls preventing
      unauthorised alteration. BIS must be able to retrieve any record within
      a reasonable time (generally 5 business days of an OEE request).

  7.3 Internal Audit Programme
      Annual internal ECP audit covering:
        - Sample of screening records: were all parties screened, at the right cadence?
        - Sample of classification records: are ECCNs current and documented?
        - Sample of shipments: were correct ECCNs and licence authorities used
          on EEI filings?
        - Training completion rates
        - Licence condition compliance
        - Distributor agreement currency
        - Deemed export controls

      Audit findings reported to senior leadership; remediation tracked to closure.

  7.4 Periodic Third-Party Assessment
      For a semiconductor equipment company with 3B001/3B002 products and sales to
      Taiwan and India, conduct a third-party ECP assessment at minimum every 2-3 years.
      Third-party assessors provide defensible evidence of programme quality if BIS
      ever investigates. BIS's own penalty guidelines give substantial credit to
      companies with externally validated ECPs.

PART TWO: SEMICONDUCTOR-EQUIPMENT-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

----------------------------------------------------------------------
A. ECCN CLASSIFICATION: 3B001 AND 3B002 IN DEPTH
----------------------------------------------------------------------

3B001 — Equipment for manufacturing semiconductor devices and materials

This ECCN has approximately 10 sub-entries (3B001.a through 3B001.j), each with
specific technical parameter thresholds. Classification is not at the ECCN level —
you must determine the specific paragraph (e.g., 3B001.a.1 for photolithography
equipment using UV or deep UV light sources). Technical differences such as:
  - Minimum feature size achievable
  - Wavelength of light source
  - Alignment accuracy
  - Film thickness uniformity

...all affect which paragraph applies and, consequently, which reasons for control
apply (NS, AT, NP, MT). Your engineering team must be involved in classification —
this is not a task that can be done purely by legal or compliance staff.

Recommendation: For each product line, obtain a CCATS determination from BIS to
lock in an official classification. CCATS provides certainty and is valuable in
enforcement situations (good faith reliance on BIS classification is a mitigating
factor).

3B002 — Equipment specifically designed for the inspection or testing of semiconductor
devices or materials

Covers parametric test equipment, wafer probers, and certain metrology tools. Similarly
technical, with NS and AT controls in most cases.

Controls and destinations:
  For NS-controlled 3B001/3B002 equipment:
    - Europe (Country Group A:5): Licence exception STA (Strategic Trade Authorization,
      § 740.20) available for most NS items
    - Japan, South Korea (A:5): STA available; favourable
    - Taiwan (Country Group B): NS column 1 and 2 on the Country Chart — check
      for licence requirement; STA not available to Country Group B; may need
      licence or alternative exception (LVS if low value; TMP for demos)
    - India (Country Group B): Same analysis as Taiwan; case-by-case

----------------------------------------------------------------------
B. FOREIGN DIRECT PRODUCT RULE (FDPR) — CRITICAL FOR YOUR COMPANY
----------------------------------------------------------------------

As a semiconductor equipment manufacturer, the FDPR is not just something you must
comply with as an exporter — your products CREATE FDPR obligations for your customers
and foreign buyers of your equipment. This is a dual exposure:

  B.1 Your Outbound FDPR Obligations
      When you export US-origin technology (3E001) to a Japanese equipment manufacturer
      who then uses that technology to build equipment, that foreign-made equipment
      may be subject to EAR under the General FDPR if destined for D:1 or E countries.
      You must assess this in your technology licensing agreements.

  B.2 FDPR Triggered by Your Equipment
      Under the Entity List FDPR (Huawei Rule, 2020) and the Advanced Computing FDPR
      (October 2022/2023), semiconductor wafer fabrication equipment controlled under
      3B001 or 3B002 is specifically designated as triggering equipment. This means:

      If your customer (Japanese or Korean equipment maker, or a Taiwanese foundry) uses
      YOUR 3B001/3B002 equipment to produce chips or equipment, those foreign-made items
      may be subject to EAR when destined for:
        (i) Entity List parties (Entity List FDPR — all destinations)
        (ii) China or Macau for advanced computing applications above the specified
             performance thresholds (Advanced Computing FDPR)

      Practical implication: Your distributor agreements and equipment sale agreements
      with foreign manufacturers must include FDPR representations:
        - Buyer acknowledges that the equipment is subject to EAR
        - Buyer agrees not to use the equipment to produce items for Entity List parties
          or for China/Macau advanced computing applications, unless separately licensed
        - Buyer agrees to flow these restrictions down in their own supply agreements

  B.3 Russia/Belarus FDPR (March 2022)
      Since March 2022, virtually all items produced anywhere in the world using any
      US-origin technology, software, or equipment are subject to EAR for Russia and
      Belarus destinations. This is nearly categorical; the exceptions are narrow.
      You should have zero Russia/Belarus sales, and your distributor agreements must
      explicitly prohibit reexport to Russia and Belarus.

  B.4 FDPR Documentation
      For each product line, document in writing:
        - Does the product incorporate US-origin technology or software controlled for NS?
        - Was the product manufactured on US-origin equipment controlled under 3B001/3B002?
        - Two-prong FDPR analysis (technology/software prong + destination prong)
        - Conclusion: subject to EAR via FDPR or not?

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C. HEIGHTENED CHINA SCRUTINY
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Even though China is not listed as a current export destination for your company, the
China dimension is pervasive given your products, customers, and the current regulatory
environment.

  C.1 October 2022 and October 2023 Advanced Computing Rules
      BIS's October 7, 2022 interim final rule and October 17, 2023 final rule imposed:
        - New licence requirements for advanced chip exports to China/Macau
        - Expanded FDPR covering items produced with US wafer fab equipment above
          performance thresholds destined for China
        - New end-use restrictions under § 744.23 for supercomputer and semiconductor
          manufacturing end-uses in China
        - Restrictions on US persons supporting development/production of advanced
          ICs in China

      Your semiconductor equipment, particularly any 3B001.c-f class tools with
      capability above specified thresholds, may be subject to these rules even for
      sales to non-China customers, if those customers supply Chinese fabs.

  C.2 Taiwan-Based Customer Diversion Risk
      Taiwan is a major diversion risk point. TSMC, UMC, and other Taiwanese foundries
      have production for Chinese customers. While a sale to TSMC itself is generally
      compliant, equipment that ends up used for production above thresholds for Chinese
      advanced chip customers may trigger questions. Your due diligence for Taiwan
      customers should document the production activities of the fab and confirm
      the equipment will not be used for prohibited China-related applications.

  C.3 MEU List and Chinese Military-Civil Fusion
      China's Military-Civil Fusion strategy means that ostensibly civilian Chinese
      entities (universities, research institutes, ostensibly commercial companies)
      may have military connections. The MEU List and the NS-CMIC (Non-SDN Chinese
      Military-Industrial Complex) list are your primary tools here. Screen for these
      even for transactions that appear wholly commercial.

  C.4 No China Sales Policy
      Consider implementing a formal "No Direct Sales to China" policy, pending
      individual transaction review and approval by the ECO and legal counsel.
      This is increasingly standard practice for semiconductor equipment companies
      and provides a defensible compliance posture given current regulatory risk.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
D. DEEMED EXPORTS — SPECIFIC TO SEMICONDUCTOR EQUIPMENT ENGINEERING CULTURE
----------------------------------------------------------------------

A 200-person semiconductor equipment company typically has a significant portion of
its workforce in engineering roles, often including foreign nationals from China,
India, South Korea, and Taiwan. The deemed export implications are substantial.

  D.1 Pre-Hire Screening
      Before extending an offer to any foreign national for a role that will involve
      access to 3E001 (technology for 3B001 equipment) or 3E002 (technology for 3B002
      equipment), conduct a deemed export screening:
        - Identify which technology the employee will access
        - Classify that technology
        - Determine the employee's nationality (most restrictive rule for dual nationals)
        - Determine if a licence would be required to export that technology to the
          employee's home country

      For Chinese nationals accessing NS-controlled 3E001 technology: a deemed export
      licence is very likely required. Apply through SNAP-R before granting access.

  D.2 Access Controls
      Implement role-based access controls for all controlled technology:
        - Physical lab access: badge control, visitor logs
        - CAD and design files: permissions by role and nationality
        - Source code repositories (GitHub/GitLab/Bitbucket): access groups controlled
          by the ECO, not just IT or engineering management
        - Test data and process recipes: treat as controlled technology

  D.3 Foreign Visitors to Your Facilities
      Any foreign national who visits your facility and is shown controlled equipment
      or technology (even in a marketing or sales context) may trigger a deemed export.
      Implement a visitor access policy: screen visitors, log their access, and ensure
      controlled technology is not disclosed without appropriate authorisation.

PART THREE: PROGRAMME LAUNCH — RECOMMENDED SEQUENCING
=======================================================================

For a company building an ECP from scratch, implement in this order:

Phase 1 (Months 1-3) — Foundations:
  - Appoint ECO; draft and execute management commitment policy
  - Begin ECCN classification of the top 20 product lines (highest revenue/highest risk)
  - Implement restricted party screening tool (at minimum, CSL screening via
    trade.gov or a commercial tool like Visual Compliance or Descartes)
  - Draft and implement the restricted party screening procedure
  - Deliver Track A training to all employees

Phase 2 (Months 4-6) — Structure:
  - Complete ECCN classification of full product portfolio
  - Draft all written procedures (Elements 3.1-3.10)
  - Conduct deemed export assessment of engineering roles
  - Begin KYC review of existing customer and distributor base
  - Deliver Track B and Track C training
  - Implement ERP integration for automated screening

Phase 3 (Months 7-12) — Maturity:
  - FDPR analysis for all product lines and customer agreements
  - Update distributor agreements with EAR compliance clauses
  - Obtain CCATS determinations for key product lines
  - Conduct first internal ECP audit
  - Deliver Track D training to ECO/legal/finance
  - Engage third-party counsel for programme review
  - Commission SNAP-R accounts and practice licence application workflows
  - Implement VSD procedure; run tabletop exercise
