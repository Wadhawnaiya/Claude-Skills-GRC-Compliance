EXPORT COMPLIANCE PROGRAMME DESIGN FOR A 200-PERSON SEMICONDUCTOR EQUIPMENT COMPANY
Covering the Seven BIS Elements and Restricted Party Screening

=============================================================================

INTRODUCTION

Semiconductor equipment is among the most tightly controlled categories of dual-use technology
in U.S. export regulations. The Export Administration Regulations (EAR), administered by the
Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) within the U.S. Department of Commerce, impose
significant obligations on exporters of items classified under Export Control Classification
Numbers (ECCNs) in the 3B category (semiconductor manufacturing equipment). Your destinations —
Europe, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and India — range from very favorable (close treaty allies)
to requiring heightened diligence (India, depending on end-use and end-user). Building a
credible Export Compliance Programme (ECP) from scratch requires implementing the seven
elements BIS has identified as core to an effective compliance framework.

=============================================================================

PART I: THE SEVEN BIS ELEMENTS OF AN EFFECTIVE EXPORT COMPLIANCE PROGRAMME

BIS has articulated these elements through its enforcement guidelines, penalty mitigation
factors, and guidance documents (including the Export Compliance Guidelines published on the
BIS website). Companies that implement all seven elements in good faith receive meaningful
mitigation credit in enforcement proceedings and, more importantly, are far less likely to
commit violations in the first place.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 1: MANAGEMENT COMMITMENT
---------------------------------------------------------------------

What it means:
Senior leadership — the CEO, Board, and executive team — must visibly and actively champion
the compliance programme. This is not a delegated-down function; it requires top-down tone
and resources.

Implementation for your company:

a) Designate a senior Export Compliance Officer (ECO) or Export Compliance Manager with
   direct reporting access to the CEO or General Counsel. For a 200-person semiconductor
   equipment company, this should be a dedicated, experienced hire — not a collateral duty
   added to the Legal or Finance function.

b) Obtain and document Board/executive-level approval of the Export Compliance Policy
   Statement. The policy should be signed by the CEO and reviewed annually.

c) Allocate budget: screening tools, legal counsel, training platforms, and dedicated staff
   time are real costs. Management must fund them adequately.

d) Establish an Export Control Committee or steering group that includes representatives from
   Sales, Engineering, Legal, Finance, and Operations. This group should meet at least
   quarterly to review compliance matters, emerging regulatory changes (e.g., Entity List
   additions), and escalations.

e) Include export compliance objectives in executive performance evaluations and incorporate
   compliance metrics in management reporting.

f) Ensure that management is briefed on significant changes to the Entity List and on any
   transactions involving heightened risk (India end-uses, unusual routing, etc.).

Why it matters for semiconductor equipment:
BIS has significantly escalated enforcement in the semiconductor space, particularly regarding
China-adjacent supply chains, advanced node equipment, and multilateral controls under the
Wassenaar Arrangement. Management buy-in is essential for the cultural authority to say "no"
to a lucrative sale that raises compliance concerns.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 2: RISK ASSESSMENT
---------------------------------------------------------------------

What it means:
A systematic, documented process for identifying, evaluating, and prioritizing export control
risks across the company's products, markets, customers, and transactions.

Implementation for your company:

a) Product Classification Review:
   - Conduct a thorough ECCN classification review for all products in your portfolio.
     Semiconductor equipment will commonly fall under ECCN 3B001 or 3B002 (equipment for
     manufacturing semiconductor devices) or potentially 3B991/3B992 for less sensitive items.
   - Engage outside counsel or a technical expert to classify products if internal expertise
     is limited. Misclassification is a common source of violations.
   - Document the classification rationale in a Classification Register, including the
     technical parameters that drove the classification decision (e.g., overlay accuracy,
     minimum feature size, throughput thresholds).
   - Identify which products qualify as EAR99 (no ECCN, subject to EAR but generally low
     risk) versus controlled ECCNs.

b) Destination/Country Risk Mapping:
   - Map your destination countries against the EAR's Country Groups (A, B, D, E).
   - Europe and Japan: generally Country Group A, with favorable license exception
     availability (e.g., License Exception STA — Strategic Trade Authorization).
   - South Korea and Taiwan: broadly favorable, but verify transaction-by-transaction.
   - India: Country Group B but not A:5 (no validated end-user list at the same level
     as close allies). Certain items require a license for India that would be license-
     exception eligible for Japan. Also review Validated End-User (VEU) program
     opportunities for repeat Indian customers.
   - Assess whether any products are subject to foreign military end-use / end-user
     (FMEU) controls under EAR Part 744.

c) End-Use Risk Assessment:
   - Semiconductor equipment is of particular concern for:
     * Military end-use (EAR 744.21 — the military end-use / end-user rule)
     * WMD end-use (EAR 744.2-.6)
     * Proliferation activities
     * Advanced node chip fabrication that may be restricted under recent semiconductor
       rules (e.g., October 2022 and subsequent BIS rules targeting advanced computing
       and semiconductor manufacturing items — "ITAR-adjacent" EAR controls)
   - Develop an end-use risk scoring matrix that factors in: customer type (IDM, foundry,
     research institution, distributor, defense contractor), product application, and
     country of ultimate destination.

d) Channel Risk:
   - Assess risks from distributors, sales agents, and resellers who may export your
     equipment onward.
   - For your destination mix, this is particularly relevant in India (where equipment
     may be resold or transferred to third parties) and Taiwan (given proximity to China
     and potential for diversion).

e) Document and update your risk assessment at least annually and whenever there is a
   significant change in your product portfolio, customer base, or the regulatory
   environment.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 3: EXPORT COMPLIANCE POLICIES, PROCEDURES, AND GUIDELINES
---------------------------------------------------------------------

What it means:
Written, accessible, and operationally integrated policies that govern how the company
conducts export-controlled activities, makes classification decisions, screens parties,
processes license applications, and responds to red flags.

Implementation for your company:

a) Core Policy Documents to Draft:
   - Export Compliance Policy (top-level, CEO-signed)
   - Product Classification Procedure (including the ECCN determination workflow)
   - Order Review and Transaction Screening Procedure
   - Restricted Party Screening Procedure (see Part II below for detail)
   - License Determination and Application Procedure
   - Red Flag Identification and Resolution Procedure
   - Technology Control Plan (TCP) for controlled technology
   - Deemed Export Procedure (for foreign nationals working with controlled technology)
   - Recordkeeping Policy (EAR requires five-year retention of export records)
   - Incident Response and Voluntary Self-Disclosure Procedure

b) Make policies searchable and accessible — host on your intranet with version control.
   Procedures should be written at an operational level: who does what, when, using
   which tools, and how decisions are documented.

c) Integration into Business Processes:
   - Embed compliance checkpoints in the order-to-cash process (CRM to ERP).
   - Require compliance sign-off before contract execution for controlled items.
   - Link classification records to the ERP system's product master data.

d) Deemed Export Controls:
   - Semiconductor equipment technology is frequently subject to deemed export controls —
     sharing controlled technical data with a foreign national in the U.S. is treated as
     an export to that person's country of nationality.
   - Implement a deemed export procedure: maintain records of foreign national employees/
     contractors with access to EAR-controlled technical data, assess their nationalities
     against license requirements, and obtain licenses where required.
   - This is especially important given that your engineering team likely includes
     nationals of China, India, Taiwan, and other countries.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 4: TRAINING AND AWARENESS
---------------------------------------------------------------------

What it means:
A systematic programme to educate employees on their export control responsibilities,
proportionate to their role and the risk level they encounter.

Implementation for your company:

a) Role-Based Training Curriculum:
   - All employees: Annual general awareness training (what are export controls, why
     they matter, what is a red flag, how to escalate).
   - Sales and customer-facing staff: In-depth training on restricted party screening,
     end-use/end-user red flags, license requirements by destination, and embargo awareness.
   - Engineering and R&D: Training on deemed exports, technology control plans, and
     classification of technical data.
   - Shipping/logistics: Training on export documentation (EEI filings, AES), destination
     controls statements, and physical security of shipments.
   - Management and ECO: Advanced training on penalty exposure, recent enforcement actions,
     and emerging regulatory changes.

b) Training Delivery:
   - Use a learning management system (LMS) to track completion and issue certificates.
   - Supplement annual training with targeted briefings after significant regulatory changes
     (BIS issues rules frequently in the semiconductor space).
   - Include export compliance scenarios in onboarding for new hires in Sales, Engineering,
     and Operations.

c) Documentation:
   - Retain training completion records. BIS views documented training as a significant
     mitigating factor in enforcement proceedings.

d) Awareness Culture:
   - Circulate brief "compliance alerts" when significant regulatory changes occur (e.g.,
     new Entity List additions of semiconductor customers, new EAR rules on advanced
     computing).
   - Maintain a confidential reporting channel for employees to raise compliance concerns
     without fear of retaliation.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 5: SCREENING, AUDITING, AND MONITORING
---------------------------------------------------------------------

What it means:
Systematic and ongoing processes to screen transactions and parties, and to audit the
compliance programme for effectiveness and gaps.

This element has two distinct components: (a) ongoing transaction/party screening, and
(b) periodic compliance auditing. Restricted party screening is covered in depth in
Part II below.

Auditing:

a) Internal Audits:
   - Conduct a comprehensive internal export compliance audit at least annually. The audit
     should cover: classification accuracy, screening process adherence, license condition
     compliance, recordkeeping, training completion, and deemed export compliance.
   - The audit should be conducted by someone independent of the day-to-day compliance
     function — either an internal audit team member trained in EAR, or outside counsel.

b) Audit Scope for Semiconductor Equipment Exporters:
   - Sample shipment records and verify EEI filings in AES (Automated Export System).
   - Verify that ECCN classifications are current and reflect current product specifications
     (engineering changes can affect ECCN).
   - Test that restricted party screening was performed and documented for audited
     shipments.
   - Review license conditions for any licenses held and verify compliance.
   - Check that technology transfers (technical data, software) to foreign nationals
     and overseas subsidiaries/customers were properly screened and authorized.

c) Monitoring:
   - Monitor regulatory changes continuously: subscribe to BIS email alerts, Federal
     Register notices, and law firm client alerts.
   - Monitor the Entity List, SDN List, and other restricted party lists for additions
     relevant to your customer/supplier base.
   - Track BIS enforcement actions for industry-specific lessons learned.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 6: HANDLING AND REPORTING EXPORT CONTROL PROBLEMS
---------------------------------------------------------------------

What it means:
Clear processes for identifying, escalating, investigating, and (where appropriate)
voluntarily disclosing export control violations or potential violations.

Implementation for your company:

a) Red Flag Escalation:
   - Define what constitutes a "red flag" (unusual payment terms, requests to remove
     technical markings, vague end-use descriptions, routing through unusual third
     countries, customer reluctance to provide end-use certificates).
   - Require employees to escalate red flags to the ECO before proceeding with a
     transaction. Establish a formal hold process.

b) Investigation Protocol:
   - When a potential violation is identified, the ECO (with Legal) should conduct a
     prompt preliminary investigation.
   - Preserve all relevant records immediately.
   - Assess whether a Voluntary Self-Disclosure (VSD) to BIS's Office of Export
     Enforcement (OEE) is appropriate.

c) Voluntary Self-Disclosure:
   - BIS strongly encourages VSDs and provides significant mitigation credit (typically
     50% reduction in civil penalty) for timely, complete, and good-faith disclosures.
   - VSDs should be submitted to OEE with a narrative of facts, root cause analysis,
     and corrective action plan.
   - Engage experienced export control counsel before submitting a VSD.

d) Corrective Action:
   - Every violation or near-miss should result in a documented root cause analysis and
     corrective action plan.
   - Update policies, training, and screening procedures as needed.

e) No Retaliation Policy:
   - Employees who report compliance concerns in good faith must be protected from
     retaliation. Make this explicit in policy and in leadership communications.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
ELEMENT 7: RECORDKEEPING
---------------------------------------------------------------------

What it means:
Systematic maintenance of all export-related records in compliance with EAR requirements,
and in a manner that supports auditing and investigation.

Implementation for your company:

a) Regulatory Requirements:
   - EAR Part 762 requires exporters to maintain records for five years from the date
     of export, reexport, or other regulated transaction.
   - Required records include: export licenses, AES filings, purchase orders, invoices,
     packing lists, shipping documents, end-use certificates, restricted party screening
     records, and export classification determinations.

b) Recordkeeping System:
   - Implement a centralized document management system for export records.
   - Ensure records are indexed and retrievable by transaction, customer, and date.
   - Link records to the ERP system where possible.

c) Technology Control / Data Security:
   - Maintain access controls for technical data stored in engineering systems (PLM, CAD,
     file servers) to prevent unauthorized access by foreign nationals or transfer to
     unauthorized destinations.
   - Log and retain records of technical data access for deemed export compliance.

d) Retention Schedule:
   - Set an automated retention schedule of at least five years, with legal hold
     capability for records under investigation.

PART II: RESTRICTED PARTY SCREENING PROCESS

Restricted party screening (RPS) is arguably the most operationally critical compliance
control for an active exporter. Transacting with a party on a restricted list is a per se
violation of U.S. law and may result in criminal prosecution, civil penalties, and
debarment — regardless of whether you knew the party was listed.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
WHICH LISTS MUST YOU SCREEN AGAINST?
---------------------------------------------------------------------

For a U.S.-based semiconductor equipment exporter, screening must cover at minimum:

1. OFAC Lists (administered by Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control):
   - Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons List (SDN List) — the most
     critical. Transacting with an SDN is generally prohibited regardless of what
     is being sold.
   - Non-SDN Sectoral Sanctions Identifications List (SSI List)
   - Consolidated Sanctions List (covers multiple OFAC programs)
   - Foreign Sanctions Evaders List
   - Sectoral sanctions lists (e.g., for Russia, Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria)

2. BIS Lists (administered by Department of Commerce):
   - Entity List (EAR Part 744, Supplement 4) — parties for whom a license is required
     for all items subject to the EAR. This list has grown dramatically in recent years
     with semiconductor-related additions, particularly Chinese entities.
   - Denied Persons List — parties denied export privileges; no U.S. person may
     participate in any export transaction involving them.
   - Unverified List — parties for whom BIS could not verify end-use; heightened due
     diligence required; license exception unavailability.

3. DDTC List (administered by State's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls):
   - Debarred Parties List (ITAR) — while your products may be EAR-controlled rather
     than ITAR, debarred parties are generally high-risk and warrant screening.

4. Additional Government Lists:
   - DHS CAATS / Consolidated Terrorist List
   - FBI Most Wanted
   - Non-SDN Palestinian Legislative Council List
   - Various country-specific sanctions lists

Best practice: Use a commercial restricted party screening (RPS) tool (e.g., Descartes
Visual Compliance, Amber Road/E2open, Thomson Reuters World-Check, Dow Jones Risk &
Compliance, Sayari, or similar) that aggregates all relevant lists into a single search
interface and auto-updates as lists change. Manual screening against individual government
websites is error-prone and not sustainable at transaction volume.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
WHEN TO SCREEN
---------------------------------------------------------------------

Screen at each of the following trigger points:

1. Pre-Opportunity / Quote Stage: Screen the prospective customer (legal entity name,
   doing business as names, and any named intermediaries) before committing engineering
   or commercial resources to a proposal.

2. Order Receipt: Screen at order entry into the ERP or order management system.
   This is the primary transaction-level control.

3. Pre-Shipment: Conduct a final screen immediately before releasing a shipment,
   particularly if there has been a gap of more than 30 days since the last screen.
   Entity List and SDN additions occur on a rolling basis.

4. Contract Execution: Screen before signing distribution agreements, service contracts,
   or technology license agreements.

5. After Significant List Updates: When BIS makes a major Entity List addition (e.g.,
   adding a major chip manufacturer or fab), proactively screen your existing customer
   and pipeline against the update.

6. New Employees / Contractors (Foreign Nationals): Screen under deemed export
   obligations — assess nationality of foreign national employees before granting
   access to controlled technology.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
WHOM TO SCREEN
---------------------------------------------------------------------

Screen all relevant parties in a transaction, not just the direct customer:

- The ordering customer (bill-to party)
- The ship-to / end-use party (may differ from the ordering customer)
- Distributors, sales agents, and resellers acting on your behalf
- Freight forwarders and customs brokers (for completeness)
- Named end-users in end-use certificates
- Ultimate consignees
- Intermediate consignees
- Any third parties introduced mid-transaction

For your destination mix:
- Taiwan: Pay particular attention to whether the customer or end-user has known ties to
  Chinese entities or Chinese-funded fabs, given the EAR's focus on preventing
  semiconductor technology from reaching restricted Chinese end-users.
- India: Screen not just the customer but the stated end-use facility and any Indian
  government or defense-affiliated entities involved.
- South Korea and Japan: Lower routine risk, but screen systematically regardless —
  lists include parties in all countries.
- Europe: Include EU and UK sanctions lists (post-Brexit, UK has its own sanctions
  regime) in addition to OFAC/BIS lists for customers in the UK.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
FUZZY MATCHING AND ALIAS MATCHING
---------------------------------------------------------------------

Restricted party screening is complicated by transliteration, name variations, and
aliases. Your screening tool must:

- Apply fuzzy matching logic (typically 80-85% match threshold is a reasonable starting
  point, adjustable based on your risk tolerance).
- Screen aliases, also-known-as (AKA), formerly-known-as (FKA), and doing-business-as
  (DBA) names.
- Screen addresses and other identifying information where available.
- Flag partial matches for human review rather than auto-clearing them.

Establish a documented match review process:
- Screening tool generates a "hit" or "potential match."
- A trained reviewer conducts manual due diligence: compare address, country, industry,
  and other identifiers against the list entry.
- Document the determination: confirmed match (transaction blocked) or false positive
  (transaction cleared, with written rationale).
- Escalate any confirmed match to the ECO and Legal.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
DOCUMENTATION
---------------------------------------------------------------------

For every screening action, retain:
- Date and time of screen
- Names and entities screened
- Lists screened against (or confirmation that the tool covers all required lists)
- Result (clear, hit, or potential match)
- For potential matches: the reviewer's analysis and determination
- Authorizing signature for cleared transactions involving any potential match

This documentation is essential for audit defense and forms part of your five-year
EAR recordkeeping obligation.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
CUSTOMER DUE DILIGENCE BEYOND LIST SCREENING
---------------------------------------------------------------------

Restricted party screening is necessary but not sufficient. BIS's Red Flag guidance
(EAR Supplement 1 to Part 732, "Know Your Customer" guidance) requires that you also
conduct broader due diligence when red flags are present.

For new customers in your markets, consider also:
- Verifying the customer's business existence and legitimacy (website, trade references,
  business registration).
- Requiring end-use certificates or statements for controlled items, particularly for
  India and any new customer in any market.
- Conducting enhanced due diligence for distributors who will resell your equipment —
  review their customer list if possible, obtain flow-down compliance representations
  in the distribution agreement.
- Implementing a Know Your Customer (KYC) questionnaire for new distributors and
  high-value customers.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
AUTOMATED INTEGRATION
---------------------------------------------------------------------

For a 200-person company with active transaction volume, integrate your RPS tool with
your ERP or CRM system to:
- Automatically trigger a screen when a new customer is added or a new order is entered.
- Block order release until screening is completed and documented.
- Generate screening certificates that attach to the shipment record.

This automation reduces the risk of human omission and creates an auditable trail.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
INDIA-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS FOR YOUR SCREENING PROGRAMME
---------------------------------------------------------------------

India presents additional considerations:

- Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and entities affiliated with it have at
  various times appeared on restricted lists. Verify current status at each transaction.
- India's defense-civil integration means some ostensibly commercial customers may
  have defense connections requiring license review under EAR 744.21.
- For certain controlled semiconductor equipment, India may require a BIS license even
  where a license exception would apply to Japan or South Korea. Verify ECCN-specific
  India license requirements.
- Pursue Validated End-User (VEU) authorization for trusted, repeat Indian customers
  to streamline ongoing transactions.

PART III: PROGRAMME IMPLEMENTATION ROADMAP (SUGGESTED SEQUENCING)

Given you are building from scratch, a phased approach is recommended:

PHASE 1 (Months 1-3): Foundation
- Hire/designate Export Compliance Officer
- Engage outside export control counsel for initial assessment
- Conduct ECCN classification review of product portfolio
- Implement commercial RPS screening tool immediately
- Draft and execute Export Compliance Policy (CEO-signed)
- Establish screening procedure and begin documenting all screens

PHASE 2 (Months 3-6): Core Programme Build
- Draft all core policy documents (classification, order review, deemed export, etc.)
- Implement role-based training programme; complete all-employee training
- Conduct comprehensive risk assessment; document in writing
- Integrate RPS tool with ERP
- Establish Export Control Committee
- Review and classify deemed export risk for foreign national employees

PHASE 3 (Months 6-12): Full Programme Operationalization
- Complete integration of compliance checkpoints into order-to-cash workflow
- Conduct first internal compliance audit
- Establish recordkeeping system and retention schedule
- Develop incident response and VSD procedure
- Submit VEU applications for qualifying Indian customers (if applicable)
- Benchmark programme against BIS guidance and peer-industry practices

ONGOING:
- Annual risk assessment update
- Annual training refresh
- Annual internal audit
- Continuous regulatory monitoring
- Quarterly Export Control Committee meetings
- Pre-shipment screening on every controlled transaction

PART IV: KEY REGULATORY REFERENCES

- Export Administration Regulations (EAR): 15 C.F.R. Parts 730-774
- BIS Entity List: EAR Part 744, Supplement No. 4
- BIS Denied Persons List: EAR Part 764, Supplement No. 2
- BIS Unverified List: EAR Part 744, Supplement No. 6
- Red Flag guidance: EAR Supplement 1 to Part 732
- OFAC SDN List: 31 C.F.R. various parts
- EAR Recordkeeping: 15 C.F.R. Part 762
- BIS Penalty Guidelines: 15 C.F.R. Part 766, Supplement No. 1
- Commerce Control List (CCL): EAR Part 774 (ECCN 3B001, 3B002 for semiconductor equipment)

Note: The semiconductor equipment sector is subject to particularly dynamic regulatory
change. As of 2024-2026, BIS has issued multiple significant rules restricting exports
of advanced semiconductor manufacturing equipment, particularly to China, including
restrictions on items that assist in producing advanced logic chips below certain node
sizes. Monitor BIS Federal Register notices closely and engage outside counsel to assess
the impact of each new rule on your product portfolio and customer relationships.
