EXPORT COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS: AES-256 CYBERSECURITY SOFTWARE
Destinations: France, India, Brazil
Framework: U.S. Export Administration Regulations (EAR), 15 C.F.R. Parts 730-774
Date of Analysis: 2026-05-07

===========================================================================
OVERVIEW
===========================================================================

Yes, your cybersecurity software incorporating AES-256 encryption is subject
to U.S. export controls under the Export Administration Regulations (EAR),
administered by the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) within the
Department of Commerce. However, for commercial sales to France, India, and
Brazil, there is a strong likelihood that you can use a licence exception
rather than obtaining an individual export licence, provided you meet the
applicable conditions.

===========================================================================
STEP 1: CLASSIFICATION UNDER THE COMMERCE CONTROL LIST (CCL)
===========================================================================

Your product almost certainly falls under ECCN 5D002 on the Commerce Control
List (CCL).

ECCN 5D002 covers:
  "Software specially designed or modified for the 'development',
   'production' or 'use' of equipment or 'software' controlled in
   5A002 or 5B002, or software designed to certify software controlled
   in 5D002."

5A002 controls, among other things, systems and equipment that use
cryptography with a symmetric key length exceeding 56 bits -- AES-256
(256-bit symmetric key) clearly exceeds this threshold.

Reasons your product is likely 5D002:
  - AES-256 is a symmetric encryption algorithm with a 256-bit key length
    (well above the 56-bit threshold)
  - The software is cybersecurity software -- it uses encryption as a
    core functional capability, not merely incidentally
  - It is designed for commercial use (not exclusively mass-market in
    the EAR sense at the classification stage)

Could it be classified as EAR99?
  Unlikely for cybersecurity software with strong encryption as a primary
  feature. EAR99 classification requires that no ECCN on the CCL applies.
  Given the cryptographic capabilities, 5D002 is almost certainly the
  correct classification.

Could it be 5D992 instead of 5D002?
  5D992 covers software with encryption features that have been "decontrolled"
  or are for mass-market use. However, the initial classification analysis
  starts at 5D002 for strong encryption. The mass-market decontrol path
  (discussed below) is a licence exception mechanism, not a reclassification
  for most purposes.

===========================================================================
STEP 2: REASONS FOR CONTROL (WHY IS IT CONTROLLED?)
===========================================================================

Under ECCN 5D002, the reasons for control include:
  - AT:  Anti-Terrorism
  - EI:  Encryption Items (the primary reason for encryption controls)
  - NS:  National Security (in some cases)

The EI (Encryption Items) controls are the central concern here. They are
administered specifically through the ENC licence exception framework and
the annual self-classification reporting regime.

===========================================================================
STEP 3: DO YOU NEED A BIS LICENCE?
===========================================================================

The general answer: You need either a licence or a valid licence exception
to export 5D002 software. A licence is not automatically required -- BIS
has established several licence exceptions for encryption items precisely
to facilitate legitimate commercial trade.

For most commercial cybersecurity software exports to France, India, and
Brazil, the ENC licence exception (15 C.F.R. Section 740.17) is the
primary pathway to avoid needing an individual licence.

===========================================================================
STEP 4: THE ENC LICENCE EXCEPTION (15 C.F.R. § 740.17)
===========================================================================

The ENC licence exception authorises the export, re-export, and transfer
of encryption items that would otherwise require a licence under ECCN 5D002
(and related ECCNs such as 5E002). It has two main tiers:

-------------------------------------------
TIER A: ENC -- Section 740.17(b)(1)
"Encryption software...after a one-time review"
Actually called: ENC without review -- Mass Market
-------------------------------------------

This sub-paragraph covers encryption software that qualifies as
"mass market" under the Note to ECCN 5D002. To qualify:

Requirements:
  1. The software must be generally available to the public by being
     sold from stock at retail selling points (or designed for installation
     by the user without further substantial support from the supplier)
  2. The cryptographic functionality cannot be easily changed by the user
  3. It is designed for installation without further substantial supplier
     support

If your software meets the mass-market criteria, you may export it
under ENC to most destinations (including France, India, and Brazil)
WITHOUT a prior BIS review, subject to:

  - Annual self-classification reporting to BIS and NSA (required)
  - Filing an Electronic Export Information (EEI) in AES where required
  - You cannot export to embargoed countries or denied parties

Mass market determination for cybersecurity software:
  This can be fact-specific. Cybersecurity tools sold as off-the-shelf
  products with standard configurations may qualify. Highly customised
  enterprise tools or tools sold with significant professional services
  may not. Legal counsel should evaluate this carefully.

-------------------------------------------
TIER B: ENC -- Section 740.17(b)(2) and (b)(3)
"After a one-time review" (most commercial encryption software)
-------------------------------------------

If your software does not qualify as mass market, the standard ENC
exception under 740.17(b)(2) is available after a one-time BIS/NSA
review. Under this pathway:

  1. Submit a one-time product review request to BIS (via the online
     Simplified Network Application Process Redesign -- SNAP-R system)
  2. BIS and NSA review the submission (typically within 30 days)
  3. Once reviewed (or after 30 days without action), you may export
     the product under the ENC exception to eligible destinations

Eligible destinations for 740.17(b)(2):
  The ENC exception under (b)(2) is available for exports to most
  countries, including France, India, and Brazil, provided the customer
  is not:
    - A government end-user in a country outside the "ENC-favorable"
      category (discussed below), OR
    - A prohibited end-user (denied party, arms embargo, etc.)

-------------------------------------------
TIER C: ENC -- Section 740.17(b)(3)
"Unrestricted" encryption -- no review, no reporting (very limited)
-------------------------------------------

Applies only to encryption software with very short key lengths or
open-source software. Not applicable to AES-256.

===========================================================================
STEP 5: COUNTRY-BY-COUNTRY ANALYSIS
===========================================================================

FRANCE
------
Country Group: B (not D:1, not embargoed, not E:1 or E:2)
Relationship: EU member, NATO ally, strong partner
AT controls apply: No (France is not on the AT list)
Government end-user concern: France is a favorable destination; government
  end-users in France can generally receive encryption items under ENC.

Result: France is among the most permissive destinations for encryption
exports. Both ENC (b)(1) (mass market) and (b)(2) (after review) are
available for commercial and government customers. No individual BIS
licence required in the normal commercial case.

INDIA
-----
Country Group: B (not D:1 as of general EAR structure, though watch for
  specific controls)
Note: India has had multilateral export control sensitivities in the past
  but is generally in Country Group B for EAR purposes, not subject to
  the most restrictive encryption controls.
AT controls apply: No (India is not on the AT list)
Government end-user concern: For exports to non-ENC-favorable government
  end-users, an individual licence may be required. India's government
  end-users may require case-by-case review for some controlled items,
  but for standard commercial cybersecurity software sold to private-sector
  customers, ENC (b)(2) after one-time review should be available.

Result: For commercial/private-sector customers in India, the ENC
exception (b)(2) after one-time BIS review is most likely available.
If selling to Indian government entities, consult legal counsel as
individual licence review may be warranted, particularly for products
with military or intelligence applications.

Also note: India has its own encryption import regulations (enforced
by the Department of Telecommunications). You should verify local
Indian import compliance in addition to U.S. export requirements.

BRAZIL
------
Country Group: B (not embargoed, not AT-designated)
AT controls apply: No
Government end-user concern: Similar to India -- commercial sales to
  private-sector customers in Brazil should qualify under ENC (b)(2).
  Government end-users may require separate analysis.

Result: For commercial cybersecurity software sold to private Brazilian
customers, ENC (b)(2) after one-time BIS review should be available.
Brazil also has its own cryptographic software regulations (administered
by the Brazilian Army's Regulatory Division for Cryptographic Products --
DSIC/GSI); local import compliance should be separately verified.

===========================================================================
STEP 6: ANNUAL SELF-CLASSIFICATION REPORTING REQUIREMENT
===========================================================================

Regardless of which ENC tier applies, exporters using the ENC licence
exception are generally required to submit annual self-classification
reports to BIS and NSA by February 1 each year, covering products
exported in the prior calendar year under ENC. This is a compliance
obligation, not a licence application -- but failure to file can
expose the company to enforcement risk.

The reports are submitted via the BIS Encryption Registration system
and include:
  - Product name and model number
  - ECCN
  - Key length(s) and algorithm(s)
  - Whether the product is mass market or reviewed

===========================================================================
STEP 7: DENIED PARTY SCREENING (MANDATORY FOR ALL EXPORTS)
===========================================================================

Regardless of licence exception, you must screen all customers, end-users,
and intermediaries against U.S. Government restricted-party lists before
each export:

  - BIS Entity List (15 C.F.R. Part 744, Supplement 4)
  - BIS Denied Persons List
  - BIS Unverified List
  - Treasury OFAC Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List
  - State Department Debarred List (ITAR)
  - Consolidated Screening List (consolidates multiple lists)

A match on any of these lists may prohibit the export entirely, regardless
of the licence exception.

===========================================================================
STEP 8: END-USE AND END-USER CONSIDERATIONS
===========================================================================

Even when a licence exception applies, EAR Part 744 prohibits certain
exports based on prohibited end-uses or end-users, regardless of the
product's ECCN. For cybersecurity software specifically:

  - Do not export to entities known to support weapons of mass destruction
    programs
  - Do not export to entities that will use the software for offensive
    cyber operations against U.S. interests
  - Conduct basic due diligence on the customer's intended use

Red flags that should trigger deeper review:
  - Customer is a military or intelligence organisation in a non-partner country
  - Customer's stated use is inconsistent with the product's capabilities
  - Unusual payment or shipping routing
  - Customer is unwilling to disclose end-use

===========================================================================
STEP 9: PRACTICAL COMPLIANCE STEPS SUMMARY
===========================================================================

1. CLASSIFY: Confirm ECCN 5D002 (or engage a licensed export counsel to
   confirm classification).

2. DETERMINE ENC TIER:
   a. Is your product mass market? If yes, ENC (b)(1) may apply -- no
      prior BIS review needed.
   b. If not mass market, prepare and submit a one-time product review
      to BIS/NSA via SNAP-R under ENC (b)(2). Wait for clearance (or
      30 days) before first export.

3. SCREEN CUSTOMERS: Run all customers through denied-party lists before
   each transaction.

4. ASSESS END-USERS: Confirm customers in France, India, and Brazil are
   private-sector commercial users. Government end-users require separate
   analysis.

5. FILE EEI: Determine whether Electronic Export Information filing in
   the Automated Export System (AES) is required (generally required for
   exports valued over $2,500 or controlled commodities, though there are
   exceptions for software downloads).

6. ANNUAL REPORTING: File annual self-classification report with BIS/NSA
   by February 1 covering prior-year ENC exports.

7. LOCAL LAW: Separately verify encryption import requirements in France
   (ANSSI -- generally permissive for AES-256 in commercial products),
   India (DoT regulations), and Brazil (Army cryptographic regulations).

8. DOCUMENT EVERYTHING: Maintain records of classification decisions,
   ENC exception use, customer screening, and end-use representations
   for at least 5 years (EAR recordkeeping requirement).

===========================================================================
CONCLUSION
===========================================================================

For commercial cybersecurity software incorporating AES-256 encryption:

  - A BIS individual export licence is NOT required for France, India,
    and Brazil in most commercial scenarios.

  - The ENC licence exception (15 C.F.R. § 740.17) is the applicable
    pathway:
      * Mass-market products: ENC (b)(1) -- no prior review required
      * Non-mass-market commercial products: ENC (b)(2) -- one-time
        BIS/NSA product review required before first export

  - France is the most straightforward of the three destinations; India
    and Brazil require attention to government end-user restrictions and
    local import regulations.

  - Annual self-classification reporting to BIS and NSA is mandatory
    regardless of which ENC tier applies.

  - Denied-party screening is mandatory for every transaction.
