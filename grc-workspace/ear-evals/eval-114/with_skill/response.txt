EAR COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS: COMMERCIAL EXPORT OF CYBERSECURITY SOFTWARE WITH AES-256 ENCRYPTION
Destinations: France, India, Brazil
Prepared under: 15 CFR Parts 730–774 (Export Administration Regulations)
Administered by: Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS), U.S. Department of Commerce

================================================================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

Your cybersecurity software incorporating AES-256 encryption is almost certainly
classified as ECCN 5D002 under the Commerce Control List (CCL). A BIS licence is
not required for commercial export to France, India, or Brazil provided you qualify
for the ENC licence exception under § 740.17. However, a one-time BIS notification
(filing via SNAP-R) is a mandatory precondition before first use of the exception.
No ongoing annual licence is needed, but recordkeeping and restricted-party
screening obligations apply to every shipment.

Below is a step-by-step analysis.

================================================================================
STEP 1 — JURISDICTION DETERMINATION (ORDER OF REVIEW)
================================================================================

Before classifying under the EAR, apply the mandatory Order of Review.

1. ITAR / USML (22 CFR Part 121): Commercial encryption software for cybersecurity
   does not appear on the United States Munitions List. It is not an ITAR-controlled
   defence article. Jurisdiction is NOT with DDTC.

2. Other agencies: Encryption software of this type falls outside NRC, FDA, DEA,
   and ATF controls.

3. Subject to EAR (§ 734.3): US-origin software is subject to the EAR regardless
   of where it is located. Your product is subject to EAR.

4. Proceed to CCL classification.

================================================================================
STEP 2 — ECCN CLASSIFICATION: 5D002
================================================================================

Category 5, Part 2 — Information Security.

Your cybersecurity software falls under:

  ECCN 5D002 — "Software" specially designed or modified for the "development",
  "production" or "use" of equipment or "software" controlled by 5A002 or 5B002,
  or software providing capabilities of equipment controlled by 5A002 or 5B002.

AES-256 is a symmetric block cipher with a key length of 256 bits, well above the
56-bit threshold that triggers encryption controls. Commercial cybersecurity
software implementing AES-256 as a primary or integral feature is a paradigmatic
5D002 product.

Reasons for Control (RFCs) for ECCN 5D002:
  - EI  — Encryption Items (primary reason for control)
  - NS  — National Security (Column 2)
  - AT  — Anti-Terrorism (Column 1)

Note: If your software also incorporates features beyond encryption — e.g.,
intrusion detection, firewall components, or vulnerability assessment tools — those
features should be evaluated separately. However, the AES-256 encryption component
alone is sufficient to produce a 5D002 classification.

If you have not already obtained an official ECCN determination, consider submitting
a Commodity Classification Automated Tracking System (CCATS) request via SNAP-R
(snap-r.bis.doc.gov) using Form BIS-748P. This creates a defensible, BIS-endorsed
classification record.

================================================================================
STEP 3 — LICENCE REQUIREMENT ANALYSIS (COUNTRY CHART)
================================================================================

The Commerce Country Chart (Part 738, Supplement No. 1) determines whether a
licence is required by cross-referencing the ECCN's RFCs against each destination.

--- France ---

Country Group membership: A:1 (Wassenaar Arrangement), A:2 (Australia Group),
A:3 (MTCR), A:4 (NSG), A:5 (allied/partner), B. NOT in any D or E group.

Country Chart result for 5D002:
  - NS Column 2: No "X" for France → no licence required on NS grounds
  - EI: France is not on the EI column with an "X" for standard commercial encryption
  - AT Column 1: No "X" for France (Country Group B, not AT-controlled destination)

Preliminary conclusion: France does not generate a licence requirement on the
Country Chart for 5D002 commercial encryption software. However, the ENC exception
framework still applies because 5D002 carries EI controls, and BIS notification
under § 740.17 is the mechanism that authorises export of EI-controlled items.

--- India ---

Country Group membership: B. India is NOT in Country Group A:5 and is not a
member of all four multilateral export control regimes. India is also NOT in D:1,
D:2, D:3, D:4, D:5, E:1, or E:2.

Country Chart result for 5D002:
  - NS Column 2: Check — India does have an "X" for NS2 controls in some CCL entries,
    but for 5D002 (EI-controlled), the ENC exception framework governs.
  - AT Column 1: No "X" for India.
  - EI: India is not a prohibited destination for ENC (not E:1 or E:2).

Preliminary conclusion: India is an eligible ENC destination. The ENC exception
under § 740.17 is available, subject to the conditions described below.

--- Brazil ---

Country Group membership: B. Brazil is not in Country Group A:5 and is not in any
D or E group.

Country Chart result for 5D002:
  - Same analysis as India applies. Brazil is an eligible ENC destination.
  - Brazil is not on the E:1 or E:2 list.

Preliminary conclusion: Brazil is an eligible ENC destination.

================================================================================
STEP 4 — ENC LICENCE EXCEPTION (§ 740.17) — DETAILED ANALYSIS
================================================================================

The ENC licence exception at § 740.17 is the primary — and almost certainly the
correct — authorisation for your commercial exports of AES-256 cybersecurity
software to all three destinations.

--- ENC EXCEPTION STRUCTURE: THREE TIERS ---

TIER 1 — § 740.17(a): Mass-Market Encryption
  Applies to: Encryption commodities and software that qualify as "mass market"
  under the criteria in § 740.17(b)(1) and Note 3 to Category 5, Part 2 of the CCL.

  Mass-market criteria (all must be met):
    1. The software is generally available to the public by being sold from stock
       at retail selling points (physical or online) without restriction.
    2. The encryption functionality cannot be easily changed by the user.
    3. Designed for installation by the user without substantial support from the
       supplier.
    4. AES-256 qualifies as a standard, widely deployed algorithm — not proprietary
       or non-standard encryption.

  If your cybersecurity software is sold commercially off-the-shelf or via
  standard download/subscription with no customised key management or bespoke
  encryption architecture, it almost certainly qualifies as mass market.

TIER 2 — § 740.17(b)(1): Source Code for Mass-Market Products
  If you are exporting the source code of a mass-market encryption product (e.g.,
  to an open-source community, a distribution partner, or for integration), this
  tier applies. One-time BIS notification is still required.

TIER 3 — § 740.17(b)(3): Non-Standard / Proprietary Encryption
  If your encryption implementation is proprietary, non-standard, or cannot meet
  the mass-market criteria above, Tier 3 applies. This requires an annual
  self-classification report rather than a one-time notification.

  ASSESSMENT: For standard AES-256 in commercially sold cybersecurity software,
  Tier 1 (§ 740.17(a)) is the most likely applicable tier. Confirm with your
  product manager that the mass-market criteria above are all satisfied.

--- THE ONE-TIME BIS NOTIFICATION REQUIREMENT ---

CRITICAL REQUIREMENT: You CANNOT use the ENC exception until you have filed the
required notification with BIS. This is not optional.

How to file:
  1. Submit Form BIS-748P-A ("Supplement to Form BIS-748P") via SNAP-R at
     snap-r.bis.doc.gov.
  2. The notification covers the product (or product line) and its encryption
     specifications (algorithm, key length, protocol).
  3. BIS does not "approve" the notification — it is self-effectuating after filing.
     You may begin exporting under ENC once the notification is submitted.
  4. One notification covers all eligible destinations; you do not need separate
     filings per country.

What the notification must include:
  - Product name and description
  - ECCN (5D002)
  - Encryption algorithms and key lengths (AES-256, and any others such as RSA,
    SHA-2, TLS version)
  - Intended markets and customer types
  - Statement that the product meets mass-market criteria (for Tier 1)

After filing: Retain a copy of the SNAP-R submission confirmation in your
compliance records. This filing must be renewed if you materially change the
encryption features of the product.

--- ANNUAL SELF-CLASSIFICATION REPORT (§ 742.15(b)) ---

All exporters relying on ENC (§ 740.17) must submit an annual self-classification
report to BIS and NSA by February 1 of each year. This report covers all products
exported under ENC during the prior calendar year. This is separate from, and in
addition to, the one-time notification.

The annual report is submitted via SNAP-R and must include:
  - Product name and version
  - ECCN
  - Encryption details
  - Approximate number of units/copies exported

STEP 5 — COUNTRY GROUP ANALYSIS FOR FRANCE, INDIA, AND BRAZIL
================================================================================

FRANCE:
  Country Group: A:1, A:2, A:3, A:4, A:5, B
  ENC eligibility: YES — France is a highly favoured destination. As an A:5 country
  and EU member, France poses the lowest ENC compliance burden. The ENC exception
  under § 740.17(a) is fully available.
  Any restrictions: None specific to France for commercial encryption software.
  Reexport: French customers may further reexport under ENC to most Country Group B
  destinations without returning to you for authorisation, subject to their own
  ENC compliance under the EAR (as foreign re-exporters, they are subject to EAR
  reexport controls if the software was of US origin).

INDIA:
  Country Group: B
  ENC eligibility: YES — India is a Country Group B destination. The ENC exception
  is available under § 740.17(a).
  Additional note: India is not a member of the Wassenaar Arrangement (A:1) or
  Country Group A:5, meaning some other licence exceptions (e.g., TSR, GBS) have
  narrower availability for India. However, for ENC specifically, Country Group B
  eligibility is sufficient.
  India-specific considerations:
    - India has its own domestic encryption regulations (IT Act, 2000 and related
      rules). While this does not affect your US EAR obligations, advise Indian
      customers that domestic Indian import/use regulations may apply independently.
    - Screen all Indian customers against the Entity List, Unverified List, and
      Military End-User List before each shipment (see Step 6 below).

BRAZIL:
  Country Group: B
  ENC eligibility: YES — Brazil is a Country Group B destination. The ENC exception
  is available under § 740.17(a).
  Brazil-specific considerations:
    - Brazil is not subject to any US embargo or special controls.
    - Brazil has its own data localisation and cybersecurity regulations (LGPD,
      ANPD rules) which are separate from EAR obligations but relevant to your
      customers' compliance.
    - Screen all Brazilian customers against all restricted party lists.

STEP 6 — END-USER AND END-USE CONTROLS (PART 744)
================================================================================

Even with ENC exception eligibility confirmed, you must screen every transaction
and every party before export.

MANDATORY RESTRICTED PARTY SCREENING:
Screen all parties — customer, distributor, end-user, broker, freight forwarder,
bank, and any intermediate consignee — against:

  1. Entity List (Supplement 4, Part 744): Licence required for all EAR items;
     most licence exceptions INCLUDING ENC are unavailable for Entity List parties.
  2. Denied Persons List (Part 764): Absolute prohibition. No exceptions apply.
  3. Unverified List (Supplement 6, Part 744): Cannot use any licence exception.
  4. Military End-User (MEU) List (Supplement 7, Part 744): Enhanced restrictions
     for items in Supplement 2, Part 744.
  5. SDN List (OFAC): Not EAR, but must be screened alongside BIS lists.

Use the Consolidated Screening List at trade.gov/consolidated-screening-list for
a single-search approach across BIS, State, and Treasury lists.

WMD END-USE PROHIBITION (§ 744.2–744.6):
No licence exception — including ENC — applies if you know or have reason to know
your cybersecurity software will be used in nuclear weapons development, missile
programmes, or chemical/biological weapons programmes. This prohibition is absolute.

RED FLAGS (§ 732.6):
Given that you are selling cybersecurity software (a sensitive dual-use item),
apply heightened diligence. Stop and investigate before proceeding if:
  - A customer refuses to identify the end-use or end-users of the software
  - The order is inconsistent with the customer's stated business (e.g., a
    mining company purchasing enterprise cybersecurity software in unusual volumes)
  - Payment arrives from an unexpected third-country account
  - The customer declines standard licence/maintenance support
  - The customer requests delivery to an unusual transhipment point

STEP 7 — DEEMED EXPORT CONSIDERATIONS (§ 734.13)
================================================================================

If your company employs foreign nationals (including remote workers in France,
India, or Brazil) who have access to the source code or controlled technology
underlying your AES-256 implementation, a deemed export analysis is required.

Releasing controlled technology or software source code to a foreign national in
the US is deemed an export to their country of nationality. For 5D002 technology,
if a licence would be required for export of that technology to the foreign
national's home country, a deemed export licence or applicable exception must be
identified.

For France: country group A:5 — deemed export risk is very low; most exceptions
available.
For India and Brazil: Country Group B — ENC exception may cover deemed exports of
5D002 software; confirm with your legal counsel that the deemed export falls within
the ENC exception terms.

================================================================================
STEP 8 — ELECTRONIC EXPORT INFORMATION (EEI) FILING (PART 758)
================================================================================

EEI filing via the Automated Export System (AES) is required when:
  - The value of the shipment exceeds $2,500 per Schedule B number and the
    destination is outside Canada (all three destinations qualify).
  - When filing, cite "ENC" in the licence exception field (not a licence number).

For software delivered electronically (download, SaaS, cloud):
  - If no physical media crosses the border, EEI filing requirements may not apply
    to the electronic transmission itself. However, the EAR still controls the
    export, and your ENC notification and recordkeeping obligations remain in full
    force regardless of delivery method.

================================================================================
STEP 9 — RECORDKEEPING (PART 762)
================================================================================

Retain the following for 5 years from the date of each export (§ 762.6):
  - Purchase orders, invoices, and contracts
  - The ENC licence exception symbol ("ENC") on all shipping and export documents
  - Your SNAP-R BIS-748P-A notification confirmation
  - Annual self-classification reports submitted to BIS and NSA
  - Denied party screening records and date of each screening
  - Any end-user statements or due diligence documentation
  - EEI/AES filing records

================================================================================
IMPORTANT CAVEATS AND LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

1. This analysis is based on the information provided: commercial cybersecurity
   software, AES-256 encryption, exports to France, India, and Brazil. If the
   software includes additional controlled features (e.g., intrusion detection
   with NS or MT controls, or cryptanalysis tools), separate ECCN entries may
   apply alongside or instead of 5D002.

2. Country classification and Country Chart "X" marks are subject to regulatory
   updates. Verify current Country Chart status in Part 738 Supplement No. 1
   before export.

3. This analysis does not address the destination countries' own import controls
   for encryption software. India, Brazil, and France (via EU dual-use regulations)
   may impose independent import licence requirements. Consult local counsel in
   each jurisdiction.

4. The ENC exception does not authorise exports to E:1 countries (Cuba, North
   Korea, Iran, Syria) or E:2 countries (Russia, Belarus) for most encryption
   items. None of your three destinations are E:1 or E:2, so this restriction
   does not apply here but is noted for completeness.

5. This analysis constitutes legal/compliance information and not legal advice.
   Engage qualified export counsel for transaction-specific determinations and
   before submitting BIS notifications or licence applications.
