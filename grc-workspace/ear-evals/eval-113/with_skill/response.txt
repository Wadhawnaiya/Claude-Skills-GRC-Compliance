SITUATION ANALYSIS: APPARENT EAR VIOLATION — ENTITY LIST RE-EXPORT OF A 3A001 SEMICONDUCTOR

You have described a serious apparent violation of the Export Administration Regulations (EAR),
15 CFR Parts 730–774, administered by the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS), U.S. Department
of Commerce. This requires immediate, structured action. Below is a complete legal and compliance
roadmap.

================================================================================
PART 1 — UNDERSTANDING THE APPARENT VIOLATION
================================================================================

1.1 The Core Legal Problem

Your original export of a 3A001-classified semiconductor component to Singapore was, on its
face, a lawful transaction — Singapore is a Country Group A:1/B country and 3A001 items (Category
3, Electronics; Product Group A, Equipment) typically require a license only for specific Reasons
for Control to certain destinations. Singapore is generally a permissive destination.

The problem arises from the re-export. Under 15 CFR § 734.14, a "reexport" is any shipment,
release, or other transfer of an item subject to the EAR from one foreign country to another.
Your Singapore customer's onward transfer of the 3A001 component to a Chinese military research
institute on the Entity List (Supplement No. 4 to Part 744) constitutes an apparent unauthorized
reexport under 15 CFR § 736.2(b)(1) (General Prohibition One — export or reexport without a
required license) and § 736.2(b)(2) (General Prohibition Two — reexport contrary to a license
condition or EAR requirement).

1.2 Why the Entity List Is Central

The Entity List imposes a license requirement for ALL items subject to the EAR — regardless of
their ECCN or whether a license would otherwise be required for the destination country — when
shipped to a listed party (15 CFR § 744.11). For virtually all Entity List entries involving
Chinese military institutions, BIS's licensing policy is "presumption of denial," meaning a
license would almost certainly not have been granted. No license exception is available for
Entity List parties. (§ 740.2(a)(3) bars license exceptions for Supplement 4 parties.)

3A001 semiconductors are controlled for National Security (NS) and Anti-Terrorism (AT) reasons,
among potentially others depending on the specific item parameters. Items with NS controls
destined for a Chinese military end-user are among the most sensitive categories BIS enforces.

1.3 China Country Group Implications

China is in Country Group D:1 (national security concern). The receiving entity — a Chinese
military research institute — implicates § 744.21 (Military End-User controls) and potentially
§ 744.22 (Military Intelligence End-User controls), in addition to the Entity List restrictions
under § 744.11. If the institute is designated on the Entity List, § 744.11 controls are
dispositive; the MEU controls are additional layers of restriction.

1.4 The "Knew or Had Reason to Know" Standard — and Why It May Not Fully Protect You

You state you did not know at the time of original sale. The EAR "reason to know" standard
(§ 772.1) is objective — BIS asks whether a reasonable person in your position, given the
available facts, should have known. BIS will examine:

  - Did you conduct Know Your Customer (KYC) due diligence on the Singapore customer?
  - Did you obtain and verify an End-User Statement (EUS) or end-use certificate?
  - Were there red flags (§ 732.6 / Supplement 3 to Part 732) that were missed or ignored?
    (e.g., customer declining to identify the ultimate end-user, unusual order volume for a
    trading company, shipping to a transshipment hub with a declared end-user in a D:1 country)
  - Did your compliance program require screening of not just the direct customer but declared
    and potential end-users?

If red flags existed and were missed, BIS may find "reason to know" even without actual
knowledge. This distinction is critical to how the enforcement case will unfold.

PART 2 — IMMEDIATE STEPS TO TAKE (THE FIRST 72 HOURS)
================================================================================

Step 1: Convene an Internal Crisis Response Team
  - Immediately involve: General Counsel (or outside export controls counsel), Export Compliance
    Officer (ECO), senior management, and relevant business unit leads.
  - Do NOT discuss the potential violation in informal emails, chat, or with parties outside the
    response team. Attorney-client privilege should be established from the outset.
  - Retain outside EAR counsel with BIS enforcement experience if not already available in-house.

Step 2: Preserve All Records — Litigation Hold
  - Issue a document litigation hold immediately covering: all records related to the original
    Singapore transaction; all communications with the Singapore customer; shipping documents,
    invoices, purchase orders, EEI/AES filings, screening records; and any communications that
    may touch on the Chinese end-user.
  - Under 15 CFR § 762.2, you are required to retain export records for 5 years. Destruction of
    records after learning of a potential violation could itself constitute an additional violation
    and would be treated as an aggravating factor in any enforcement action.

Step 3: Stop Any Related Transactions
  - Immediately place the Singapore customer on hold for all pending orders.
  - Screen the Singapore customer against all restricted party lists (BIS Entity List, Denied
    Persons List, Unverified List, MEU List, OFAC SDN List) — if they do not themselves appear,
    evaluate whether they are a knowing facilitator whose future reliability is now compromised.
  - Identify any other transactions involving the same Singapore customer or any other customer
    that may have involved re-export to Chinese military entities.

Step 4: Conduct a Preliminary Internal Investigation
  - Reconstruct the original transaction: classification basis for 3A001, license determination,
    any license exception claimed, end-user screening conducted, due diligence documentation.
  - Establish what you now know about the Chinese military institute recipient: when you learned
    it, from what source, and what evidence exists.
  - Assess scope: Is this an isolated transaction or is there a pattern? Are there other shipments?

Step 5: File an Initial VSD Notification to Preserve the VSD Date (see Part 3 below)
  - Do not wait until the full investigation is complete. File a brief initial notification to OEE
    as soon as you have confirmed a likely apparent violation. This preserves your VSD date.

================================================================================
PART 3 — THE VOLUNTARY SELF-DISCLOSURE (VSD) PROCESS UNDER § 764.5
================================================================================

3.1 What a VSD Is

A Voluntary Self-Disclosure (VSD) is a self-initiated notification to BIS's Office of Export
Enforcement (OEE) of an apparent violation of the EAR, a license, or an order. It is governed
by 15 CFR § 764.5 and BIS's enforcement guidelines (Supplement No. 1 to Part 766).

Filing a VSD is strongly recommended — and in a matter of this seriousness, failing to disclose
carries substantially greater risk than disclosing. (See Part 4 on the disclosure vs. non-
disclosure calculus.)

3.2 The Two-Step VSD Process

STEP A — INITIAL NOTIFICATION (file immediately upon confirming a likely violation):

  - Written letter or email to: Office of Export Enforcement, Bureau of Industry and Security
  - Content: Company name and contact; general description of the apparent violation (item,
    destination, parties involved, approximate date); a statement that you are filing an initial
    VSD notification and will follow with a full submission.
  - Purpose: This locks in your VSD date. BIS will credit the disclosure from the date of the
    initial notification, not the date of the full submission.

STEP B — FULL VSD SUBMISSION (within 180 days of initial notification):

The full VSD must be a comprehensive written narrative including:

  (a) Company identification: name, address, EIN, CAGE code if applicable
  (b) Detailed facts: chronological narrative of all relevant events from order placement through
      discovery of the apparent violation
  (c) Transaction details for each affected shipment: shipper, consignee, declared end-user,
      actual end-user (the Chinese military institute), item description, ECCN (3A001),
      license exception claimed or license number, date of export, value of shipment
  (d) Legal analysis: which EAR provisions appear to have been violated (e.g., General Prohibition
      One under § 736.2(b)(1), § 744.11 Entity List requirements)
  (e) Root cause analysis: what controls failed, why the re-export was not caught
  (f) Remedial actions already taken: hold on customer, litigation hold, internal investigation
  (g) Proposed corrective actions: enhanced due diligence, updated screening, distributor
      agreement revisions, training enhancements (see Part 5)

3.3 What Happens After Filing

  - OEE will acknowledge receipt and assign a case number.
  - OEE may request additional information, documents, or interviews.
  - OEE may conduct End-Use Checks (post-shipment verifications) independently.
  - Resolution options:
      - No-Action Letter: Violation found minor; no penalty recommended
      - Warning Letter: Violation acknowledged; no monetary penalty; letter placed on record
      - Administrative penalty with VSD reduction: Negotiated settlement
      - Referral to Department of Justice for criminal investigation (reserved for egregious cases)

For a first-time violation with a strong compliance program and full cooperation, the likely
outcome for a non-willful violation is a warning letter or a significantly reduced administrative
penalty. However, given the Entity List party and military end-user dimensions, BIS will treat
this as a sensitive matter and the resolution will not be automatic.

PART 4 — CIVIL PENALTY EXPOSURE: DISCLOSING VS. NOT DISCLOSING
================================================================================

4.1 Penalty Structure Under § 764.3 and Part 766

Civil penalties for EAR violations are assessed per violation — and each shipment can constitute
multiple violations (wrong destination, wrong end-user, failure to obtain license = multiple
counts from a single transaction):

  Maximum civil penalty: Greater of $374,474 per violation (FY2025 inflation-adjusted figure)
                         OR 2 times the value of the transaction

For a single semiconductor shipment of even modest value, penalty exposure without mitigation
could easily reach $500,000–$1,000,000+ per transaction, and if multiple shipments are
involved, liability compounds accordingly.

Given the involvement of an Entity List military end-user in China — among the highest-priority
enforcement targets for BIS — OEE will apply heightened scrutiny and may treat this as an
egregious case if willfulness or recklessness is found.

4.2 The Mitigation Effect of a VSD

Under Supplement No. 1 to Part 766, BIS's penalty mitigation guidelines expressly identify
a VSD as a strong mitigating factor. In practice:

  WITH a VSD + strong ECP + full cooperation + remediation:
    - Non-egregious violations: Often resolved via warning letter or significant penalty reduction
      (50% or more reduction from base penalty)
    - Egregious violations: Penalty reduction of 25–40% from the otherwise-applicable maximum,
      plus reduced risk of Denial of Export Privileges

  WITHOUT a VSD:
    - If BIS discovers the violation independently (e.g., through an end-use check, tip-off,
      or customs data), the deliberate failure to disclose becomes an aggravating factor
    - BIS can assess the full statutory maximum; denial of export privileges becomes more likely
    - If the failure to disclose was deliberate, it may independently constitute a violation
      under § 764.2 (acting with knowledge of a violation)

4.3 Criminal Referral Considerations

Criminal penalties under § 764.2 apply to willful violations:
  - Individuals: Up to 20 years imprisonment + up to $1 million per violation
  - Corporations: Up to $1 million per violation

Criminal referral to DOJ is reserved for cases involving deliberate evasion, proliferation
risk, or knowing facilitation of military programs. Your scenario — unknowing original export
with subsequent discovery — is not the paradigm criminal case. However:

  - If the internal investigation reveals that employees had actual knowledge and concealed it,
    criminal exposure becomes real.
  - Failure to disclose after discovery, if deliberate, significantly raises criminal risk.
  - Cooperating fully with BIS via VSD and demonstrating good faith substantially reduces
    the probability of criminal referral.

4.4 The Disclosure Decision — Bottom Line

Given the facts as described (no knowledge at time of sale, discovered post-shipment), the
risk calculus strongly favors disclosure:

  DISCLOSE (VSD):
    + Strong penalty mitigation
    + Demonstrates good faith; likely outcome is warning letter or reduced civil penalty
    + Reduces criminal referral risk
    + Demonstrates effective compliance culture to regulators
    - Requires full cooperation, candor, and remediation commitment

  DO NOT DISCLOSE:
    - If BIS independently discovers the violation (highly likely for Entity List cases,
      which receive heightened scrutiny including end-use checks), maximum penalties apply
    - Deliberate non-disclosure is itself an aggravating — and potentially independent —
      violation
    - Vastly higher civil and criminal exposure
    - Possible Denial of Export Privileges, which would effectively end your ability to export

The decision to file a VSD should be made with export counsel, but the analysis here almost
universally points toward disclosure.

PART 5 — REMEDIATION STEPS
================================================================================

Following the VSD, BIS expects to see credible, specific corrective actions. These must be
included in the full VSD submission and implemented. Recommended steps:

5.1 Immediate Transaction-Level Remediation
  - Suspend all shipments to the Singapore customer pending investigation.
  - Notify the Singapore customer in writing that they must provide full documentation of all
    downstream transactions involving your products. (Do not tip off that you are filing a VSD,
    but you can require end-use information under your contractual terms.)
  - Assess whether the Singapore customer themselves should be reported to OEE separately as
    a knowing transshipper (§ 764.2(e) prohibits any person from facilitating a prohibited
    transaction).

5.2 Compliance Program Enhancements

  (a) Restricted Party Screening — Enhanced:
      - Implement transactional screening not just of the direct customer but also of declared
        end-users on export documentation.
      - For Singapore and other transshipment-risk jurisdictions (Hong Kong, UAE, Turkey,
        Malaysia): require an end-user certificate identifying the ultimate end-user before
        order acceptance — not just at shipment.
      - Re-screen all existing high-risk distributor relationships against all BIS lists and OFAC.

  (b) Know Your Customer (KYC) — Strengthen:
      - Require End-User Statements (EUS / BIS-711) for all 3A001 and similarly controlled items
        destined for known transshipment hubs.
      - Conduct enhanced due diligence: customer site visits or third-party due diligence reports
        for high-value/high-risk customers.
      - Add contractual representations and warranties in distributor and reseller agreements:
        downstream customers must be screened; re-export to D:1 country end-users requires
        separate authorization; customer must notify you of any re-export requests.

  (c) Red Flag Review:
      - Audit the original transaction file to identify whether red flags existed. If they did,
        implement a formalized red flag checklist for order acceptance (see § 732.6 and Supplement
        3 to Part 732).
      - Require ECO sign-off on any transaction involving a customer in a known transshipment
        jurisdiction when the product is NS-controlled.

  (d) Training:
      - Conduct immediate targeted training for sales, logistics, and customer management teams
        on Entity List obligations, re-export responsibilities, and transshipment red flags.
      - Annual refresher training going forward with documented completion records (5-year
        retention per § 762.6).

  (e) Distributor Agreement Revisions:
      - Update all distributor and reseller agreements to include: export compliance
        representations, re-export restrictions, right to audit, flow-down of EAR obligations to
        sub-distributors, and termination rights for compliance violations.

  (f) Post-Shipment Verification (Internal):
      - For controlled items shipped to transshipment-risk destinations, implement a post-shipment
        follow-up process: contact the customer 30–60 days post-delivery to confirm receipt and
        request confirmation of end-use/end-user as declared.

5.3 Internal Audit
  - Conduct an immediate retroactive audit of all shipments to this Singapore customer for the
    past 5 years (the recordkeeping period under § 762.6). Identify any other transactions that
    may involve similar issues and include them in the VSD.
  - BIS will expect a comprehensive disclosure — a VSD that omits related violations discovered
    in the same investigation will be treated as an incomplete disclosure.

5.4 Leadership and Governance
  - Brief senior leadership and board (if appropriate) on the apparent violation and the VSD
    decision.
  - Assign ownership of the remediation plan to the ECO with executive sponsor accountability.
  - Document all remediation actions with dates and evidence — BIS will review these as part of
    the VSD resolution process.
