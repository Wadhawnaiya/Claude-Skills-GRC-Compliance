EU AI ACT COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS: AI DIAGNOSTIC IMAGING TOOL IN A HOSPITAL SETTING
CE-Marked Class IIa Medical Device (EU MDR 2017/745)

================================================================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

Yes, you likely need to take additional steps beyond your existing CE marking under the EU MDR. The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689, in force from August 2024, with most provisions applying from August 2026) establishes a parallel, complementary regulatory layer that interacts with — but does not fully replace — your existing medical device obligations. Whether the CE marking already covers you depends critically on which conformity assessment route was used and whether it was specifically updated to address the AI Act's requirements.

================================================================================
1. CLASSIFICATION OF YOUR AI SYSTEM UNDER THE EU AI ACT
================================================================================

1.1 High-Risk AI System Classification

Your AI diagnostic imaging tool almost certainly qualifies as a HIGH-RISK AI SYSTEM under the EU AI Act. Annex III of the Regulation lists categories of AI systems presumed to be high-risk. Point 5(a) of Annex III explicitly includes:

  "AI systems intended to be used as safety components of products, or as products themselves, covered by the Union harmonisation legislation listed in Annex I..."

EU MDR (Regulation 2017/745) is listed in Annex I of the EU AI Act. Since your tool:
  (a) Is covered by EU MDR as a Class IIa medical device, AND
  (b) The vendor designates it as a "safety component" of your radiology department's operations,

...it falls squarely within the high-risk category under Annex III, Point 5.

1.2 What "Safety Component" Means

The EU AI Act defines a safety component as a component of a product or system that fulfils a safety function for that product or system, or the failure or malfunctioning of which endangers the health and safety of persons. In diagnostic imaging AI, failures (false negatives missing cancer, false positives triggering unnecessary procedures) directly affect patient safety. The vendor's characterization appears legally sound and works against any argument that this system is lower-risk.

1.3 No Exemption for Already CE-Marked Devices

There is no blanket exemption in the EU AI Act for AI systems that are already CE-marked under EU MDR. The two frameworks operate in parallel.

================================================================================
2. THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN EU MDR CE MARKING AND THE EU AI ACT
================================================================================

2.1 The "Fast-Track" Pathway Under Article 8 and Annex I

The EU AI Act creates a mechanism for AI systems that are already subject to EU harmonisation legislation listed in Annex I (which includes EU MDR). Under Article 8(1), high-risk AI systems that are already covered by those sector-specific laws may meet some EU AI Act requirements through compliance with those sector-specific laws — BUT ONLY IF:

  - The existing sector legislation covers the same objectives and requirements as the corresponding EU AI Act obligations; AND
  - The conformity assessment was conducted by a Notified Body and already addresses AI-specific risks.

This is NOT a full exemption. It is a potential streamlining mechanism, often called the "single conformity assessment" pathway.

2.2 What EU MDR Does and Does Not Cover

EU MDR (2017/745) already requires, for Class IIa devices:
  - Risk management systems (ISO 14971)
  - Clinical evaluation and post-market surveillance
  - Quality management systems (typically ISO 13485)
  - Technical documentation
  - Notified Body involvement in conformity assessment

However, EU MDR does not fully address all EU AI Act requirements. Specific AI Act obligations NOT typically covered by EU MDR alone include:
  - Transparency and explainability requirements for AI-specific decision logic
  - Human oversight mechanisms specific to AI systems (as opposed to general device supervision)
  - Data governance requirements specific to training, validation, and test datasets
  - Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity requirements framed in AI-specific terms
  - AI system logging and auditability of AI decisions (immutable logs of AI outputs)
  - Conformity with technical specifications for AI accuracy and bias mitigation
  - Post-market monitoring specifically targeting AI model drift and retraining events

2.3 The "Presumption of Conformity" Does Not Apply Automatically

Your existing CE mark does not automatically create a presumption of conformity with the EU AI Act's requirements. The AI Act's presumption of conformity mechanisms relate to adherence to harmonized standards developed under the AI Act (e.g., by CEN/CENELEC) or to common specifications adopted by the European Commission — most of which were still under development as of mid-2025.

================================================================================
3. OBLIGATIONS UNDER THE EU AI ACT THAT APPLY TO YOU AS A DEPLOYER
================================================================================

The EU AI Act distinguishes between "providers" (those who develop and place the AI system on the market) and "deployers" (those who use the AI system in a professional context). As a hospital, you are a DEPLOYER. However, deployers of high-risk AI systems have significant, non-trivial obligations.

3.1 Deployer Obligations Under Article 26

As a deployer you must:

  a) USE THE SYSTEM AS INTENDED: Only use the AI system in accordance with the provider's instructions of use. Any use outside the intended purpose may transfer provider obligations to you.

  b) IMPLEMENT HUMAN OVERSIGHT: Designate individuals with the necessary competence, authority, and resources to exercise meaningful human oversight over the AI system. In a radiology context, this means ensuring qualified radiologists can understand, question, and if necessary override AI outputs. Human oversight must be real and not merely nominal.

  c) MONITOR FOR ANOMALIES: Conduct ongoing monitoring to detect unexpected performance or outputs inconsistent with the system's intended purpose. You must have processes in place to detect when the system may be operating outside its validated parameters (e.g., patient population drift, equipment changes affecting image quality).

  d) SUSPEND USE WHEN RISKS ARISE: If you identify a risk to health, safety, or fundamental rights not addressed by the provider, you must inform the provider and, if necessary, suspend use. You must have a defined protocol for this.

  e) MAINTAIN LOGS: To the extent technically possible, ensure the keeping of automatically generated logs. You must retain logs for at least six months (or as otherwise required by EU or national law, which may be longer in healthcare contexts).

  f) INFORM AFFECTED PERSONS: Natural persons (patients) subject to decisions from the AI system have the right to be informed that they are subject to an AI system. You must have a mechanism for disclosing AI involvement to patients and, upon request, providing a meaningful explanation of the AI's role in their care pathway.

  g) CONDUCT A FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS IMPACT ASSESSMENT (FRIA): Under Article 27, deployers that are bodies governed by public law OR operators of critical infrastructure (hospitals may qualify as both) must conduct a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment before deploying the high-risk AI system. This assessment must:
     - Describe the processes in which the AI system will be used
     - Identify the categories of natural persons affected
     - Assess specific risks to fundamental rights (non-discrimination, dignity, privacy)
     - Identify measures to address those risks
     - Be documented and registered

  h) REGISTER IN THE EU DATABASE: Under Article 49 and the EU AI Act's database provisions, deployers that are public bodies must register their use of high-risk AI systems in the EU-wide database (managed by the European AI Office). Private hospitals may have analogous obligations depending on how "public body" is interpreted in your jurisdiction.

3.2 Operator Obligations That May Shift to You

If you materially modify the AI system (change its purpose, retrain it, significantly alter its use environment beyond what the provider envisioned), you may assume provider obligations — including full conformity assessment obligations. Carefully document the scope of your deployment to avoid inadvertently becoming a "provider" under the AI Act.

================================================================================
4. OBLIGATIONS THAT REMAIN WITH THE VENDOR (PROVIDER)
================================================================================

While your vendor bears primary responsibility for the AI system's conformity as a high-risk AI system, you should verify that they have fulfilled their obligations, because deploying a non-compliant system exposes you to enforcement risk.

Confirm with your vendor that they have:

  a) Registered the AI system in the EU database for high-risk AI systems (Article 49)
  b) Drawn up EU Declaration of Conformity specifically under the AI Act (not just under MDR)
  c) Affixed the CE marking in compliance with AI Act requirements (noting that for Annex I products, the AI Act CE marking is folded into the existing CE mark but must specifically cover AI Act compliance)
  d) Established a post-market monitoring system for the AI model specifically, tracking model performance, drift, and incidents under AI Act Article 72
  e) Provided you with instructions of use that specifically address AI Act requirements: intended purpose, performance levels, known limitations, foreseeable misuse scenarios, human oversight measures, cybersecurity requirements
  f) Established an EU-based representative if they are a non-EU provider
  g) Implemented a quality management system covering the AI system's lifecycle

================================================================================
5. TECHNICAL REQUIREMENTS YOU SHOULD VERIFY
================================================================================

5.1 Accuracy, Robustness, and Cybersecurity (Article 15)

The AI system must be designed and developed to achieve an appropriate level of accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity throughout its lifecycle. You should verify with your vendor:
  - The validated performance metrics (sensitivity, specificity, AUC) on representative European patient populations
  - How the system performs on subpopulations (age, sex, ethnicity, equipment type) — bias and fairness testing
  - Adversarial robustness provisions
  - Cybersecurity measures protecting the AI model from manipulation

5.2 Data Governance

The training, validation, and test datasets must have met requirements for relevance, representativeness, and freedom from errors. Request evidence from your vendor that:
  - Training data included representative patient populations for your clinical use cases
  - Data governance practices met EU AI Act Article 10 standards
  - Any known data limitations are disclosed in the instructions of use

5.3 Logging and Auditability

Ensure your IT infrastructure can capture and retain the logs that the AI system automatically generates. These logs must be capable of tracing AI outputs back to specific inputs (patient images) and must be kept for the legally required period. In healthcare, this likely means integration with your RIS/PACS and conformance with your clinical records retention policies.

================================================================================
6. TRANSITIONAL PROVISIONS AND TIMELINE
================================================================================

The EU AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024. For high-risk AI systems covered by Annex III (which includes your system), the compliance deadline is 2 August 2026. However:

  - Prohibited AI practices: Applied from 2 February 2025
  - GPAI model obligations: Applied from 2 August 2025
  - High-risk Annex III systems (your category): 2 August 2026
  - High-risk Annex I systems (products under harmonisation law): 2 August 2027

IMPORTANT DISTINCTION: Your system likely falls under BOTH Annex I (as an EU MDR product) and Annex III (as an AI safety component). Where a system is covered by both, the more specific classification should be applied, but regulatory guidance from the European AI Office should be consulted. The 2027 deadline for Annex I products may apply, but you should not assume the later deadline applies without verifying your system's precise classification.

For systems already on the market before the AI Act compliance dates, transitional provisions in Article 111 may provide additional time, subject to conditions (no significant changes to the system after the relevant dates).

================================================================================
7. INTERACTION WITH OTHER EU LAW
================================================================================

7.1 GDPR

AI diagnostic imaging involves special category health data under GDPR. Your AI Act compliance must be coordinated with your GDPR obligations, particularly:
  - Legal basis for processing health data in AI systems
  - Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) under GDPR Article 35 (likely mandatory for high-risk AI processing health data at scale)
  - Rights of data subjects including the right not to be subject to solely automated decisions (Article 22 GDPR) — important where AI influences clinical decisions
  - Data retention and deletion obligations

7.2 EU MDR Post-Market Surveillance

Your EU MDR post-market surveillance plan should be updated to specifically monitor AI-specific failure modes: model drift, dataset shift, performance degradation on new equipment, and unexpected error patterns. This aligns with but goes beyond the AI Act's post-market monitoring requirements.

7.3 NIS2 Directive

Hospitals are designated as "essential entities" under NIS2. Your AI system introduces cybersecurity considerations that must be addressed in your NIS2 compliance framework, particularly regarding supply chain security (your vendor as a critical supplier) and incident reporting for AI system failures.

================================================================================
8. PRACTICAL STEPS YOU SHOULD TAKE NOW
================================================================================

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (Within 3-6 Months):

1. Obtain documentation from your vendor confirming their AI Act compliance roadmap and whether they have or will obtain EU Declaration of Conformity under the AI Act in addition to EU MDR CE marking.

2. Review the vendor's instructions of use specifically for AI Act-required content (human oversight measures, performance limits, known risks).

3. Designate a responsible person internally (could be your medical AI governance lead, Chief Medical Information Officer, or equivalent) with oversight responsibility for AI Act compliance.

4. Conduct or commission a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) under Article 27 if you are a public hospital or operate as critical infrastructure.

5. Map your existing EU MDR technical file and quality management system to the EU AI Act's high-risk AI system requirements to identify gaps.

SHORT-TERM ACTIONS (6-12 Months):

6. Establish or update your human oversight protocols for AI-assisted radiology: define the radiologist's supervisory role, decision authority, and documentation requirements when AI outputs are used.

7. Implement log retention policies for AI system outputs, integrated with your clinical records management.

8. Develop a patient information and transparency mechanism (consent documentation, patient-facing notices) disclosing AI involvement in diagnostics.

9. Update your IT vendor management framework to treat your AI vendor as a high-risk supplier under both AI Act and NIS2.

10. Register your use of the AI system in the EU database (if applicable for deployers in your jurisdiction).

MEDIUM-TERM ACTIONS (12-24 Months):

11. Implement ongoing performance monitoring: establish thresholds for acceptable AI performance, protocols for suspending use if performance degrades, and a clear escalation path to notify the vendor.

12. Coordinate your AI Act compliance with your GDPR DPIA, NIS2 security obligations, and EU MDR post-market surveillance plan into a unified AI governance framework.

13. Train radiology staff on AI Act obligations: their role in human oversight, incident reporting, and documentation requirements.

14. Establish contractual protections with your vendor: require notification of model updates, retraining events, performance changes, and regulatory status changes; require cooperation in your incident reporting obligations.

================================================================================
9. DOES THE CE MARKING ALREADY COVER YOU?
================================================================================

DIRECT ANSWER: No, not fully.

Your existing CE marking under EU MDR:
  - DOES demonstrate conformity with medical device safety and performance requirements
  - DOES involve Notified Body assessment for a Class IIa device
  - DOES address risk management, clinical evaluation, and quality management
  - MAY overlap with some EU AI Act technical requirements where the MDR requirements are equivalent

Your existing CE marking DOES NOT:
  - Constitute a Declaration of Conformity under the EU AI Act
  - Address AI-specific transparency, explainability, and logging requirements
  - Cover your deployer obligations under the AI Act (FRIA, human oversight protocols, patient information)
  - Automatically register the system in the EU AI Act database
  - Satisfy the AI Act's specific accuracy, robustness, and bias testing requirements as framed in the AI Act

WHAT THE VENDOR SHOULD PROVIDE: The vendor should be updating their conformity assessment and technical documentation to cover EU AI Act requirements, and should issue a supplementary or updated Declaration of Conformity that specifically addresses AI Act compliance in addition to EU MDR conformity. If your vendor claims the existing CE mark is sufficient without any additional steps, that claim should be examined very carefully and legal/regulatory advice sought.

================================================================================
10. KEY RISKS OF NON-COMPLIANCE
================================================================================

- Market surveillance authorities (national competent authorities for AI in each Member State) can require corrective actions, withdrawal, or prohibition of use
- Administrative fines under the AI Act for providers: up to EUR 15 million or 3% of global annual turnover for violations of high-risk AI system obligations; up to EUR 30 million or 6% for prohibited AI practice violations
- Fines for deployers are determined by Member States and may be significant
- Patient safety incidents involving non-compliant AI systems create liability exposure under both the AI Act and EU MDR
- Reputational risk in a regulated healthcare environment
- Potential conflict with your hospital's accreditation requirements if AI governance is found inadequate

================================================================================
CONCLUSION
================================================================================

Your AI diagnostic imaging tool is almost certainly a high-risk AI system under the EU AI Act, and the existing CE marking under EU MDR does not alone satisfy the EU AI Act's requirements. You face a dual regulatory framework: continue meeting EU MDR obligations while also building out the additional governance, oversight, documentation, and transparency infrastructure required by the AI Act. The compliance deadline for your category of system is 2 August 2026 (or possibly 2027 for Annex I products — seek guidance on this point).

You should engage your vendor immediately to confirm their AI Act compliance roadmap, begin your internal gap assessment, and conduct a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment. Given the clinical stakes and regulatory complexity, engaging a regulatory affairs specialist with specific EU AI Act and EU MDR expertise is strongly recommended.
