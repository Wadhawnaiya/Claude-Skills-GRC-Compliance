EU AI ACT COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS: AI DIAGNOSTIC IMAGING TOOL IN A HOSPITAL SETTING
CE-Marked Class IIa Medical Device (EU MDR Regulation 2017/745) / Safety Component

====================================================================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
====================================================================================

Your CE marking under EU MDR (Regulation 2017/745) does NOT fully cover you under the
EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689). The diagnostic imaging AI tool is classified as
HIGH-RISK under the EU AI Act via Article 6(1) — the Annex I "safety component" path —
and carries significant additional obligations for both the vendor (as provider) and your
hospital (as deployer). The CE mark from the MDR conformity assessment is integrated into
the AI Act conformity procedure but does not replace it; you must ensure the vendor has
completed the additional AI Act-specific steps and that your hospital fulfils all deployer
obligations. Below is a full structured analysis.

====================================================================================
STEP 1: SCOPE AND ROLE IDENTIFICATION
====================================================================================

Your hospital is a DEPLOYER under Article 3(4) of the EU AI Act: you are using an AI
system under your own authority in the course of professional activity (operating a
radiology department). The vendor who developed and CE-marked the diagnostic imaging AI
tool is the PROVIDER under Article 3(3).

This distinction is critical: the AI Act assigns different — though overlapping —
obligations to providers and deployers. Your primary obligations arise under Article 26,
but you also bear responsibilities related to monitoring, incident reporting, and ensuring
the provider has met its obligations before you deploy.

====================================================================================
STEP 2: AI SYSTEM DEFINITION CHECK (ART. 3(1))
====================================================================================

An AI diagnostic imaging tool that analyses medical images (X-rays, CT scans, MRI) to
generate diagnostic predictions, findings, or recommendations clearly meets the Article
3(1) definition of an AI system: it is a machine-based system that, for given objectives
(detecting pathologies, anomalies, or conditions), infers from inputs (medical images and
patient data) how to generate outputs (diagnostic predictions or recommendations) that
affect real environments (clinical decisions and patient care). The threshold is met.

====================================================================================
STEP 3: PROHIBITED PRACTICES SCREEN (ART. 5 — IN FORCE FROM 2 FEBRUARY 2025)
====================================================================================

None of the eight prohibited practices under Article 5 apply to a standard diagnostic
imaging AI tool used for radiology support:

- No subliminal manipulation (Art. 5(1)(a))
- No exploitation of vulnerabilities (Art. 5(1)(b))
- No social scoring (Art. 5(1)(c))
- No predictive criminal profiling (Art. 5(1)(d))
- No untargeted biometric database scraping (Art. 5(1)(e))
- No prohibited workplace emotion inference (Art. 5(1)(f))
- No sensitive-attribute biometric categorisation (Art. 5(1)(g))
- No real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces for law enforcement (Art. 5(1)(h))

The system is not prohibited and may proceed to risk classification.

====================================================================================
STEP 4: RISK TIER DETERMINATION — HIGH-RISK VIA ART. 6(1), ANNEX I PATH
====================================================================================

This system is HIGH-RISK under BOTH available paths under Article 6.

PATH A — ART. 6(1): SAFETY COMPONENT OF AN ANNEX I PRODUCT (PRIMARY PATH)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
Article 6(1) classifies an AI system as high-risk when two cumulative conditions are
satisfied:

Condition 1: The AI system is a safety component of a product (or is itself such a
product) covered by Annex I harmonisation legislation.

SATISFIED: Annex I, Section A, Item 11 of the EU AI Act explicitly lists "Medical
devices — Regulation 2017/745 (EU MDR)" as Annex I harmonisation legislation. Your
diagnostic imaging AI tool has been CE-marked under EU MDR as a Class IIa medical
device. Your vendor additionally describes it as a "safety component for your radiology
department." Either characterisation satisfies this limb: a CE-marked medical device
under the MDR is per se an Annex I product, and the vendor's own characterisation as a
safety component confirms the safety role.

Condition 2: The product must require third-party conformity assessment under the
relevant Annex I legislation.

SATISFIED: Under EU MDR Regulation 2017/745, Class IIa medical devices require
involvement of a notified body in the conformity assessment procedure. This is a
mandatory third-party assessment requirement — the self-certification route is not
available for Class IIa devices. The second condition is therefore met.

Conclusion on Path A: Both conditions under Article 6(1) are met. The AI diagnostic
imaging tool is definitively a HIGH-RISK AI system under the EU AI Act.

PATH B — ART. 6(2), ANNEX III: INDEPENDENT ALTERNATIVE BASIS
-------------------------------------------------------------
Even without the Annex I path, Annex III, Area 5(a) provides an independent basis for
high-risk classification: "AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of competent
authorities or public authorities for the purpose of evaluating the eligibility of natural
persons for public assistance benefits and services, including healthcare." Where the
hospital is an NHS-equivalent or publicly funded hospital, this path may also apply.
However, the Annex I path under Article 6(1) is the primary and more certain
classification route here.

====================================================================================
STEP 5: DOES THE CE MARK UNDER EU MDR COVER YOU UNDER THE EU AI ACT?
====================================================================================

THE SHORT ANSWER: NO — THE MDR CE MARK DOES NOT SUBSTITUTE FOR AI ACT COMPLIANCE,
BUT IT IS INTEGRATED INTO (NOT SEPARATE FROM) THE AI ACT CONFORMITY PROCEDURE.

Here is the precise legal position:

1. INTEGRATION, NOT REPLACEMENT (ART. 43(3) AND RECITAL 88)
   For AI systems that are safety components of Annex I products, Article 43(3) of the
   AI Act provides that the conformity assessment of the AI system shall be carried out
   as part of the conformity assessment procedure applicable under the relevant Annex I
   legislation — in this case, the EU MDR procedure. The notified body that assessed
   the medical device under EU MDR therefore also forms part of the AI Act conformity
   pathway. There is no entirely separate AI Act notified body assessment for these
   systems.

   However — and this is the critical point — this integration means the MDR conformity
   assessment procedure must be conducted in a manner that ALSO covers compliance with
   the AI Act's Section 2 requirements (Arts. 9–15). The existing MDR CE certification
   alone, conducted before the AI Act applied, does not accomplish this. The notified
   body would need to have assessed compliance with the AI Act's specific requirements
   (risk management system under Art. 9, data governance under Art. 10, technical
   documentation under Art. 11, logging under Art. 12, transparency/instructions for use
   under Art. 13, human oversight under Art. 14, accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity under
   Art. 15) as part of or alongside the MDR assessment.

2. TIMING: AI ACT OBLIGATIONS FOR ANNEX I SAFETY COMPONENTS APPLY FROM 2 AUGUST 2027
   The AI Act's high-risk obligations for Annex I product safety components (including
   medical devices under EU MDR) do not apply until 2 August 2027 — one year later than
   the 2 August 2026 date for Annex III systems. This gives the vendor and your hospital
   additional time, but planning must begin now.

3. SEPARATE EU AI ACT DECLARATION OF CONFORMITY REQUIRED
   Article 47 requires a separate EU Declaration of Conformity for AI Act compliance.
   This is distinct from the EU Declaration of Conformity under the MDR. The AI Act DoC
   must specifically confirm compliance with all applicable AI Act requirements and must
   be maintained for 10 years from market placement. Your vendor must draw this up.

4. CE MARKING CARRIES DUAL MEANING BUT DUAL REQUIREMENTS
   After completing AI Act-compliant conformity assessment, the CE marking affixed under
   Article 48 of the AI Act signals conformity with both the MDR and the AI Act. The
   same physical CE mark is used, but the legal basis is dual: the vendor must have
   satisfied both regulatory frameworks. A CE mark obtained solely under the MDR before
   AI Act applicability is insufficient to signal AI Act conformity.

5. EU AI DATABASE REGISTRATION (ART. 49)
   The vendor, as provider, must register the AI system in the EU AI database before
   market placement (for Annex I systems, this registration obligation also applies).
   MDR registration (EUDAMED) is a separate database and does not substitute for EU AI
   database registration.

====================================================================================
STEP 6: PROVIDER OBLIGATIONS — WHAT YOUR VENDOR MUST DO
====================================================================================

As the hospital, you should verify that your vendor has met or has a compliant plan to
meet the following obligations before 2 August 2027 (the applicability date for Annex I
safety component systems):

ART. 9 — RISK MANAGEMENT SYSTEM
The vendor must establish and maintain a continuous, lifecycle-spanning risk management
system covering all known and reasonably foreseeable risks to patients, clinical staff,
and third parties. This must include five documented steps: risk identification, risk
estimation/evaluation, post-market monitoring integration, risk mitigation (design-first
hierarchy), and residual risk acceptability assessment. Testing against pre-defined
metrics before market placement is required.

ART. 10 — DATA AND DATA GOVERNANCE
Training, validation, and testing datasets must be representative of the patient
populations and imaging conditions the system will encounter in your radiology department.
Bias examination and gap identification are mandatory. If special-category health data
was used to detect/correct bias in training, the vendor must satisfy all six conditions
of the Article 10(5) exception.

ART. 11 — TECHNICAL DOCUMENTATION (ANNEX IV)
Comprehensive technical documentation per Annex IV must be created before market
placement and kept up to date throughout the system's lifecycle. This must include
the full description of the system, training methodologies, data used, validation
results, risk management documentation, and all changes made over the lifecycle.
Retention period: 10 years from market placement.

ART. 12 — AUTOMATIC LOGGING CAPABILITY
The system must be technically capable of automatically generating event logs throughout
operation. For a diagnostic imaging tool, this means logging should capture the
circumstances surrounding outputs (image inputs, model version, output confidence scores,
timestamps) to enable post-incident reconstruction and audit.

ART. 13 — TRANSPARENCY AND INSTRUCTIONS FOR USE
The vendor must provide your radiology department with clear instructions for use
covering: the system's intended purpose, performance metrics (accuracy, robustness),
known limitations, human oversight measures, input data specifications, and logging
mechanisms. These instructions are the basis on which your hospital's deployer
obligations rest.

ART. 14 — HUMAN OVERSIGHT DESIGN
The system must be designed to allow radiologists and clinical staff to:
- Understand the system's capabilities and limitations
- Recognise automation bias (over-reliance on AI output without independent clinical
  judgment)
- Correctly interpret AI outputs — including interpretability or explainability features
  showing which image features drove a finding
- Override or disregard AI outputs
- Intervene via a stop/interrupt mechanism

ART. 15 — ACCURACY, ROBUSTNESS, AND CYBERSECURITY
The vendor must declare and maintain appropriate accuracy levels throughout the system's
operational lifetime. Robustness against errors, image quality variations, and
foreseeable misuse is required. Cybersecurity protections must address data poisoning,
model poisoning, adversarial image attacks, and confidentiality attacks.

ART. 16 — FULL PROVIDER CHECKLIST
The vendor must complete all 12 provider obligations, including: compliance with Arts.
9–15; quality management system (Art. 17); technical documentation maintenance; EU
Declaration of Conformity (Art. 47); CE marking (Art. 48); and EU AI database
registration (Art. 49).

ART. 17 — QUALITY MANAGEMENT SYSTEM
The vendor must have a documented QMS covering 13 components, including regulatory
compliance strategy, design control, testing and validation, data management, risk
management, post-market monitoring, and serious incident reporting procedures. Existing
MDR QMS requirements (ISO 13485) can be adapted and integrated to avoid duplication,
but must be supplemented to cover AI Act-specific elements.

ART. 72 — POST-MARKET MONITORING SYSTEM
The vendor must establish a post-market monitoring system proportionate to the risks,
collecting and analysing performance data from deployed systems throughout operational
lifetime. Existing MDR post-market surveillance requirements can be integrated into
this system (Recital 88 and Art. 72 permit integration with sectoral post-market
monitoring obligations).

====================================================================================
STEP 7: DEPLOYER OBLIGATIONS — WHAT YOUR HOSPITAL MUST DO (ART. 26)
====================================================================================

As the deployer, your hospital bears the following independent obligations under Article
26, applicable from 2 August 2027 for Annex I systems (note: some general obligations
take effect on the broader 2 August 2026 date if the system is also classifiable under
Annex III):

1. INSTRUCTIONS COMPLIANCE (ART. 26(1))
   Use the AI diagnostic imaging tool only in accordance with the vendor's instructions
   for use (Art. 13). Do not use the system for purposes, patient populations, or imaging
   modalities outside those specified by the vendor.

2. STAFF COMPETENCE AND OVERSIGHT ASSIGNMENT (ART. 26(2) AND ART. 14(4))
   Assign human oversight of AI outputs to radiologists or clinical staff with the
   necessary competence, authority, and resources. Train oversight personnel on the
   system's capabilities and limitations, and specifically on automation bias risk —
   the tendency to over-rely on AI outputs without independent clinical assessment.

3. INPUT DATA CONTROL (ART. 26(3))
   Where your hospital controls the input data (e.g., image acquisition protocols,
   patient preparation, PACS integration), ensure input data is relevant and
   sufficiently representative. If your patient population differs materially from the
   training dataset described in the vendor's technical documentation, flag this to the
   vendor and assess whether additional validation is needed.

4. CONTINUOUS MONITORING (ART. 26(4))
   Monitor the operation of the AI system in your radiology department. If you identify
   risks to health, safety, or fundamental rights arising from actual use, you must
   notify the vendor/importer/distributor AND the relevant market surveillance authority.
   Where appropriate, suspend use pending investigation.

5. SERIOUS INCIDENT REPORTING (ART. 26(5) AND ART. 73)
   Immediately notify the vendor if the AI tool is involved in a serious incident —
   meaning an event that directly or indirectly leads or could lead to death, serious
   damage to health, or serious and irreversible harm. Additionally notify the market
   surveillance authority of the Member State where the incident occurred. For a hospital,
   this interacts with your existing MDR vigilance reporting obligations; Art. 72 and
   Recital 88 contemplate integration of these reporting channels.

6. LOG RETENTION (ART. 26(6))
   Retain automatically generated logs of the AI system's operation for a minimum of
   6 months. Where applicable Union or national law (including MDR post-market
   surveillance requirements) imposes longer retention periods, the longer period applies.

7. GDPR DPIA (ART. 26(9))
   Conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment under GDPR Article 35 before deploying
   the system if the processing of health data (which is special-category data under
   GDPR Article 9) presents a high risk to patient rights. For a diagnostic imaging AI
   processing identifiable patient health data, a DPIA is almost certainly required and
   should be completed before deployment.

8. FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS IMPACT ASSESSMENT (ART. 27)
   If your hospital is a public authority or body, Art. 27 may require a Fundamental
   Rights Impact Assessment before deploying high-risk AI systems. This assessment covers:
   the processes in which the system will be used, the time period and frequency,
   categories of persons affected, specific risks to those persons, and planned human
   oversight measures. Even where Art. 27 does not apply as a legal mandate (e.g., for
   private hospitals), conducting a FRIA-equivalent assessment is strongly recommended
   given the vulnerability of the patient population.

STEP 9: SPECIFIC ANSWER ON CE MARKING — WHAT IT COVERS AND WHAT IT DOES NOT
====================================================================================

Your existing CE mark under EU MDR (Regulation 2017/745) demonstrates that:
- The diagnostic imaging AI tool has undergone notified body review as a Class IIa
  medical device
- The device meets EU MDR safety and performance requirements for medical devices
- The device's clinical performance and safety have been assessed under MDR Annex IX
  or XI procedures

Your existing CE mark does NOT demonstrate that:
- The AI system complies with AI Act Article 9 (risk management system for AI risks)
- The AI system complies with AI Act Article 10 (AI-specific data governance)
- The AI system was assessed against AI Act Article 11/Annex IV (AI technical
  documentation requirements — which go beyond MDR technical file requirements)
- The AI system has automatic logging capability compliant with Art. 12
- The instructions for use meet Art. 13 requirements (which include AI-specific
  transparency requirements such as automation bias warnings and oversight instructions)
- The system is designed with Art. 14 human oversight mechanisms
- The system has been assessed for AI-specific cybersecurity risks under Art. 15
- The vendor has drawn up an AI Act EU Declaration of Conformity (Art. 47)
- The system is registered in the EU AI Act database (Art. 49)

The pathway forward under Art. 43(3) is that the MDR notified body assessment is
INTEGRATED into the AI Act conformity procedure — not replaced by it. Your vendor must
work with their notified body to extend the conformity assessment scope to cover the
AI Act's specific requirements, produce the AI Act Declaration of Conformity, and
register in the EU AI database. This is additional work on top of the MDR CE marking,
not a duplication of it.

====================================================================================
STEP 10: PENALTIES FOR NON-COMPLIANCE
====================================================================================

Failure to comply with AI Act obligations for providers and deployers can result in
fines under Article 99:

- Violations of provider/deployer obligations (Arts. 16, 26, etc.): up to €15,000,000
  or 3% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
- Incorrect or misleading information provided to authorities: up to €7,500,000 or 1%
  of total worldwide annual turnover.

Market surveillance authorities in each Member State have enforcement powers including
requiring corrective action, withdrawal from service, and recalls (Art. 74). The MDR's
existing market surveillance infrastructure will interface with AI Act enforcement for
medical device AI systems.

====================================================================================
CONCLUSION
====================================================================================

Your hospital's AI diagnostic imaging tool is unambiguously HIGH-RISK under the EU AI
Act via Article 6(1) — it is a safety component of a product (Class IIa medical device
under EU MDR, an Annex I law) that requires third-party conformity assessment. High-risk
obligations for Annex I safety components apply from 2 AUGUST 2027.

The CE mark under EU MDR is NOT sufficient to cover EU AI Act compliance. It is
integrated into the AI Act conformity pathway (Art. 43(3)) but does not replace the
additional AI Act-specific requirements: AI risk management (Art. 9), data governance
(Art. 10), AI technical documentation (Art. 11/Annex IV), automatic logging (Art. 12),
AI-specific transparency/instructions for use (Art. 13), human oversight design (Art. 14),
AI accuracy and cybersecurity assessment (Art. 15), AI Act EU Declaration of Conformity
(Art. 47), and EU AI database registration (Art. 49).

As the deployer, your hospital must: use the system per vendor instructions, assign
competent human oversight and train for automation bias, retain logs for at least 6
months, monitor operations and report serious incidents, conduct a GDPR DPIA, and
(if a public body) a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment.

Engage your vendor now to confirm their AI Act compliance roadmap, obtain contractual
commitments regarding their provider obligations, and begin building your internal
governance framework for AI oversight in your radiology department. The 2 August 2027
deadline allows time for preparation, but the governance infrastructure — training,
monitoring protocols, incident reporting pathways, and DPIA — should be in place at or
before initial deployment.
