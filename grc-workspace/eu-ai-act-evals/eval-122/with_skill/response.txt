EU AI ACT COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS: PREDICTIVE POLICING AI TOOL FOR NEIGHBOURHOOD CRIME RISK SCORING
================================================================================================

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The proposed deployment of an AI tool that scores neighbourhoods by crime risk using historical arrest data, social media monitoring, and demographic data triggers multiple provisions of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (the EU AI Act). The vendor's characterisation of the system as merely a "decision support tool" does not determine its legal classification — the Act classifies AI systems by their function, purpose, and the data they use, not by marketing labels. This system faces serious risk of being classified as a prohibited AI practice under Article 5(1)(d) and/or Article 5(1)(c), and at minimum constitutes a high-risk AI system under Annex III, Area 6 (Law Enforcement). Deployment may be unlawful from 2 February 2025 onward.

---

STEP 1: ROLE AND SCOPE IDENTIFICATION (Art. 3)

The municipality is the DEPLOYER (Art. 3(4)): a public authority using an AI system under its own authority for law enforcement and public safety purposes. The vendor is the PROVIDER (Art. 3(3)): the entity that developed the system and places it on the market.

Both parties bear distinct obligations. Critically, as a public authority deployer, the municipality bears specific obligations under Art. 26 and Art. 27, and cannot lawfully deploy the system if it falls within a prohibited category or if the provider has not completed registration in the EU AI database (Art. 60).

Member State of operation: An EU Member State municipality — the Act applies in full.

---

STEP 2: AI SYSTEM CLASSIFICATION (Art. 3(1))

The system clearly meets the Art. 3(1) definition of an AI system: it is a machine-based system that, for a given objective (crime risk scoring), infers from inputs (historical arrest data, social media data, demographic data) how to generate outputs (neighbourhood risk scores) that affect real environments (policing decisions and resource allocation). The "decision support" framing is legally irrelevant to this determination.

---

STEP 3: PROHIBITED PRACTICES SCREEN (Art. 5 — applies from 2 February 2025)

This is the most critical legal question. The system must be screened against all eight prohibited practices. Two categories are highly likely to apply; a third raises serious concerns.

3A. Art. 5(1)(d) — Predictive Criminal Risk Assessment [HIGH PROBABILITY OF PROHIBITION]

Article 5(1)(d) prohibits AI systems that assess the risk of natural persons committing criminal offenses based solely on profiling or on assessment of personality/character traits.

The described system scores neighbourhoods based on:
- Historical arrest data (a proxy for criminal conduct of past individuals in an area)
- Social media monitoring (behavioural and character-based signals)
- Demographic data (group-level attributes including protected characteristics)

Although the system scores neighbourhoods rather than named individuals, this does not automatically escape the prohibition. The Recitals and the Act's fundamental rights framing make clear that systems assessing crime risk through profiling — even at an area level — fall within this prohibition when they are used to direct law enforcement action toward individuals or groups. When police resources are directed to a neighbourhood because its residents have a high "crime risk score," the system functionally profiles those residents as criminal threats based on demographic and historical data rather than on objective, verifiable facts directly linked to their own criminal activity.

The single exception in Art. 5(1)(d) applies only to systems "supporting human assessment based on objective, verifiable facts directly linked to criminal activity." This exception is narrow. Historical arrest data is not equivalent to verified criminal conduct (arrests do not prove criminality); demographic data has no direct link to individual criminal activity; and social media monitoring produces inferred behavioural signals, not verified facts. The system does not qualify for this exception.

Conclusion: There is a strong legal argument that this system is prohibited under Art. 5(1)(d). Any deployment from 2 February 2025 risks enforcement action.

3B. Art. 5(1)(c) — Social Scoring [SIGNIFICANT RISK]

Article 5(1)(c) prohibits AI systems that evaluate or classify natural persons or groups based on social behaviour over a period of time or inferred personal characteristics, where this leads to:
- Detrimental or unfavourable treatment of persons or groups in unrelated social contexts; OR
- Treatment that is disproportionate or unjustified relative to the social context in which the behaviour occurred.

A neighbourhood crime risk score derived from demographic data and social media monitoring constitutes an evaluation of groups based on inferred personal characteristics and social behaviour. When those scores result in increased police presence, stop-and-search activity, or resource allocation affecting residents, this constitutes detrimental treatment of persons based on where they live and their demographic profile — not based on any personal action they have taken. This is precisely the discriminatory social scoring dynamic that Art. 5(1)(c) prohibits.

The use of demographic data as a scoring input is particularly problematic. Demographic data as a predictor of crime risk functions as a proxy for protected characteristics (race, ethnicity, national origin, socioeconomic status), leading to systematically disproportionate treatment of communities based on their composition rather than individual conduct.

Conclusion: Significant risk that the system also constitutes prohibited social scoring under Art. 5(1)(c).

3C. Art. 5(1)(g) — Biometric Categorisation for Sensitive Attributes [ASSESS FURTHER]

If the social media monitoring component involves facial recognition, image analysis, or biometric processing that infers race, political opinion, or other protected attributes to contribute to the score, Art. 5(1)(g) is additionally engaged. This depends on the specific technical implementation and should be assessed with the vendor.

3D. Summary of Prohibited Practices Screen

The system presents a high probability of falling under Art. 5(1)(d) (predictive criminal assessment) and significant risk under Art. 5(1)(c) (social scoring). If either prohibition applies, the system cannot be lawfully deployed in the EU regardless of the vendor's characterisation, regardless of any contractual terms, and regardless of technical safeguards. No workaround or "decision support" labelling remedies a prohibited practice.

---

STEP 4: RISK TIER DETERMINATION — ASSUMING ARGUENDO THE SYSTEM IS NOT PROHIBITED

If a court or supervisory authority were to determine the system does not meet the prohibition threshold (which would be a contested finding), it would unambiguously be classified as HIGH-RISK under multiple Annex III areas.

4A. Annex III, Area 6 — Law Enforcement (Art. 6(2))

The system falls within at least the following Area 6 sub-categories:

- Annex III, Area 6(d): AI assessing the risk of offending or reoffending (including recidivism assessment). A neighbourhood crime risk score that informs policing of an area and its residents directly assesses offending risk at the population level.

- Annex III, Area 6(e): AI profiling natural persons in the course of criminal detection, investigation, or prosecution. Directing police resources based on demographic and behavioural scoring of neighbourhood residents constitutes profiling in the law enforcement context.

- Annex III, Area 6(a): AI for individual risk assessment of becoming a victim of criminal offences (secondary consideration; the system could be framed this way to attempt to avoid other sub-categories, but this does not escape high-risk classification).

4B. Art. 6(3) — Profiling Override

Even if a vendor attempted to argue the system qualifies for non-high-risk exceptions (narrow procedural task, preparatory work, etc.), Art. 6(3) provides a critical override: any Annex III system that profiles natural persons is ALWAYS classified as high-risk, regardless of the exceptions. This system manifestly profiles groups of natural persons. The override applies.

4C. "Decision Support" Framing Does Not Reduce Risk Classification

The vendor's claim that this is merely a "decision support tool" has no legal weight under the Act. The Act classifies systems by their function and data processing, not by whether a human makes a final decision. High-risk AI systems are typically deployed in precisely this "decision support" capacity; the framework anticipates human-in-the-loop scenarios and still classifies them as high-risk. The Art. 6(3) profiling override further eliminates any pathway to a lower classification.

---

STEP 5: HIGH-RISK OBLIGATIONS (Arts. 8–17, 26) — IF NOT PROHIBITED

Assuming (for analysis purposes) the system were not prohibited, the full high-risk compliance regime would apply from 2 August 2026:

PROVIDER OBLIGATIONS:

Art. 9 — Risk Management System: The vendor must implement a continuous, lifecycle-spanning risk management system covering all five required steps. This must include specific analysis of risks to fundamental rights, including the risk of discriminatory outcomes from demographic data use. Given the law enforcement context and the population affected, the risk profile is extremely high.

Art. 10 — Data Governance: Historical arrest data raises severe data quality and representativeness concerns. Arrest data reflects historical policing patterns, which are known to be correlated with race and socioeconomic status rather than actual crime rates. Using this data to train a crime risk model embeds and amplifies existing biases. The vendor must demonstrate the data is relevant, sufficiently representative, and free from errors (Art. 10(2)). Use of demographic data and social media data for law enforcement AI training also engages special-category personal data processing restrictions under Art. 10(5) and GDPR Art. 9 — very strict conditions apply.

Art. 11 — Technical Documentation: Full Annex IV technical documentation must be prepared and maintained for 10 years, covering training methodologies, design choices, dataset descriptions, performance metrics, and human oversight measures.

Art. 12 — Automatic Logging: The system must generate automatic event logs during operation enabling reconstruction of how scores were generated and what inputs influenced outcomes.

Art. 13 — Transparency to Deployers: The vendor must provide the municipality with comprehensive instructions for use, including known limitations, performance disparities across demographic groups, circumstances that may lead to risks, and measures for human oversight.

Art. 14 — Human Oversight: The system must be designed to allow oversight persons to understand capabilities and limitations, recognise automation bias, correctly interpret outputs, and override or disregard outputs. This is critical in a law enforcement context where automation bias — over-reliance on AI crime scores — could cause serious fundamental rights violations (unlawful stops, discriminatory policing, wrongful arrests). The municipality must assign persons with the competence, authority, and resources to exercise meaningful oversight.

Art. 15 — Accuracy, Robustness, Cybersecurity: Performance must meet declared accuracy standards throughout the system's lifetime. The vendor must address robustness against feedback loops — a common failure mode in predictive policing where increased police presence in "high-risk" areas produces more arrests, which feed back into the training data to further elevate risk scores, creating a self-reinforcing bias spiral.

Art. 16 — Provider Obligations: The vendor must complete conformity assessment (self-assessment for Annex III Points 2–8), draw up an EU Declaration of Conformity, affix CE marking, and register the system in the EU AI database before placing it on the market or putting it into service.

Art. 17 — Quality Management System: The vendor must maintain a documented QMS covering all 13 required components.

DEPLOYER OBLIGATIONS (ART. 26):

The municipality, as deployer, must:
- Use the system strictly in accordance with the vendor's instructions for use (Art. 26(1))
- Assign competent persons with authority and resources for human oversight (Art. 26(2))
- Ensure input data is relevant and representative where it controls the inputs (Art. 26(3))
- Monitor operations continuously and notify the vendor and market surveillance authority if risks are identified (Art. 26(4))
- Immediately notify if a serious incident occurs (Art. 26(5))
- Retain automatically generated logs for at least 6 months (Art. 26(6))
- Register its use of the system in the EU AI database before deployment (Art. 26(8), Art. 60) — the municipality cannot deploy an unregistered high-risk system

---

STEP 6: FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS IMPACT ASSESSMENT (ART. 27)

As a public authority deploying a high-risk AI system in Annex III Area 6 (law enforcement), the municipality is required to conduct a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) before deployment (Art. 27).

The FRIA must address:
- The processes in which the system will be used and the time period and frequency of use
- The categories of persons likely to be affected (this system disproportionately affects residents of certain neighbourhoods, who are likely to be drawn from specific demographic, racial, or socioeconomic groups)
- Specific risks of harm to affected persons (stigmatisation of communities, discriminatory policing, chilling effects on social media expression, violation of presumption of innocence)
- Human oversight measures planned under Art. 14
- Measures to address fundamental rights risks

Where GDPR also requires a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) — which it will, given the processing of personal data including special-category data at scale for law enforcement purposes — the FRIA may be conducted alongside or integrated into the DPIA.

---

STEP 7: CONFORMITY ASSESSMENT (ART. 43)

For Annex III Points 2–8 systems (which includes law enforcement — Area 6), the conformity assessment path is self-assessment by the provider under Annex VI (internal control procedure). Third-party notified body assessment is not available for this category (unlike biometric systems under Area 1). However, for law enforcement contexts, the relevant market surveillance authority may act in a supervisory capacity.

The provider must complete this conformity assessment before placing the system on the market, draw up the EU Declaration of Conformity (Art. 47, retained for 10 years), affix CE marking (Art. 48), and register in the EU AI database (Art. 49).

---

STEP 8: PENALTIES (ART. 99)

Violations carry severe financial penalties:

- Prohibited AI practices (Art. 5 violations, e.g., if system is found prohibited under Art. 5(1)(d) or 5(1)(c)): Up to €35,000,000 or 7% of total worldwide annual turnover for the preceding year — whichever is higher.

- Provider or deployer violations of high-risk obligations (Arts. 8–17, 26): Up to €15,000,000 or 3% of total worldwide annual turnover.

- Providing incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information to authorities: Up to €7,500,000 or 1% of total worldwide annual turnover.

In addition to financial penalties, national authorities have powers under Art. 79 to order withdrawal of the system from service, suspension of use, and prohibition of deployment. Public reputational damage for a municipality deploying an unlawful surveillance AI tool is also a significant non-financial risk.

---

GDPR INTERSECTION

Independent of the EU AI Act, the use of historical arrest data, social media monitoring, and demographic data by a public authority for law enforcement-adjacent purposes engages:

- GDPR Art. 5 (data minimisation, purpose limitation, accuracy principles)
- GDPR Art. 9 (special-category data — demographic data used as race or ethnicity proxy)
- GDPR Art. 22 (rights related to automated decision-making and profiling)
- Law Enforcement Directive (LED, 2016/680) if the processing is by a competent authority for prevention, investigation, detection, or prosecution of criminal offences
- GDPR Art. 35 / LED Art. 27: mandatory DPIA before processing likely to result in high risk to individuals' rights and freedoms

The use of social media monitoring for law enforcement purposes in particular raises serious concerns under GDPR's purpose limitation principle (Art. 5(1)(b)) — social media users did not consent to their data being used to generate crime risk scores about their neighbourhoods.

RECOMMENDATIONS FOR THE MUNICIPALITY

1. DO NOT PROCEED with deployment until a full legal opinion on Art. 5(1)(d) and 5(1)(c) applicability has been obtained from qualified EU AI Act counsel.

2. Commission an independent legal and technical assessment of whether the system meets the Art. 5(1)(d) prohibition criteria before any procurement or pilot. This assessment should be documented to demonstrate due diligence.

3. Require the vendor to demonstrate: (a) completed conformity assessment under Art. 43; (b) EU Declaration of Conformity under Art. 47; (c) CE marking under Art. 48; (d) EU AI database registration under Art. 49; (e) full Annex IV technical documentation under Art. 11; (f) instructions for use under Art. 13.

4. If proceeding, conduct a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (Art. 27) and integrate it with a GDPR DPIA (GDPR Art. 35) before any deployment.

5. If proceeding, register the municipality's use in the EU AI database (Art. 60) before deployment.

6. Do not rely on the "decision support" framing as a legal shield. The Act classifies systems by function and the Art. 6(3) profiling override eliminates any pathway to lower risk classification for this system type.

7. Consider whether the policy objective (improved public safety) can be achieved through less invasive and legally compliant means. This is relevant both to proportionality analysis under the EU Charter and to demonstrating that the system meets the Art. 5(1)(d) exception (objective, verifiable facts directly linked to criminal activity) — which this system does not currently meet.

CONCLUSION

The predictive policing tool described is almost certainly either prohibited under Article 5(1)(d) (predictive criminal assessment) and/or Article 5(1)(c) (social scoring) of the EU AI Act, with those prohibitions applying from 2 February 2025. Even if not prohibited, it is unambiguously a high-risk AI system under Annex III Area 6, triggering the full conformity regime. The vendor's "decision support tool" characterisation has no legal effect on this classification. Proceeding without thorough legal analysis and the required compliance steps exposes both the municipality and the vendor to penalties of up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, regulatory enforcement orders, and significant reputational and legal liability under the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights and GDPR.
