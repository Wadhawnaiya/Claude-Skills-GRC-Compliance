EU AI ACT OBLIGATIONS FOR A GPAI MODEL PROVIDER
200B Parameter LLM, 15T Token Training, ~3×10²⁴ FLOPs, Open-Source Release
Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — Compliance Analysis

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

SECTION 1: ROLE AND SCOPE IDENTIFICATION (Art. 3)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Your organisation is a PROVIDER under Art. 3(3) of the EU AI Act — you are a
natural or legal person developing a GPAI model and placing it on the market
under your own name or trademark (even via an open-source repository). The
Act applies to you if any of the following are true:

  • You are established in the EU; OR
  • The model is placed on the EU market or put into service in the EU; OR
  • The outputs of the model are used in the EU.

Open-source release does not remove provider status. You remain the provider
who places the model on the market. Downstream users who fine-tune or deploy
the model may themselves become providers or deployers of derived AI systems,
but your obligations as the upstream GPAI model provider persist.

SECTION 2: GPAI MODEL CLASSIFICATION (Art. 3(63), Art. 51)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

2.1 Does your model qualify as a GPAI model under Art. 3(63)?

Yes — all four qualifying criteria are met:

  (i)  LARGE-SCALE TRAINING: 15 trillion tokens and ~3×10²⁴ FLOPs constitutes
       training with "large amounts of data using self-supervision at scale."

  (ii) SIGNIFICANT GENERALITY: A 200B parameter GPT-class LLM is designed to
       perform a wide range of distinct tasks — summarisation, translation,
       coding, reasoning, question-answering, content generation, etc.

  (iii) MULTIPLE PURPOSES: The model can serve multiple different purposes
       whether deployed directly or integrated into downstream AI systems.

  (iv) NOT IN PRE-RELEASE R&D: You intend to place it on the market via
       open-source release, so the pre-release exclusion does not apply.

Conclusion: Your model IS a GPAI model within the meaning of Art. 3(63). The
GPAI obligations framework (Arts. 51–55) governs your obligations, not the
general high-risk AI system framework (Arts. 8–17) — GPAI models are assessed
under Chapter V of the Act, not Chapter III.

2.2 Does your model have SYSTEMIC RISK under Art. 51?

CRITICAL FINDING — NO SYSTEMIC RISK (at this stage):

The Art. 51 automatic presumption of systemic risk is triggered when training
compute exceeds 10²⁵ FLOPs (100 septillion floating point operations).

Your model: ~3×10²⁴ FLOPs
Threshold:  10²⁵ FLOPs (i.e., 1×10²⁵ FLOPs)

Your compute is approximately one-third of the systemic risk threshold. You do
NOT automatically qualify as a systemic risk GPAI model under Art. 51(1).

HOWEVER — IMPORTANT CAVEATS:

  (a) COMMISSION DESIGNATION (Art. 51(2)): The European Commission retains
      the power to designate a model as having systemic risk based on criteria
      independent of the FLOPs threshold, including:
        • High-impact capabilities identified through model evaluation
        • Broad reach across the EU (e.g., a popular open-source model)
        • Potential negative effects on public health, safety, public security,
          or fundamental rights
        • Other criteria in Annex XIII (parameter count, user base size,
          domain specificity, multimodal capabilities)

      A 200B parameter model with broad open-source distribution could attract
      Commission attention under Annex XIII criteria, particularly if it
      achieves wide adoption or demonstrates high-impact capabilities on
      standard evaluations.

  (b) DYNAMIC THRESHOLD (Art. 51(3)): The Commission may amend the 10²⁵ FLOPs
      threshold via delegated acts. As compute efficiency improves and smaller
      models achieve greater capabilities, the threshold may be lowered,
      potentially capturing your model in future.

  (c) MONITORING OBLIGATION: Providers who "know or should reasonably know"
      their model is approaching the threshold should monitor total compute
      across all training runs. If you conduct additional pre-training,
      continual training, or other large-scale compute operations, the
      cumulative FLOPs may approach or exceed the threshold.

  (d) NOTIFICATION TRIGGER: If you cross the threshold, you must notify the
      AI Office within 2 weeks (Art. 51(4)).

CONCLUSION ON SYSTEMIC RISK: As currently described, you are a standard GPAI
model provider, NOT a systemic risk GPAI model provider. Your obligations are
governed exclusively by Art. 53 (universal GPAI obligations) and the
open-source exception therein, as detailed below.

SECTION 3: OPEN-SOURCE EXCEPTION — WHAT IT DOES (AND DOES NOT) COVER
(Art. 53(2))
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Art. 53(2) provides a significant but conditional exemption for open-source
GPAI model providers. Providers releasing GPAI models under open-source or
free licences are EXEMPT from the following Art. 53 obligations:

  EXEMPT:  Art. 53(1)(a) — Technical documentation (Annex XI content)
  EXEMPT:  Art. 53(1)(b) — Downstream provider information (Annex XII content)

However, ALL of the following obligations continue to apply even to open-source
GPAI providers:

  MANDATORY: Art. 53(1)(c) — Copyright compliance policy
  MANDATORY: Art. 53(1)(d) — Public training content summary

And critically:

  MANDATORY: If the model is later designated as having systemic risk (whether
  by crossing the FLOPs threshold or by Commission designation), the
  open-source exception ceases to apply and FULL Art. 53 + Art. 55 obligations
  are immediately triggered.

The practical effect: your open-source release strategy provides meaningful
regulatory relief today, but does not immunise you from future obligations if
your model's reach or capabilities lead to a systemic risk designation.

SECTION 4: YOUR MANDATORY OBLIGATIONS AS AN OPEN-SOURCE GPAI PROVIDER
(Art. 53(1)(c) and (d) — Applies from 2 August 2025)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

4.1 COPYRIGHT COMPLIANCE POLICY — Art. 53(1)(c)
    [Directive (EU) 2019/790 on Copyright in the Digital Single Market]

You must implement and maintain a policy to comply with EU copyright law,
specifically:

  REQUIREMENT: Establish mechanisms to respect text and data mining (TDM)
  opt-outs reserved by rights-holders under Art. 4(3) of Directive 2019/790.

Under Art. 4(3) of the DSM Copyright Directive, rights-holders may opt out of
permitting TDM of their works for purposes other than scientific research.
These opt-outs must be expressed in a machine-readable format (e.g., robots.txt
directives, HTTP headers, or metadata tags).

Practical implementation requirements:

  (a) DOCUMENTATION: Maintain records of your training data sources and
      demonstrate that rights-holder TDM opt-outs were honoured during training.

  (b) TECHNICAL MECHANISMS: Implement systems to detect and respect opt-out
      signals when crawling or acquiring training data. For an already-trained
      model, this means documenting the compliance steps taken during the
      training data acquisition phase.

  (c) PUBLIC DESCRIPTION: The policy must be publicly described — rights-holders
      and the public must be able to understand what mechanisms exist.

  (d) ONGOING APPLICABILITY: If you conduct further training runs or fine-tune
      the model on new data, the policy must govern those activities.

Note: Failure to respect TDM opt-outs could expose you to copyright
infringement liability under national law implementing Directive 2019/790,
independent of AI Act enforcement. The AI Act Art. 53(1)(c) obligation is the
regulatory compliance layer on top of substantive copyright law.

4.2 PUBLIC TRAINING CONTENT SUMMARY — Art. 53(1)(d)

You must draw up and make publicly available a sufficiently detailed summary
of the content used to train your GPAI model.

Format: Must follow a template published by the European Commission (template
required to be issued by the Commission under the Act; currently being
developed through the GPAI Code of Practice process).

Content requirements (based on the Act and draft Codes of Practice guidance):

  (a) CATEGORIES OF TRAINING DATA: Describe the types and sources of data
      used — web crawl data, books, code repositories, scientific papers,
      etc. — at a level of granularity that enables understanding without
      necessarily revealing proprietary details.

  (b) LANGUAGES: Languages represented in the training corpus.

  (c) DATA PROCESSING: Overview of filtering, deduplication, quality
      screening, and safety-related data curation steps applied.

  (d) EXCLUSIONS AND OPT-OUTS: Description of how TDM opt-outs and
      exclusion lists were applied.

  (e) SCALE: Approximate data volumes (you have publicly stated 15T tokens,
      which would satisfy this element).

  (f) TIME PERIOD: When data was collected and from what time periods.

Purpose of this obligation: Enable downstream AI system providers who
integrate your model, and the general public, to understand what the model
was trained on — relevant for bias assessment, copyright attribution, and
downstream compliance.

Publication: The summary must be publicly available, meaning accessible
without restriction (consistent with the open-source ethos of your release).
Publishing it alongside your model card or in your model repository would
satisfy the publication requirement.

SECTION 5: OBLIGATIONS THAT APPLY IF OPEN-SOURCE EXCEPTION IS LOST
(Art. 53(1)(a) and (b) — Would Apply if Systemic Risk Designated)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

While not currently mandatory given your open-source status and sub-threshold
compute, understanding these obligations is essential for risk planning.

5.1 TECHNICAL DOCUMENTATION — Art. 53(1)(a) [Annex XI Content]

If your open-source exception were lost, you would need to prepare and
maintain technical documentation covering (Annex XI):

  • General description: intended purpose, types of tasks performable,
    interaction modes (API, inference endpoint, local deployment)
  • Development process: data sources, data processing and filtering methods,
    training techniques (architecture, training objectives, RLHF or other
    alignment steps), compute infrastructure, and total compute used
  • Testing and evaluation: benchmarks used (e.g., MMLU, HumanEval, BIG-Bench),
    evaluation results, safety testing (red-teaming) methodology and outcomes
  • Known limitations: performance degradation conditions, hallucination rates,
    biases identified, out-of-distribution failure modes
  • Foreseeable risks: misuse scenarios, potential for harmful outputs
  • Responsible disclosure: contact information for reporting safety issues,
    vulnerability disclosure programme
  • Keep up-to-date and provide to AI Office and national authorities on request

5.2 DOWNSTREAM PROVIDER INFORMATION — Art. 53(1)(b) [Annex XII Content]

If required, you must document and make available to downstream AI system
providers who integrate your model:

  • Capabilities and limitations (enabling them to meet their own obligations)
  • Integration instructions and API/interface specifications
  • Security measures and recommendations
  • Results of evaluations conducted

Note: Even under the open-source exception, voluntarily providing model cards,
system cards, and integration documentation is strongly advisable — it protects
downstream developers and reduces the risk that your model is misused in ways
that could attract regulatory or reputational consequences.

SECTION 6: ADDITIONAL OBLIGATIONS IF SYSTEMIC RISK IS DESIGNATED
(Art. 55 — Would Apply Only if Systemic Risk Triggered)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

For completeness and contingency planning:

6.1 MODEL EVALUATION AND ADVERSARIAL TESTING (Art. 55(1)(a))
  • Conduct model evaluation according to standardised protocols and
    state-of-the-art tools
  • Conduct adversarial testing / red-teaming to identify systemic risks
  • May be conducted in cooperation with the AI Office

6.2 RISK ASSESSMENT AND MITIGATION (Art. 55(1)(b))
  • Assess and mitigate possible systemic risks from development, placement,
    and deployment, including effects on public health, safety, security,
    and fundamental rights at Union level

6.3 SERIOUS INCIDENT REPORTING (Art. 55(1)(c))
  • Track, document, and promptly report serious incidents and corrective
    actions to the AI Office and relevant national competent authorities
  • Report without undue delay upon becoming aware of an incident

6.4 CYBERSECURITY (Art. 55(1)(d))
  • Ensure adequate cybersecurity protection for the model, physical
    infrastructure, and supply chain
  • Includes protection against model weight theft, unauthorised access,
    and adversarial attacks on model integrity

SECTION 7: PROHIBITED PRACTICES SCREEN (Art. 5 — Applies from 2 Feb 2025)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

As a GPAI model provider releasing a general-purpose foundation model, the
Art. 5 prohibited practices apply to AI SYSTEMS, not to GPAI models in
isolation. However:

  • You must not design, train, or configure the model to perform prohibited
    practices (e.g., you must not intentionally embed capabilities for social
    scoring, mass biometric surveillance, or manipulation techniques).

  • Downstream deployers who use your model to build AI systems will bear
    primary Art. 5 liability for those systems. However, if you knowingly
    provide a model specifically designed to facilitate prohibited uses, you
    may face regulatory scrutiny and reputational consequences.

  • Your open-source release means you have limited control over downstream
    use — this makes your model documentation, usage policies, and Acceptable
    Use Policy (AUP) critically important as risk mitigation tools, even
    though they are not strictly mandated by the Act for a non-systemic-risk
    GPAI provider.

SPECIFIC PROHIBITED PRACTICES TO CONSIDER IN MODEL DESIGN:

  Art. 5(1)(a): Do not train or configure the model to use subliminal or
  manipulative techniques that distort behaviour causing significant harm.

  Art. 5(1)(c): Do not build social scoring functionality into the model.

  Art. 5(1)(e): Do not enable or encourage untargeted facial recognition
  database creation through model outputs.

  Art. 5(1)(f): Do not design the model specifically for workplace or
  educational emotion inference.

SECTION 8: HIGH-RISK AI SYSTEM FRAMEWORK — NOT DIRECTLY APPLICABLE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The high-risk obligations under Arts. 9–17 and 26 (risk management system,
data governance, technical documentation, record-keeping, transparency,
human oversight, accuracy/robustness, quality management system) apply to
HIGH-RISK AI SYSTEMS, not to GPAI models qua models.

Your obligations are:

  AS GPAI PROVIDER: Arts. 51–55 (Chapter V) — as analysed above.

  IF YOU ALSO DEPLOY the model in a high-risk use case yourself (e.g., you
  build and deploy a high-risk application on top of your own GPAI model):
  You would then ALSO bear provider obligations under Arts. 9–17 for that
  downstream high-risk AI system.

  FOR DOWNSTREAM PROVIDERS: Those who integrate your GPAI model into their
  own AI systems and deploy them in high-risk contexts will bear the Arts. 9–17
  obligations for their systems. Your Annex XII information (where required)
  is intended to facilitate their compliance.

SECTION 10: PENALTIES FOR NON-COMPLIANCE (Art. 99)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Violations of GPAI model obligations (Arts. 53–55) constitute violations of
provider/deployer obligations and are subject to:

  MAXIMUM FINE: €15,000,000 OR 3% of total worldwide annual turnover,
  whichever is higher (Art. 99(3)).

  For GPAI systemic risk violations specifically, the Commission itself may
  conduct investigations and impose fines directly (in addition to national
  competent authority enforcement).

  For SMEs and startups: the lower of the fixed amount or percentage applies
  (Art. 99(6)).

Enforcement of GPAI obligations rests primarily with the AI Office (Art. 64).

SECTION 11: COMPLIANCE PATHWAY AND CODES OF PRACTICE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The EU AI Act provides three compliance pathways for GPAI obligations:

  1. CODES OF PRACTICE (Primary pathway currently): Voluntary codes developed
     with AI Office involvement are the primary interim mechanism. The GPAI
     Code of Practice has been under development since late 2024 with the
     AI Office. Adherence to an approved Code creates a presumption of
     conformity. You should monitor and consider joining the Code of Practice
     process.

  2. HARMONISED STANDARDS: When published under a Commission mandate to
     European standards bodies (CEN/CENELEC), compliance with harmonised
     standards creates a presumption of conformity. Standards are still being
     developed.

  3. ALTERNATIVE ADEQUATE MEANS: Providers may demonstrate compliance through
     other methods acceptable to the Commission.

RECOMMENDATION: Engage with the GPAI Code of Practice process through the
AI Office, even as an open-source provider. The Code provides practical
guidance on implementing Arts. 53(1)(c) and (d), and participation
demonstrates good faith to regulators.

SECTION 12: GOVERNANCE CONTACTS AND OVERSIGHT BODIES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

AI OFFICE (Arts. 64–68): Primary regulator for GPAI models. Established
within the European Commission. Key contact point for:
  • Systemic risk notifications (Art. 51(4))
  • GPAI Code of Practice development
  • Serious incident reporting (Art. 55(1)(c)) if systemic risk applies
  • Model evaluation and compliance investigations
  • Access requests for model weights, documentation, training procedures

SCIENTIFIC PANEL (Arts. 68–70): Independent experts who may alert the AI
Office about high-impact capabilities that could trigger systemic risk
designation. May conduct capability assessments relevant to your model.

NATIONAL COMPETENT AUTHORITIES: Member State authorities retain market
surveillance powers and can request technical documentation. For an
open-source provider, the relevant national authority is typically in the
Member State where you are established.

SECTION 13: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (before or at release):

  1. TRAINING DATA AUDIT: Conduct a thorough review of your training data
     sources to identify any rights-holders who expressed TDM opt-outs under
     Art. 4(3) of Directive 2019/790. Document your review. This is the
     foundation of your Art. 53(1)(c) compliance.

  2. DRAFT COPYRIGHT COMPLIANCE POLICY: Publish a clear, publicly accessible
     policy describing how you identified and respected TDM opt-outs, what
     mechanisms exist for rights-holders to raise concerns, and your commitment
     to ongoing compliance.

  3. DRAFT TRAINING DATA SUMMARY: Prepare your public training content summary
     per Art. 53(1)(d). Publish it in your model repository and on your website.
     Even before the Commission template is published, a comprehensive model
     card addressing training data provenance is advisable.

  4. IMPLEMENT ACCEPTABLE USE POLICY (AUP): While not strictly required for a
     non-systemic-risk open-source GPAI provider, an AUP prohibiting use for
     Art. 5 prohibited practices is a critical risk mitigation measure and
     demonstrates responsible deployment.

  5. MONITOR COMPUTE ACCUMULATION: Track total training compute across all
     runs. Maintain an internal record. If future fine-tuning or additional
     pre-training runs approach the 10²⁵ FLOPs threshold, initiate notification
     procedures.

  6. ENGAGE WITH AI OFFICE: Consider proactive engagement with the AI Office
     and the GPAI Code of Practice process. Early engagement is viewed
     favourably and provides guidance on evolving expectations.

ONGOING:

  7. MONITOR COMMISSION DESIGNATION CRITERIA: Watch for any Commission
     guidance or decisions under Art. 51(2) and Annex XIII that could affect
     your model's designation status as it achieves wider adoption.

  8. SAFETY INCIDENT TRACKING: Even without a formal Art. 55 obligation,
     maintain internal records of known safety issues, misuse reports, and
     significant harms. This positions you for compliance if systemic risk
     obligations are later triggered.

  9. SUPPLY CHAIN TRANSPARENCY: Provide good-faith model cards, evaluation
     results, and safety documentation to support downstream providers meeting
     their own obligations — this is both best practice and reduces the risk
     that downstream misuse is attributed to inadequate upstream disclosure.
